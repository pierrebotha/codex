BETWEEN TWO WORLDS — OPTIMAL VALENCE CURVE
Full 24-Chapter Emotional Architecture

Authored in consultation with Between Two Worlds - Rewrite Blueprint.txt and Between Two Worlds - Canon Bible.txt.
This document is a structural design specification and deviation audit, not a prose draft.


================================================================================
I. GOVERNING PRINCIPLES
================================================================================

WHAT THIS DOCUMENT IS FOR
The valence curve is the emotional spine of the novel. It specifies the dominant
pressure level of each chapter — not its prose tone, not its subject matter, but
the amount of forward tension the reader should feel as they move through it. A
chapter scored at 4/10 is not a failure. It is structural breathing room. A
chapter at 9/10 is not automatically climactic. It is pressure-building. The
placement of each chapter on the curve, and its relationship to the chapters
immediately before and after it, is the architecture that prevents hedonic
adaptation and keeps the reader in a state of sustained incompletion.

THE VALENCE SCALE
  1-2    Repose or reflective low pressure. The world settles.
  3-4    Warm but unsettled. Forward curiosity, low threat.
  5-6    Active tension and forward pull. Something is at stake, but not yet in crisis.
  7-8    Strong danger, rupture, or moral pressure. The reader leans forward.
  9-10   Peak emotional or narrative crisis. Survival, rupture, catastrophic recognition.

CORE DESIGN RULES

Rule 1 — No sustained plateau.
Never allow more than two consecutive chapters in the same pressure band (within
1 point of each other). The third chapter must either rise or fall by at least 2
points. A plateau kills forward momentum more reliably than any single bad scene.

Rule 2 — Every high requires a corrective dip.
After any chapter at 8 or above, the next chapter must drop to 6 or below. This
is not a weakness in the structure. It is the reader's breath being given back
before the next compression. Without the dip, the reader goes numb.

Rule 3 — The curve is not a single arc.
It does not simply rise from Ch1 to the summit and descend. It oscillates within
an ascending envelope (Part I-III) and a descending envelope (Part IV), with two
distinct peaks — one physical, one relational — and a secondary low at the
opening of Part IV before the final ascent to Ch22.

Rule 4 — Warmth chapters carry undertow.
Any chapter in the 3-5 range must contain at least one element of structural
unease: class shame, threatened community, political shadow, or unsettled desire.
Pure warmth without undertow is not comfort for the reader. It is flatness.

Rule 5 — The symmetry principle.
Symmetry here does not mean a mirror image. It means that the emotional weight of
the novel's opening arc is architecturally balanced against the emotional weight
of its closing arc. The pivot is Chapter 13 (On the Mountain) — the midpoint of
the 24-chapter structure and the site of the first peak. Chapters 1-12 are the
ascent envelope; Chapters 14-24 are the descent-and-final-rise envelope. The
novel does not end where it began. It ends, not at collapse, but at costly motion.

Rule 6 — Ch22 is the summit, not the end.
The emotional maximum of the book (Ch22, The Last Argument, 10/10) is not the
final chapter. Two chapters follow it. This is the correct architecture. A novel
that ends on its maximum has nowhere to resolve. Chapters 23-24 are the moral
landing: grief, then chosen motion. They must read as active, not as aftermath.

HEDONIC ADAPTATION PREVENTION
The reader's emotional response to sustained high pressure flatlines within two
chapters if the intensity is not relieved. The curve has been designed so that
relief chapters (3-5 range) appear predictably after each escalation cluster,
and the source of tension shifts each time the pressure rebuilds: first social,
then cultural, then erotic, then bodily, then political, then epistolary, then
communal — so even when the pressure level is similar, the form of the threat
is new.

tension — it is the reader watching Ruth choose comfort over her own conscience
(the unread letter is the structural proof).

NOTE ON CONSECUTIVE 6s (Ch7-8):
This is the only permitted consecutive-same-level pair in the design. The
justification is that Ch7 and Ch8 cover different cultural worlds (Kaapse
domestic versus District Six public) and carry genuinely different registers
(intimate versus plural). The undertow must be kept distinct between them:
Ch7 ends on domestic precarity; Ch8 ends on the explicit political threat to
the district. If either chapter fails to establish that distinction in the
prose, one must be regraded.

PART III ARC SHAPE
8 → 8 → 9 → 8 → 6 → 7 → 8 → 9
The section opens high (Ch11), sustains and deepens (Ch12), spikes to first peak
(Ch13), stays high but shifts source (Ch14), releases deliberately (Ch15), rises
again through the uncanny (Ch16), returns to high through letters (Ch17), and
builds to near-peak at the close (Ch18).

The essential design feature is the Ch15 valley: score 6, after two 8s and a 9.
Without this valley, Part III is a wall of pressure that numbs rather than
compels. The valley is brief, purposeful, and immediately followed by upturn.

PART IV — THE LAST BREAK (Ch19-24)
Final arc goal: irreversible rupture, dread, catastrophe, grief, action.
This section opens with an event (not a mood), descends into the north before
ascending to the summit, then resolves through grief into motion. The architecture
is: spike (Ch19) → measured dread (Ch20) → cold grief (Ch21) → summit (Ch22)
→ tenderness (Ch23) → costly resolve (Ch24).

PART IV ARC SHAPE
9 → 7 → 8 → 10 → 7 → 8
The section opens near-peak, drops to dread, rises to cold grief, reaches the
true summit, drops into tenderness, then rises again to costly resolve. The
close at 8 (not 10, not 5) is the critical design decision: the novel should
end with Ruth in motion at elevated pressure, not with achieved rest.

KEY STRUCTURAL MOMENTS ON THE CURVE
- Ch4 (7):   Hinge chapter. First irreversible moral perception. Sarah.
- Ch6 (4):   The curve's lowest point. Warmth must carry undertow or it becomes
             a hole in the structure, not a breathing space.
- Ch13 (9):  First peak. Physical. The mountain and the body's survival.
- Ch15 (6):  The deliberate valley between Ch13 and the second escalation.
             Without it, Part III becomes a wall.

             since the first chapter.

SYMMETRY ANALYSIS
The novel achieves structural balance across its pivot at Ch13 through the
following mirrored elements:

  Opening movement (Ch1-5):
  Ascent from 6 to moral hinge at 7, then corrective release at 5.
  Ends on deliberate low.

  Closing movement (Ch19-24):
  Opens near-peak at 9 (the irreversible act has been committed), descends
  to dread at 7, rises through grief at 8, peaks at 10, then releases to 7
  before closing at 8.

  The symmetry is not numerical identity — it is architectural: both the opening
  and the closing arcs feature a corrective dip after the highest point in that
  section, followed by a renewed rise. The novel opens on a promise of motion
  (6) and closes on an act of motion (8). The moral weight between those two
  moments of motion is the novel's argument.

VII. DESIGN RATIONALE — KEY STRUCTURAL DECISIONS
================================================================================

WHY CH13 IS THE FIRST PEAK, NOT CH18 OR CH22
The mountain chapter (Ch13) carries the physical crisis — the body in actual
mortal danger. Placing this at the novel's midpoint (Ch13 of 24) has three
functions:
  1. It prevents the reader from arriving at the relational summit (Ch22) already
     exhausted from a sustained physical climax earlier on the same arc.
  2. It provides the rebirth motif that earns the second half of the novel: Ruth
     has survived physically. The question becomes whether she can survive morally.
  3. It creates the structural comparison that makes Ch22 inevitable: mountain
     survival is a single terrible afternoon. The Lena rupture has been twenty
     years in preparation. The reader should feel the difference in weight.

WHY CH6 IS THE CURVE'S LOWEST POINT
Ch6 sits at 4/10: the lowest scored chapter in the entire novel. This is not an
accident or a weakness. Placing the emotional floor at Ch6 (not Ch1 or Ch2) has
two functions:
  1. The reader has now moved far enough through the novel that warmth feels
     earned rather than premature. Two hard social chapters precede Ch6. The
     warmth is therefore credible.
  2. Immediately after the curve's floor, Part II opens its escalation sequence.
     The reader enters Ch7's cultural intoxication from a position of emotional
     openness, not of exhaustion. The contrast between Ch6's warmth and Ch7's
     vitality is one of the novel's structural pleasures — but only if Ch6 does
     not become so comfortable that the reader stops leaning forward.

WHY CH24 CLOSES AT 8 AND NOT AT A LOWER SCORE
A novel of this kind — in which the protagonist has suffered, broken free, and
chosen a difficult autonomous direction — must not end on peace. Peace would
be a lie about what this kind of life costs. But it must also not end on despair
(which would make the preceding 23 chapters pointlessly accumulative). Closing
at 8 — Ruth in motion, at elevated cost, without a destination fully visible —
is the only honest ending: not triumphant, not defeated, but alive and still
under the pressure of a chosen life.

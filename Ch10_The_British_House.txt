# Chapter 10: The British House

The invitation had been on the desk for five days before Ruth allowed herself to read it as something that required an answer.

The notebook was beside it, closed. She had not opened it since Friday.

It sat beside the Greek grammar and the scholarship folder, a cream envelope, the handwriting firm and forward-slanting, the kind formed by years of girls' school penmanship instruction. She had opened it on the Tuesday and read it once and set it down again without committing to anything. She had read it again on Wednesday evening after tutorial, when the lamp was on and the room was quiet and the mountain was pressing close against the window glass. The card inside was thick and slightly scented, nothing she could name precisely, something floral and dry, and the words on it ran in a clean, authoritative hand: Mrs Audrey Lawrence and Colonel Thomas Lawrence request the pleasure of Miss MacLeod's company at Bellevue, Oranjezicht, Saturday 12th March, seven o'clock. At the bottom, in a slightly different ink: Your father's cousin sends her warmest regards. We have heard wonderful things from William. We hope you will come.

Now it was Saturday and the light outside was already failing towards five.

Ruth stood before the spotted mirror on the wardrobe door and adjusted her collar for what she estimated was the ninth or tenth time. The glass was old, its silvering failing at the edges so that the outermost inch of her reflection dissolved into the cream-painted wall behind her. She looked at herself in the centre of what remained. The dress was the better of the two she owned, dark wool, cut square at the shoulder, worn to a slight shine along the left hip where she had been pressing the scholarship folder against her body for months. She had pressed the dress the previous night with a damp cloth and the residence's communal iron. The seam along the right sleeve still held a ghost of a pucker she had not managed to smooth out, and she had looked at it for a long time before deciding it would only be visible in certain light.

She fastened the top button. She unfastened it again.

The room's winter cold was in the fabric, in the floor, in the stone sill of the window where the condensation had sat all morning. It was in her, in the particular set of her shoulders, in the way she kept glancing at the card on the desk and then looking deliberately away from it. She had known this posture since the convent. It was the posture of a girl standing outside a room in which assessment was already in progress.

She picked up Judith's silk square from the desk. She had been carrying it since the previous Friday, folded once in her coat pocket, retrieved each morning with the intention of returning it, each evening replaced in the pocket untouched. She pressed it between her fingers now. It was pale green and silk-smooth, carrying the last of the Cohen apartment: beeswax, lavender, and the particular warm air of a house where arguments happened over good wine and no one was required to perform composure. She knotted it at her throat. It pulled the dress together and gave the collar something to settle against.

She looked at her hands. The nails were short and clean. The skin below the second knuckle of the right index finger still held a dark half-moon of library ink that three days of the residence's harsh yellow soap had not fully lifted. Her hands were the hands she had always had. The nails of a girl who worked. The ink of a girl who read. She looked at them for a moment and put them at her sides.

Through the window the mountain was already losing its definition, the upper rock face fading from the mottled grey-brown of daylight into the undifferentiated dark of evening. The south-easter was making its rounds, a long unvarying note in the eaves, the pines beyond the playing fields moving in slow collective agreement. The city below the slopes was turning its lights on one by one, small, warm assertions against the large cold of the night.

The scholarship folder lay on the desk. She did not open it.

She put on her coat and went to the door.

The corridor outside Room 412 was quiet. Saturday had emptied the residence in stages: the socials first, trailing cedar and laughter by five o'clock, gone by half past with their weekend ease; then the day-students; then the ordinary working evenings of the scholarship girls behind their doors. Behind one door, a typewriter clicking with steady concentration. Behind another, a radio barely audible, a voice reading something in Afrikaans, the words too muffled to distinguish. Ruth walked the length of the corridor to the high window at the far end and looked out at the ocean.

The Atlantic at this hour was a flat hammered grey, already losing its light from the west, the surface smooth in the lull between the day-wind and the night-wind. She stood for a moment with her forehead not quite touching the glass, the cold of it radiating into the few inches of air between them. Then she went down the stairs.

She had telephoned the taxi firm that afternoon from the residence payphone, listening to the Bakelite click of the dial and then the distant ring of a line reaching across the city. The man who answered quoted her a price for Oranjezicht without asking how she proposed to pay it. She had said yes and given him the address and then stood in the phone box for a moment after the receiver was down, listening to the street.

She waited at the kerb in the cold. The pines along the residence path moved above her with a dry, rhythmic sound, needle-heavy and carried on the evening wind. Somewhere down the hill, a tram was working its way up from Observatory, its overhead lines giving their high harmonic against the dark. She stood with her coat buttoned and her small clutch bag held in both hands and watched the road.

The taxi appeared at the bottom of the hill, one headlamp working, the engine a low complaint that grew closer and then cut as the driver pulled to the kerb. A black car with a dented near-side fender. The driver was a middle-aged man in a flat cap, a cigarette burning in the corner of his mouth, who did not offer to take her bag. He put the car in second before she had the door fully closed.

'Oranjezicht,' Ruth said. 'The Lawrence house. Bellevue, upper Hof Street.'

He nodded without looking at her and pulled out.

The car moved through streets that changed register with each block. Below the university's stone walls and pines, Observatory arrived: terraced houses pressed close, iron balconies with washing lines running between them, the week's sheets still out in the night air, two boys kicking a tin can along the pavement without looking up as the taxi passed. The houses grew wider apart as the road climbed. The trees changed from the straggling municipal planes of the lower slopes to the planted species of the upper suburbs, oaks and a copper beech heavy and dark behind one wall, and the dense, dark laurels that the English families had brought from home a century before and that had grown, in the Cape climate, to a size that nothing in England ever grew to.

Through the vibrating glass Ruth watched the lights of the lower city fall away on her left. The docks first, the dark shapes of the freight vessels under the arc lights with the small, moving figures of the longshoremen against the white. Then the terraced streets of the District, the iron balconies and the lamp-glow in individual windows and the dense concentration of lives arranged to use every square foot of available space. Then the flat rooftops of the lower suburbs giving way, the geometry loosening. The Atlantic was briefly visible between two buildings, wide and very dark, no light on it, only the idea of open water, and then the buildings closed again and they were on the upper roads and the city was below them.

The houses up here set back behind walls. The gardens Ruth glimpsed above the wall tops were green and attended, the hedges cut recently, the pale ends of the trimmed branches showing clean in the lamplight. The laurels along the street verge were enormous, glossy and utterly still, as if the wind above them moved by arrangement and the laurels had declined to participate. A dog barked behind one of the walls and fell silent immediately. The street smelled of wet stone and pine resin and, below that, very faintly, wood smoke from a fireplace somewhere in the houses above.

Ruth held her small bag in her lap. The card was inside it. She did not open the bag.

The taxi stopped before a pair of gates painted a deep, formal black. They were tall and slightly ornate at the top, the iron worked into acanthus leaves with a gilded finial on each gate, the gold remaining in patches where the Cape weather had not yet stripped it. The gates stood open. A gravel drive curved away through the lower garden and rose towards the house at the far end, visible through the dark: double-storey, Victorian in construction, with deep verandas on both levels and the sitting-room lights burning warm behind glass. Two other cars were parked along the verge near the gate posts.

The driver put on the handbrake.

Ruth counted coins and handed them across. His hand was large and calloused, the knuckles pale in the car's interior light. He closed his fist around the fare without looking at it and without looking at her. The car reversed, turned, and descended the slope between the laurels, its tail-lights two small red points in the dark of the street until the laurels closed and they were gone.

She was alone at the gates. The open ironwork stood on either side of her, present and formal, the hinges massive and oiled and held wide. The house at the top of the drive was lit and the gramophone inside it was audible from the kerb, thin and domestic, reduced to a thread of orchestral music by distance and cold air. The mountain was directly above the house, invisible in the dark, but the wind that came down off the rock face was cold and mineral and carried the smell of the stone.

She adjusted the silk square at her throat and walked up the drive.

The garden on either side was old and heavy, the roses cut back for winter into a collection of careful, architectural stumps, a fig tree in the lower corner trained against the wall the way the convent trees had been trained, its branches spread flat and tied, domesticated out of any shape that resembled its wild self. Her boots made a carrying sound on the raked gravel with each step. The veranda steps were flagstone, worn smooth in the centre. Red geraniums in pots on either side of the front door, still holding their colour under the lamp. The door was painted dark green, its brass fittings polished to a deep reflective shine, slightly distorting her shape as she came up the last of the steps.

Before she could reach for the bell, the door opened.

The woman who opened it was Cape Coloured, somewhere in her fifties, wearing a grey cotton dress under a soft white apron with no starch in it, the fabric loose and long-washed. Her hair was pulled back under a white cloth. She had the calm, settled manner of a person who had been answering this door for years and had long since stopped treating it as remarkable.

'Miss MacLeod?' she said.

'Yes.' Ruth was briefly surprised and then was not.

'Come in. Mrs Lawrence is in the drawing room.' A pause, quiet and deliberate. 'May I take your coat?'

Ruth gave her the coat. The woman folded it once over her arm with careful economy, the movement exact, the effort precisely calculated for the task. Ruth watched her do it and thought, without meaning to think it, of Sarah's hands closing around the wax-paper parcel of brisket in the Cohens' kitchen four weeks ago, the same quality of practised weight in the wrists. The thought arrived without announcement and left the same way. She followed the woman into the hall.

The hall was warm and still in a way the outside was not, smelling of cut flowers and beeswax, the coal fire drawing steadily in the adjacent room. Dark oak boards under an oriental carpet, deep red and blue, worn pale in a track down its centre where the generations had walked. A barometer in a mahogany case on the wall above the coat stand. A framed survey map of the Cape Province, hand-coloured, with the old Dutch names for the mountain ranges legible in small brown script. Beside the drawing-room door, a brass umbrella stand with two walking sticks and a furled umbrella.

The drawing-room doors were open. Ruth heard the voices before she entered: three conversations running, the gramophone playing Brahms, the fire's crack, a woman's laughter cut short. She stood for a moment in the doorway.

The room was large and furnished with things that had been very good when new and had held their character through decades of use. Two sofas and a scatter of chairs arranged around the fireplace. A writing table by the far window. A drinks cabinet in the corner with the decanters set out and already in use. The bookshelves ran floor to ceiling on the wall beside the door, their contents a mixture of proper books, a complete set of something in burgundy bindings, some volumes shelved horizontal for want of attention, and on the top shelf a series of objects: a naval sextant in its wooden case, a framed photograph of a ship at anchor in pre-war harbour light, a small bronze horse with one foreleg raised.

Nine people. Ruth counted without appearing to count.

Mrs Lawrence was on the nearest sofa with her back to the door, a narrow woman in dark blue, grey hair set and lacquered. She was talking to a man who stood with his drink held at waist height and his tie at the second button, laughing at something she had said. Two younger men by the drinks cabinet, both with the short-back-and-sides of men who had done service recently enough to still be performing it, one in a naval uniform. A couple by the fire, both fiftyish, the woman turning a book spine in her hands. A young woman alone near the windows, whose particular thinness and stillness set her apart from the room's general movement.

And in the corner chair, separated from the others by a clear two feet of unoccupied carpet, a man Ruth identified as Thomas Lawrence.

He was not old. Forty-something, thin in a way the body did not choose so much as arrive at, his hands wrapped around a glass of water rather than the whisky the others were drinking. His face had the quality of a face that is keeping itself very still. The jaw set, the gaze fixed on a particular point in the fire above the grate, the stillness slightly off in the way that a painting of a person is slightly off from a person: too controlled to be natural, too sustained to suggest comfort. His uniform was an old one, cleaned and pressed, as if he had put it on and then not changed again. He did not look up when Ruth came in.

Mrs Lawrence rose with a quick, efficient movement. Her handshake was firm and dry. Once, released.

'Miss MacLeod. I am so pleased you came.' The conviction behind it was practised but genuine, the two things not in contradiction. She looked at Ruth with the same accuracy that the letter had promised. Her gaze went briefly to the silk square and back. 'William wrote about you, you know. He is not a natural correspondent. He writes rarely and says even less in what he writes. But he wrote about you.' She tilted her head in the direction of the drinks cabinet. 'What will you drink? The sherry is a Manzanilla and quite decent, if sherry agrees with you. The man in the naval uniform is Pearce and he is better company than he appears.'

The man in question turned at the sound of his name and said that he had heard much worse, and poured the sherry without being asked and handed it to Ruth with the direct, easy manner of a man accustomed to rooms like this one and not unduly troubled by them. He had been at Tobruk and was now seconded to Simon's Town, he told her, and was extraordinarily dull, which she should know from the start, and she laughed before she could arrange her face against it.

The couple at the fire were the Rawlings, he a King's Counsel at the Cape Bar and she the daughter of a Port Elizabeth clergyman who had read Classics at Stellenbosch. She observed Ruth with a precise, contained interest when the scholarship was mentioned.

'Latin and Greek,' Mrs Rawlings said. 'You'll be in Arbuthnot's lectures.'

'When he's not reading us Agamemnon.'

'He is always reading someone Agamemnon,' Mrs Rawlings said. She held her drink and looked at the fire for a moment. 'The question he never asks is what Clytemnestra was doing for the ten years her husband was at Troy. The women in those plays have always done their suffering offstage. Arbuthnot calls that a structural feature.'

She said this last thing into the fire rather than to Ruth directly, but Ruth heard it. She filed it carefully in the place where she kept things she had not yet understood.

The man in the grey suit was Morrison, in land surveying. He had a voice that filled space without requiring attention, the satisfied, steady sound of a man for whom work was the primary moral category. The suit itself was better than the man, which she noted and then did not know what to do with. He talked about permit complications arising from the proposed relocations in the District and the lower suburbs, the logistical burden on his firm, and he used the word unfortunate three times in six sentences and appeared not to notice.

A young man in a white jacket moved through the room with the dishes. Ruth looked at him more carefully after the first glance and revised her initial impression: he was not young, forty at least, the whiteness of the jacket making the age harder to read. He moved with a practised absence of presence, offering the smoked salmon on its silver tray and the small pastry rectangles on their cloth, and the room absorbed his movement without acknowledging it. The conversations continued at their same volumes. The ring of glasses against each other was unchanged as he passed. He reached Mrs Rawlings. She took a pastry without looking at his hand and continued speaking. He reached Morrison. He reached Pearce.

He reached Ruth.

She looked directly at his face. He met her eyes for a fraction of a second, the expression nothing that she could name: not resentment, not subservience, not the bright professional warmth of someone who needs the gratuity. Simply the face of a person doing a task, making an assessment, reserving everything else. She took a pastry. He moved on. She watched him cross to the drinks cabinet, set the tray down, and pick up the second tray with the same measured rotation of purpose, his back to the room.

Across the room, in his corner chair, Thomas Lawrence was watching her.

She did not look away immediately. He did not look away either. He had the gaze of a man who had stopped caring about the social cost of looking directly at another person. Not rudeness. Something that had come after rudeness was no longer a consideration. After a moment the door from the dining room opened and something in the room shifted and Thomas Lawrence returned to his fire.

Dinner was laid in the adjacent room, set for ten and smelling of roast lamb and the beeswax-and-polish atmosphere of a large room kept carefully. Ruth was seated between Pearce and the young woman from the windows, who turned out to be Gillian, Thomas Lawrence's daughter from his first marriage, twenty-two years old, reading law.

'My father asked me to say he was sorry he hadn't spoken to you earlier,' Gillian said, quietly, serving herself potatoes without looking at Ruth.

'I didn't mind,' Ruth said.

Gillian set the dish down. A small, deliberate thing moved across her face. 'Most people want an explanation. He doesn't usually mention it.'

The conversation at the table ran into politics the way conversation in the city always ran into politics that winter, the same current beneath every other subject. Morrison spoke about the Group Areas planning as a logistical matter, the complications arising for his firm from the proposed removals, the resource allocation, the permit backlog. He spoke of it as a problem of scheduling, and the word unfortunate did its work again.

Rawlings said, after some time, that the government was proving very good at systems. He said 'the current government' in the tone one uses for weather: observational, at a remove, neither endorsement nor opposition and not quite neutral either. 'Efficient and cruel,' he added. 'Those two things are not in tension for them. They have made them the same thing.'

Mrs Lawrence looked down the table. 'Thomas was with men who had that view of organisation,' she said. 'Efficient and rational. They were building supply roads in the desert that lasted ten years.' She picked up her wine glass. 'The roads are still there.'

Nobody said anything for a moment. The young man in the white jacket moved around the table with the wine. He reached Morrison and refilled the glass and Morrison did not look up. The sentence continued uninterrupted. The young man moved behind Rawlings, behind the woman in the green dress, behind Mrs Lawrence. The table's conversations ran on at their same consistent level, the movement of the wine around the table as absorbed and unremarked as the ticking of the clock in the hall.

Ruth looked at her lamb. She thought of Sarah's hands on the wire brush at the Cohens' kitchen sink, the grey fat flying against the white tiles, the rhythm of the scrubbing. She thought of what Sarah had said about Hanover Street. She took a mouthful of the lamb and tasted the rosemary and the fat and the attention that had gone into the cooking of it and thought of who had cooked it and where.

Mrs Lawrence caught her looking at the fish kettle on the sideboard. 'You're from William's people,' she said. 'The north country. The mist-belt. I knew that landscape during the war, slightly. The Letaba valley. The scale of it changes you. People in Cape Town think the mountain is the definitive experience of scale. But they would think differently if they had stood in the north at flood.'

'My mother's kitchen floor was lye,' Ruth said. She had not intended to offer it. It came from somewhere below the management she was performing on herself, surfacing before she had decided to let it. 'She scrubbed it every Tuesday. The wood went white.'

Mrs Lawrence looked at her directly. 'That kind of country teaches a different arithmetic,' she said. She did not explain what she meant. She did not ask Ruth to.

After dinner the group moved back to the drawing room. Ruth drifted, carried by the room's current, towards the french doors that opened onto the lower veranda. They were slightly open, the cold coming through the gap in a purposeful stream, and she stood near it without choosing to, drawn by the cold in the same way she was always drawn by exits.

Thomas Lawrence was on the veranda.

She could see him through the glass of the open door, standing at the railing with his back to the room, the mountain above him entirely absent in the dark, only its pressure felt in the quality of the down-wind, the specific cold of stone at altitude coming off it. His hands were on the railing on either side of the water glass. The posture was not relaxed. It was the posture of someone gripping something to stay still.

She had been watching him for perhaps thirty seconds when he turned. She had not moved away in time. He had seen her.

After a moment he said, 'Are you fond of the dark?'

Ruth pushed the door open further and stood in its frame. The cold was immediate on her face. 'I find it quieter,' she said.

He absorbed this. 'Come out, then. If you don't mind the cold.'

She did not mind the cold. The veranda was wide, the flagstone damp and slightly slippery with it, and the mountain pressed close against the sky above the house, not visible but wholly present, the way a room is present even in the dark. The wind was direct here, not the building-deflected version that reached the drawing room, but the whole Cape Doctor coming down off the rock face without interruption. It found her collar immediately and the gap between her coat and her neck.

'They've had me going to a man in Wynberg,' Thomas said, his hands still on the railing, not looking at her. He spoke in the flat, reading tone of a person describing something that concerned someone else. 'Three months. It is, apparently, proceeding well.' He lifted the water glass and set it down. 'I behaved oddly at dinner. I don't like people to worry without information.'

'You ate your dinner and didn't talk much,' Ruth said. 'In my experience, that's not always odd.'

He made a sound through his nose that might have been a laugh. He looked at the dark of the garden. 'MacLeod,' he said. 'North country.'

'The lowveld. Tzaneen district. The Letaba valley.'

'I don't know the north. I've spent my life south of the Hex River. The Karoo occasionally. The Boland.' He turned the water glass on the railing. Its base made a quiet grinding sound on the iron. 'The men I served with came from everywhere. Paarl. Aberdeen, Scotland. One man from the Caprivi Strip. We shared a tent for four months. He could name every tree on the strip and I couldn't name one.' He was quiet for a moment. The wind moved through the laurels in the lower garden, a slow, collective giving. 'El Alamein,' he said. He said it simply and exactly, as if it were a place you could point to on a map, and that was answer enough. He did not say it again.

Ruth stood beside him at the railing with her hands on the cold iron. She had been in the proximity of this before. Not the same experience: she did not know what he was carrying. But she knew the unexploded quality of a person who had been somewhere and come back holding something the visible world had no container for. The convent had carried it in the older sisters, in some of the girls, in the way certain silences were a different substance to other silences, denser, more specifically shaped, requiring a particular quality of stillness from the people near them. She had learned, in the convent, to be still in that particular way. Not empty. Present, and without demand.

She did not say anything. She looked at the dark of the garden and the darker mass of the mountain above the house and the small specific lights of the lower city far below, each one burning in a window where someone was still awake, and she waited.

'You know the high country at night,' Thomas said.

'I know the escarpment,' she said. 'From the farm in the dark you could hear things that had no names. Not animals. The rock. The cooling. Something in the geology.'

He looked at her. 'The Triassic,' he said, with quick, alert interest, the tone of a man for whom knowledge of this kind was a relief, an alternative register to the other knowledge he was managing. 'The escarpment is Triassic sandstone with a Pre-Cambrian basement beneath it. Very old. When you hear the dark there you're hearing something four hundred million years deep.' He paused. 'I read geology sometimes. It helps with the scale of ordinary things.'

'Yes,' Ruth said.

The drawing-room door opened behind them. Gillian looked out.

'Father. Mrs Rawlings is asking.'

'I know,' Thomas said. He looked at Ruth once, briefly, with the same direct and unperforming gaze she had first caught across the drawing room. Then he went in.

Ruth followed him after a moment, the cold still on her face. The drawing room's warmth was immediate and complete. She stood with the last of her sherry, which had gone warm, and looked at the fire. Across the room, the young man in the white jacket was collecting glasses from the mantelpiece with the same measured purpose with which he had moved through the entire evening, his back to the conversations, his purpose unaltered, the room's noise running around him without meeting him.

By ten o'clock the gathering had thinned. Morrison went first, his departure registering as a specific easing of something in Ruth's chest that she did not attempt to account for. The Rawlings twenty minutes later, Mrs Rawlings pressing a card into Ruth's hand for a Schumann series at the School of Music before Ruth had agreed to attend it. Pearce remained, turned towards Gillian with a focussed, considerate attention that Gillian appeared to be calmly assessing.

Mrs Lawrence found Ruth by the bookshelf.

'You must come again,' she said. The conviction in it was the same as at the beginning of the evening. 'William will be glad to know you came. He worries very quietly, which is the kind that costs the most.' She looked at Ruth in the measured way she had looked at her all evening, the gaze of a woman who reads rooms accurately. '"The kind of girl who reads everything in the room," he said. I thought that was a careful description from a man who is not given to them.'

Ruth said goodnight and found her coat on the stand. The woman in the soft apron was not there, only the stand and the hall's warmth and the sound of washing-up from beyond the kitchen door at the corridor's end, a rhythmic, muffled sound: water and crockery, the ring of a pan set on the drying rack, steady and unhurried.

She stood in the hall with her coat over her arm for a moment.

Gillian telephoned for a taxi from the hall telephone and stood waiting with Ruth in the hallway's warmth, neither of them talking much, the gramophone still audible through the closed drawing-room door.

'You were good with him,' Gillian said.

Ruth looked at her.

'On the veranda.' Gillian turned the telephone's cord between two fingers once and released it. 'Most people try to talk him back into the room. You didn't.'

The taxi sounded its horn at the bottom of the drive. Ruth said goodnight and went down the veranda steps.

The drive was fully dark now, the lamplight from the gate posts reaching only partway up the gravel. Her boots made their sound on the pale stones. She was perhaps halfway down when she stopped.

The upper storey of the house was lit in one window. The room above the dining room. A figure stood at the glass, not quite resolved, the interior light making the glass partially reflective, showing her the top of the dark garden and the mountain's mass above the roofline. The figure was still. It might have been Thomas. The light was behind it and the night was in front of it and she could not be certain of what she was seeing, only that someone was standing in a lit room on the upper floor while everyone else was still below.

She stood on the gravel in the cold with the wind coming down off the mountain. The shape in the window did not move.

She went through the gates.

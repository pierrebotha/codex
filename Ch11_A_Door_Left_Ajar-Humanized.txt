# Chapter 11: A Door Left Ajar

It was Thursday, and the notebook on the desk had been closed since the Friday before.

Ruth had been working at the same eight lines of Tibullus for an hour. The lines were not difficult. She had finished them three times and crossed the result through three times, not because the translation was wrong but because the rhythm of it kept failing to match the rhythm of her mind, which was not on the desk.

The arrangement had been made by Judith on Tuesday, in the corridor outside Latin. There is a gathering on Thursday. Brownlow Road. A friend of mine. You should come. Said without elaboration, without the slight social inflection Judith used for an event that required preparation. When Ruth had asked what kind of gathering, Judith had looked at her for a fraction of a second and answered, A small one. Women I know. You would like them. Then moved off down the corridor before Ruth could ask anything else. Ruth had filed the fraction of a second in the place where she filed things she had not yet understood.

The light at her window was the early-autumn light of the Cape, thin gold, late, the mountain already losing its definition though it was barely past five. The south-easter was not blowing tonight. The residence was full of the audible movements of a building of women preparing for something or recovering from it. Behind one wall a violin scale, paused and resumed. Behind another, a typewriter ticking with the steady concentration of a girl writing a letter she did not want to put down.

She set the pen down. She closed the Tibullus. She left the notebook closed.

In the spotted wardrobe glass she wore the same dark dress she had worn to the Lawrences', because she owned only two and the other was at the laundry. She did not press it. She adjusted the collar once and let it stand. She did not put on Judith's silk square. She left it folded in the coat pocket where it had been sitting since the previous Friday.

She walked the length of the corridor to the high window at the far end and looked out at the ocean. The Atlantic at this hour was a flat grey, lighter than the sky, the surface entirely smooth. She stood for a moment with her forehead not quite touching the glass and then went down the stairs.

Judith was at the bottom of the residence drive, reading something in the failing light. A novel, French, the cover a soft ochre, unfamiliar to Ruth. Judith looked up.

'You came,' she said.

'You said I should.'

'I said I thought you would like them. Different thing.' She closed the book and tucked it into the side pocket of her coat. 'I am pleased.'

They walked down towards Burg Road. The pines along the path moved in the slow, dry agreement of evening. The city below was beginning to set its lights, one street at a time, the orange working its way up the lower slopes.

'Tell me who they are,' Ruth said.

'You will meet them.'

'Tell me anything.'

Judith looked at her sideways. 'Clara is the friend. The flat is hers. She is two years out of UCT, classics, the same as you. She is on the leader desk at the Cape Times and writes a column under another name. She reads more than anyone I know.'

'And the others?'

'A doctor at Groote Schuur. A photographer. A woman who teaches Italian at the university extension school in town. One or two others, perhaps. It changes.' Judith adjusted her scarf, the green one, against the cool of the descending air. 'It is not a structured evening. It is not literary in the way Mrs Rawlings means literary. It is a kitchen with a lot of women in it. Clara likes new people occasionally. I told her about you.'

'What did you tell her?'

Judith smiled slightly without looking at her. 'That you read everything in the room before sitting down. The same thing my mother said.'

The tram came up Main Road. They took it as far as the lower city and then changed for the slow climb of the Kloof Street tram into the streets that lay above the city centre and below the rock face. The light went out of the air entirely on the climb. The tram filled with the smell of damp wool and the low metallic warmth of the brake mechanism. Ruth stood at the rail. Judith sat with the French novel closed on her lap and her hands folded over it.

The tram set them down at the corner of Kloof Street and a side road that climbed steeply against the slope of the mountain. They walked up. Brownlow Road was narrow and old, tilted hard against the slope, the houses pressed close together on the lower side and stepped up the upper side in stages, each one a little higher than its neighbour. The houses were Edwardian, narrow-fronted, painted in fading creams and blues, their stoeps stepping straight onto the narrow pavement. Geraniums in pots. A cat on a sill, watching the street and not them.

Number twenty-seven was a double-storey terraced house, its front garden no more than a strip of crushed gravel and a fig tree against the side wall. The downstairs windows were lit and curtained. Judith led her past them to a side passage between number twenty-seven and the house next door, where a flight of iron steps climbed up the outside of the building to a separate door at the upper floor.

The door at the top was painted a deep, settled green. It was slightly ajar. A line of warm light fell out of the gap onto the iron of the landing and spilled onto the worn boards of the top step.

Judith did not knock. She pushed the door open with the side of her boot and went in.

Inside, the flat was narrow and warm and full of the very specific smell of wood smoke from a low fire, lemon, and something herbal from a kitchen Ruth could not see. The front room was lit by two table lamps and the small open hearth, the curtains drawn, the floorboards bare and worn pale in a track from the door to the hearth. A woman was on the sofa with a glass of dark red wine held away from herself, looking at the fire. Two more women were at the small dining table along the side wall, one of them sketching something on the back of an envelope while the other looked over her shoulder. Their bodies were close together at the table in the way that bodies are close when their proximity has stopped being remarked.

Three. Ruth counted without appearing to count.

A fourth came through from a doorway at the back of the room, drying her hands on a tea towel.

She was tall, thin, in her mid-twenties, dark hair cut short above her collar, a man's grey shirt under an old cardigan, trousers, bare feet. There was nothing about her dress that announced anything in particular. The trousers were what a woman in this room wore on a Thursday and the bareness of her feet was the casual ease of someone in her own house. But the alignment of her shoulders, the way she stood holding the tea towel, registered to Ruth with a clarity Ruth had no language for.

'Judith,' the woman said.

'Clara.'

Clara nodded once at Ruth without smiling. Not unfriendly. Working out the room.

'Ruth MacLeod,' Judith said. 'The one I mentioned.'

'Latin and Greek.'

'Yes,' Ruth said.

Clara looked at her for a moment longer than was social and then looked away. 'There is tea on the side. Or wine, if Frances has not finished it.'

'I have not,' Frances said from the sofa, lifting the glass without turning. 'I am pacing myself.'

'She is not pacing herself,' Clara said. 'Sit down anywhere. Take what you want.'

Then she went back into the kitchen.

Ruth took a cup of tea from the tray on the side table. She did not sit. She stood near the table because the wall behind it gave her something to align against. The room continued its conversations as if she had been absorbed without being introduced. Frances said something to the women at the table. The woman with the envelope laughed, a low laugh that was older than her face. The other answered her in Italian, in a quick aside that did not appear to be intended for the room, and they laughed together over the envelope.

The door behind Ruth had not been closed. The line of warm light still fell onto the iron of the landing. The small cool draught from the stairwell came through and met the heat of the fire halfway across the room.

Ruth stayed where she was.

Frances on the sofa was perhaps thirty-five, tired in a way that had settled into the line of her shoulders. Her hair was pinned in a way that had clearly been done at five in the morning and not touched since. The wine in her glass was not her first. She was not drunk. She was the precise distance from sober that medicine allowed, and Ruth, watching her without making a study of it, recognised the distance from women she had seen at Mass at the convent on Sunday mornings and from her own father at the kitchen table on certain Saturdays. Frances did not look at Ruth and did not pretend not to. She left her in the air of the room without comment.

The two at the table were Hannah and Mathilde. Hannah was sketching, in a quick economical hand, the outline of something Ruth could not see from where she stood. Mathilde was looking, sometimes correcting a line with a small movement of her finger over the paper without touching the paper. Mathilde was older, perhaps forty, dark hair lightly grey at the temples, the sort of face that did not arrange itself for a room. She was wearing a high-collared blouse the colour of old ivory. When she said something to Hannah it was in the same low Italian she had used before, and Hannah answered in the same Italian without looking up. They had clearly come into the language together in another country and brought it with them.

Clara came back through with another bottle. She stopped beside Ruth at the table.

'Tibullus,' she said, glancing at the dark spine of the book Ruth had brought through habit and laid down beside the tea cup, then at Ruth. 'Are you on him at this point?'

'A passage. We are not formally on him.'

'Which?'

Ruth said the lines.

Clara looked at her, attentive. 'The Delia poems.' She paused. 'Do you read them as he wants them read.'

'Mostly.'

'Mostly.' She turned the bottle in her hand without opening it. 'And the rest?'

Ruth held the cup of tea against her palm. The cup was warm. The room was waiting, in the way that rooms with women in them sometimes wait, attentive without making an event of the attention.

'He wants us to take Delia as the centre,' Ruth said. 'He arranges his absence around her. The reader is positioned to feel the loss of her. But the loss does not behave the way real loss behaves. It is too ordered. The poems have been sorted before they reach the page. I find I do not believe him about Delia.'

Clara made a sound. Nothing like a laugh. Approval that did not announce itself.

'Who do you believe him about?'

'Marathus.'

A flicker passed across the room. Frances did not look up. Hannah and Mathilde at the table did not interrupt their envelope. But the air did the thing air does in a room of women when an unspoken thing has been brushed against without being named.

'Why him,' Clara said.

'Because the poems about him are uneven. They embarrass themselves. They do things Tibullus did not plan to do and could not edit out. That is how I read sincerity.'

Clara looked at her, fully and without urgency.

'Sit down, Ruth,' she said.

Ruth sat down at the table.

Mathilde looked up from the envelope and said, in English now, 'You have not read him at the convent, then.'

'The convent did not give us Tibullus.'

'No. They did not give us Catullus either, in Florence. But we read him.' Mathilde returned to the envelope. 'Some books do not need to be set.'

Hannah, beside her, drew another line on the paper. Ruth, looking now, saw that the sketch was a roof line of the harbour buildings on the Foreshore, drawn quickly from memory, the cranes rising above the warehouses.

'You are a photographer,' Ruth said, a question and not.

'I am.'

'What were you doing on the Foreshore today?'

Hannah looked up. Her eyes were a clear pale grey. 'A commission. They want photographs of the new harbour works. The light there is impossible because everything is concrete and water and there is too much of both. I came home and I cannot stop drawing it.'

'Hannah does not photograph people any more,' Frances said from the sofa, without turning.

'Frances.'

'I am only telling Ruth.'

'You are telling Ruth a complicated thing as a simple thing.' Hannah's tone did not change but something in her face did, briefly. She set the pencil down. 'I photograph harbours and buildings now. I used to photograph people. I stopped.'

She did not say why. She picked the pencil back up.

Ruth filed this in the same place she had filed the fraction of a second in the corridor outside Latin.

The conversation drifted into the reportage of the evening. Frances had been at Groote Schuur all day and had a story about a surgical registrar whose handwriting was so bad that the night sister had developed a private ledger of what he meant when he wrote certain things. Hannah said the registrar's father had been at her parents' house for tennis last summer and had identical handwriting. Frances said this confirmed her suspicion that the family had been conducting medicine by guesswork for two generations. Mathilde said something in Italian and Hannah translated for the table: the Italians had a saying about doctors' handwriting that did not survive translation. Frances poured herself another half a glass.

Through this Clara stayed at the table, sometimes contributing, sometimes not. She did not ask Ruth a further direct question. She did not look at Ruth in any sustained way. But Ruth was aware, from the way the conversation moved around her, of being the new presence in a room that had decided, provisionally, to make space for her without testing her any further this evening.

She was also aware of the door. It had not closed. The constant draught off the stairwell had not changed. The wood smoke from the hearth was being drawn very faintly towards the door and out, and the fire had to work harder than it would have done otherwise, and the room was warm without being hot. The arrangement was deliberate. She had understood that from the beginning without articulating it.

Then there were footsteps on the iron stairs.

Two pairs, climbing. The room did not stop. Frances's glass did not go down. Hannah did not lift the pencil. But Ruth saw Mathilde's hand still on the back of Hannah's chair. Saw Clara turn her head, very slightly, towards the door. Saw the shift in the angle of Frances's shoulders. The footsteps reached the top landing. Paused. A man's voice said something in Afrikaans to another man's voice, both of them too low for the words to carry. Then the steps moved on, past the green door, towards the next flight of stairs that climbed to whatever was above the flat.

Mathilde's hand left the back of the chair. Clara turned back to the table. Frances took the next sip of her wine.

'The neighbours,' Clara said quietly to Ruth, as if it were only a piece of practical information about the building. 'Two brothers. They keep the sort of hours that involve coming home at this time.'

Ruth nodded. Her tea cup had cooled at some point. She had not noticed when.

The conversation resumed as if it had never paused, but Ruth's count of the room had altered. She understood now that the door left ajar was tactical. A closed door at the top of an iron staircase in a flat with five women in it required explanation in a way that an open door did not.

Frances said, after a moment, 'I do not like enclosed rooms.'

'Which is why we leave the door,' Mathilde said, not unkindly.

'Among other reasons.'

Frances did not elaborate. Ruth understood that this was true and was also a more general explanation, and she understood, with the same clarity with which she had understood Clara's standing earlier, that some things in this room were said in such a way as to mean two things at once and she was being shown how to listen.

Clara got up after a while and went to a small bookshelf in the corner. She brought back a slim brown volume and set it down beside Ruth on the table without opening it.

'Borrow this,' she said. 'There is a passage in the second elegy. Marathus on the boat. Tell me what you make of it.'

She went back into the kitchen.

Ruth set the volume next to the cup of tea. The cover was cloth, soft from use, faintly warm where the lamp fell on it. She did not open it.

Frances looked up. 'You are pleased.' Not to Ruth. To the room.

'We are always pleased when Clara is pleased,' Hannah said. 'It saves us trouble.'

Mathilde laughed once into Hannah's shoulder and corrected another line on the envelope.

By half past nine Frances had gone to catch the last tram back to Observatory, leaving with the careful dignity of a woman who had been pacing herself precisely. Hannah and Mathilde left together not long after, not making a thing of leaving together, picking up a single coat from the same hook as if a single coat were enough between them. The flat thinned without seeming to. Judith was on the sofa now, talking to Clara at the table about something that had happened at the Cohens' the previous Friday, a quarrel between Mr Cohen and a man who had come for advice about a letter to a paper. Ruth listened without being included in the listening, the brown volume on the table beside her hand.

She rose. 'I should go.'

Clara looked at her. 'You should. The last tram down Kloof is at ten. Judith, are you coming or staying?'

'I am coming.'

Clara walked them to the door. The door was still ajar. She had not closed it once during the entire evening. Ruth had been peripherally aware of this, the way a person is peripherally aware that the air in the room is moving, and only now understood the full sequence of reasons for it.

Clara saw Ruth notice. She did not explain a second time.

'Goodnight, Ruth.'

Clara did not put a hand on Ruth's arm. She did not lean in. She held the door frame with one hand and stayed in her own doorway, and her gaze was steady and entirely without commentary, and Ruth understood, without being told, that this restraint was itself a form of address and that she was being asked, without being asked, to register it.

She registered it.

Then she went down the iron stairs after Judith and did not look back at the door.

The street was cold at the lower end of Brownlow Road, the night-wind off Lion's Head finding the gap between her coat collar and her neck. The houses they had passed coming up were dark now. Only the streetlamps along Kloof Street lit the way down, the lamps yellow and irregularly spaced. A dog barked once, somewhere up the slope, and stopped.

Judith said nothing at first. They walked.

At the corner of Kloof Street she said, 'You did well.'

Ruth did not answer.

'She does not lend the Tibullus to many people.'

'Why did she lend it to me?'

'Because of what you said about Marathus.'

Ruth held the brown volume against her side, under the coat. The cloth of it was no longer warm. The cool of the night had gone into it through the coat.

'And what does it mean,' Ruth said, 'that she lent it to me.'

Judith looked at her with the regard with which Judith sometimes looked at her, the regard of a woman who had grown up in a different city and was trying to translate it without translating too much.

'Nothing has to mean anything yet,' Judith said. 'You will see her again or you will not. She has put the question without asking it. That is how it is done.'

The tram came up Kloof Street and they got on. Ruth sat by the window on the down-side and looked at the city falling away below them, the streetlamps along the slopes one by one, the ocean dark and unlit beyond them, the District a collected pattern of lit windows further south. She thought, very briefly and without finishing the thought, of Sarah's hands closing the wax-paper parcel of brisket in the Cohens' kitchen, of Thomas Lawrence's hand on the cold iron of the railing in Oranjezicht, of the woman in the soft white apron who had folded her coat with such practised economy. Each was a different kind of door.

She did not finish the thought.

The tram took them down to the lower city. Judith got off two stops before her own to make sure Ruth had the right connection back to the residence, and then they parted at the lower stop with the brevity of women who had said what they were going to say. Ruth rode the last tram up Main Road alone with the brown volume in her lap.

The residence was quiet when she came in. The corridor lamp was on at the far end. The rest was dark. She walked the length of the corridor to her own door with the purposeful sound of her boots on the boards.

She put the brown volume on the desk beside the still-closed notebook.

She did not open either.

She unlaced her boots and set them aside, and she sat on the edge of the bed, and she looked at the corner of the desk where the brown volume lay against the spine of the notebook, the two objects making a small geometry against the lamp.

Behind her on the wall the mountain pressed close in the dark, invisible and entire.

Down the corridor a door closed, and another door opened and closed, and somewhere in the building a kettle went on and a quiet voice spoke to another quiet voice, and the residence, like any building of women in a city in 1949, settled into the particular night it was going to have.

She turned the lamp out.

She sat in the dark on the edge of the bed with her hands in her lap, and she listened to the wind beginning to pick up in the pines along the residence drive.

She did not close her own door.

# Chapter 12: Names for Hunger

The Jagger Library was a sanctuary of high, vaulted ceilings and the muffled, reverent sound of turning pages—a sound that Ruth found mirrored the dry, intellectual dust of a hundred years being disturbed. It was a place where the mountain's wind was reduced to a low, rhythmic thrum against the thick glass of the windows, a Stable Geography that the state used to insulate its own thinkers from the disturbance of the world below. Ruth spent her Tuesday mornings here, tucked into a wooden carrel that smelled of cedar, linseed oil, and the sharp, synthetic sting of old paper. In the silence, her mind began to expand, the structures of ancient syntax providing a scaffold for her own disordered thoughts, her Nervous Constitution.

She thrived on the difficulty of the task. The more complex the translation, the more she could disappear into it, her fingers moving across the page with a Mechanical, Bored Precision that masked her own desperate hunger for belonging. Today, the text was a passage from the *Aeneid*, the words of a hero driven by a divine mandate he couldn't quite refuse. *Arma virumque cano.* I sing of arms and the man. It was a language of conquest, of order, of the state. It was a polished grammar that validated the exclusions of the living. But as she sat in the silent room, the words felt like a hollow, metallic thud against the stone. They didn't have a name for the hunger she felt after the mission school meetings or the Friday nights at the Cohens'.

'You’re staring at the ink again, Ruth,' a voice whispered from the next carrel. 

It was Judith, her emerald scarf a dark, expensive shadow in the library's sepia light. She move with a quick, confident grace that suggested she had no fear of the Registrar's stamp or the Warden's administrative review. She smelled of an expensive shop in the city, a scent that Ruth found both alluring and invasive, a piece of the Double Life that Judith seemed to navigate with such easy, unthinking confidence.

'I'm parsing,' Ruth said, her voice a low, melodic hum that cut through the library's silence. 

'You're hiding,' Judith corrected, her eyes dancing with a light that Ruth didn't yet know how to translate. 'You think if you're thorough enough with the dead, the living won't be able to find you. But Arbuthnot is waiting. The Sophocles seminar is in an hour, and he's still talking about the Proportion. He thinks the Classics are a Stable Geography that can protect the mind from the Dark Grammar of the present. He thinks the hexameter is a wall.'

Ruth looked at the page. The Latin was a machinery of control, a grammar of empire. It was the Logic of the Dead, and she was starting to feel the isolation of the archives. 'I can't find the names here, Judith. Not the real ones. The ones Sarah uses. The ones Clara uses.'

'Then don't look in the library,' Judith said, her voice dropping a register as a librarian moved past with a Mechanical, Bored Precision. 'The university treats knowledge like a prize to be won, a curated self they can award a fellowship to. But the city... the city is a theatre of discovery that doesn't require a matriculation code. Come to the flat tonight. Clara says the door is left ajar for a girl who looks like she's about to be eaten by the rock. She has books there, Ruth. Books that don't have the university stamp. Books that have been unmade.'

The walk from the university campus was always a somatic act of unmaking. The campus was a Stable Geography of grey granite, high vaulted ceilings, and a pervasive, quiet confidence that seemed to have been imported directly from a London that no longer existed. At UCT, every movement was measured with a Mechanical, Bored Precision that literalised the partitioning she only heard about in the Karoo. The stone steps were scrubbed to a Pious Precision, the grass was kept in a state of controlled, emerald isolation, and the students moved in tidy, unthinking currents. To walk down the slope was to feel the Academic Shroud beginning to slip, the Logic of the Dead losing its grip on her Nervous Constitution. 

The descent toward the city was as much a change in climate as in altitude. The thin, brittle air of the upper slopes, scrubbed clean by the heights and carrying the scent of pine and old stone, gave way to the heavy, salt-tasting weight of the urban basin. As she walked, Ruth felt the weight of her scholarship folder under her arm, the cardboard edges fraying against her ribs. It was a warrant of her own curated self, a document that proved she was a guest in the house of knowledge. But as the university's white pillars receded, the authority of the folder seemed to dissolve in the coastal glare.

As she entered Gardens, the city’s order began to fracture. The houses here were different—narrower, closer to the street, their facades a map of peeling cream and weathered stone. Instead of the university’s cedar-scented archives, these buildings carried the scent of domesticity: fried onions, laundry soap, and the sharp, synthetic sting of coal smoke from the kitchen ranges. The flat was located in a building of grey stone and peeling cream paint that sat on the edge of Gardens, where the mountain's shadow was a constant, cool presence. Its windows were high and narrow, its paint shedding like skin from a sun-baked Karoo snake. The iron gate at the entrance groaned with a low-frequency tremor that made Ruth’s teeth ache, a sound of industrial necessity that the residential drawing rooms of the Lawrences would never permit.

Ruth followed the stairs, the wood creaking under her boots with a rhythmic, ghoema-beat of warning. Every door she passed was a Theatre of Discovery, a world of secrets that the Registrar’s judgment would never acknowledge. She heard the muffled, high-pitched frequency of a radio play in a language she didn't recognize, the clatter of a domestic argument behind a locked door, and the smell of a heavy, spice-laden stew. She felt the Atmospheric Pressure of the building—a social weight that pushed against her ribs, a sense of being an interloper in a sanctuary of the unnamed.

Clara was sitting by the fireplace, her hands cradling a book whose cover was a plain, unmarked grey. She didn't look up as Ruth entered; she remained perfectly still, her presence a anchor in the shifting light of the room. The only sound was the crackle of the wood and the distant, rhythmic shush of the sea through the open window. The light in the room was a soft, bruised violet, caught from the setting sun and held by the heavy velvet curtains that smelled of clove and cold air.

'You're back,' Clara said, her voice carrying no surprise. It was a smooth, modulated instrument that reached Ruth through the haze of clove smoke. 'Most people take longer to decide if the door is worth the draft. The university teaches you to fear the cold, doesn’t it? To prefer the warmth of the library’s cedar halls even when they’re full of ghosts. They want you comfortable in your ignorance. They want you to be a Warden of the State before you’ve even learned your own name.'

'I wanted to know the names,' Ruth said. She sat on the floor, the cold wood biting into her legs, the physical sensation a grounding force against the Atmospheric Pressure of her own uncertainty. 'In the university, we have names for the gods and the Fates. We have the Logic of the Dead to validate the exclusions of the living. We parse the Latin to prove we belong to the order. But they don't say anything about... this. They don't have a name for the hunger that doesn't fit the syllabus. Arbuthnot speaks of the Proportion as if it were a law of physics. But here, the proportion is all wrong. Here, the view is everything.'

'They wouldn't,' Clara said, finally looking at her. Her eyes were sharp, intelligent, and unblinking. She was wearing a man's white shirt, the sleeves rolled up to reveal forearms that were tanned and muscular, a somatic act of refusal that Ruth found both alluring and invasive. 'The university is a machine for maintaining the order. It’s a Bureaucratic Theatre where they perform the stability of empire while the foundations are rotting. And this... this is the disorder. This is the part of the map they leave blank because they don't have the stomach to draw the shore. They'll teach you the Proportion to prevent you from naming the hunger of the living.'

She handed the grey book to Ruth. It was a translation of a French novel, a brittle manuscript with yellowed pages that smelled of cheap tobacco and intellectual dust. Ruth opened it to a page where the text was interrupted by narrow margins of handwritten notes. The ink was a dark, saturated blue, the letters formed with a quick, purposeful grace.

'Read the part about the unburied,' Clara suggested, her gaze fixed on the fire. 

Ruth cleared her throat. Her voice sounded thin in the vaulted room, a translation of her own hunger. '“The state requires the dead to be silent,”' she read. '“But the unburied have a voice that the law cannot parse. They exist in the Dark Grammar of the streets, in the gaps of the Stable Geography. To love outside the boxes is to become unburied. It is a dangerous freedom that moves through the city like a draft.”'

The words felt like a somatic act of recognition. They weren't just descriptions; they were warrants. They mirrored her own Between Two Worlds state—the girl who was too educated for the farm and too provincial for the Lawrences, the girl who was scholarship-narrowed and District-awakened. She felt a low-frequency tremor in her own hands as she held the book. The French translation didn't use the polite, academic register of the university; it used the language of the living.

The shelves of the flat were not like the Jagger Library's cedar-scented stacks. There were no barcodes, no institutional stamps, no leather-bound diaries that carried the scent of expensive perfume and the unmistakable frequency of wealth. These were brittle, typed manuscripts and slim collections of poetry passed from hand to hand like secrets. Many had no names on the covers, only codes or initials. They smelled of clove, old paper, and a pervasive, quiet desperation that Ruth found more honest than Aunt Catherine's performance of recovery.

Ruth stood up and began to explore the labels. She found a volume of poems by Constantine Cavafy, the Greek letters appearing like small, dark birds on the page. She found the poem about the windows and read it silently, her mind parsing the modern Greek with a somatic focus she never applied to Homer. The words spoke of a view that was not allowed, a perspective that the state’s Stable Geography would find threatening. It mirrored the way she looked at the mountain from the siding—not as a rock, but as a witness to her own unmaking.

'Judith says you're thorough,' Clara said, watching her. 'But thoroughness is just another way to get caught if you don't know what you're looking for. You're looking for a name for the hunger, aren't you? A name that doesn't sound like a judgment. A name that doesn't require a confession. A name that isn't a sentencing.'

'I'm looking for a way to exist without apologizing for the space I take up,' Ruth said. She thought of her father’s Mechanical, Bored Precision as he traced the edge of his coffee mug, of her mother’s Pious Precision in the kitchen. 'On the farm, the silence is the only language we have. In the university, the noise is the only language they allow. But here... here the silence has a name. Here, the air is thick with it.'

'That's the most expensive thing in the city,' Clara murmured. 'The freedom to occupy a room without the Registrar's permission. To be a person who doesn't need to be translated. But you're carrying a lot of Dangerous Freedom for a scholarship girl, Ruth. You’ve got the north in your name and the District in your heart. That's a recipe for a Nervous Constitution if ever I saw one. The Registrar knows, doesn't he? He's already marked you. He's already looking for the crack in the facade.'

Ruth flinched at the mention of the Registrar. She thought of the stamp hitting her folder with a heavy, final thud. 'He says I'm on probation. He says I must be indifferent to the world's violence. He says I'm a representative of the British House.'

'Indifference is a luxury for the dead,' Clara said, her voice dropping a register as the light failed and the shadows grew long. 'The living have to choose. And you've already chosen the District. You've already chosen the Bo-Kaap lights. You think the mountain hasn't noticed? You think the state hasn't started the new laws? The Immorality Act is just the beginning, Ruth. They want to turn the city into a series of boxes. They want to keep every world inside its own separate room.'

The Atmospheric Pressure of the room shifted as Clara reached out. Her fingers traced the edge of Ruth's jaw, a gesture so simple and so consequential that it made the very air feel electric. It was a recognition. It was a somatic act of recognition that bypassed the brain and went straight to the bone. 

Ruth didn't flinch. She felt the touch as a physical anchor, a counter-weight to the isolation she felt at the university. In the Jagger, she felt like a ghost among the archives; here, she felt like a person whose body was finally being read in its original language, not a translation. The scent of clove cigarettes and cold air was a thick, sensory soup that she wanted to inhabit forever.

'You're not afraid of the rock, are you?' Clara asked. 

'I've lived with it my whole life,' Ruth said. Her voice felt steady, anchored by the truth of what she was feeling. 'The farm, the convent... they were all made of rock. The Geography of Silence was a condition of my upbringing. But this... this is the first time the rock has felt as if it might be a shelter. This is the first time I've heard a song that didn't sound like a translation of something else.'

'It's a shelter until it collapses,' Clara warned, but she didn't move her hand away. Her touch was a promise, a dangerous freedom that didn't have a name for the mahogany and the brisket of the Cohen table. 

Ruth looked at the books again, the brittle pages and the cracked spines. They were a constellation of resilience, a way of existing that required the maintenance of a schedule that didn't acknowledge the human cost of the transition. She realized that the most expensive thing in the city was the freedom to love outside the state's boxes. 

She picked up another volume—a slim book of poems by a woman who spoke of desire as a language of loss. The prose was sharp, idiomatic, and unmistakable. It spoke of Clandestine Intimacy as a somatic reality, a deep, resonant vibration that made her believe, for a moment, that the maps could be rewritten.

'Listen to this,' Ruth said, her fingers tracing the foreign script. '“The gods do not forgive a lack of attention. But they also do not forgive those who try to hide from the Fates behind a pile of books. Curiosity is a somatic act of refusal.”'

Clara laughed, a sharp, delighted sound. 'Arbuthnot would hate that. He thinks the books are the shield. He thinks the Classics are a Stable Geography that can protect the mind from the Dark Grammar of the present. But you know better, don’t you, Ruth? You’ve seen the mountain from the siding. You know the rock doesn’t care about your prizes or your matriculation codes. You know the rock is always listening.'

'I know that the higher I climb, the more I have to hide,' Ruth said. 'The Lawrences, the university... they’re all rooms with views. But the view is always a mirror. They only see the version of me that fits their map. They only see the scholarship girl with the northern vowels.'

'And here?' Clara asked, her gaze steady. 

'Here... I'm a witness,' Ruth said. 

The conversation shifted then, moving away from the books and into the somatic reality of the risk they were taking. Clara spoke of the wardens of the state, the men in grey overalls who were already installing the Whites Only signs at Muizenberg, the men who were tagging the buildings in District Six with a Mechanical, Bored Precision.

'They’re not just building walls,' Clara said, her voice dropping a register as the firelight failed. 'They’re building a language of erasure. They’ll use the Immorality Act to turn our desire into a crime. The state is obsessed with Social Hygiene. They want to prevent the "mixture" of desire just as much as the "mixture" of people. They want to keep every world inside its own separate box. They want a city that is clean, Ruth. Clean like a hospital. No noise. Just the boxes and the silence.'

Ruth felt a sharp wave of dread. She understood that she was on a threshold that could not be uncrossed. Once you know the door is there, you can't go back to pretending it's just a wall. The city was opening for her, but every door she walked through seemed to reveal the same dark, hardening grammar of a map that was being rewritten in the language of exclusion. 

'I don't want the safety of the curated life,' Ruth said, her voice sounding louder than she intended in the quiet room. 'I'm tired of the Proportion. I'm tired of the Logic of the Dead. I want a name for the hunger, even if the name is a sentencing.'

Clara smiled then, a brief, delighted sound. 'Then you've already chosen. But don't think the name is a sanctuary. In this city, a name is a weapon. And you're going to have to learn how to use it before the wardens of the state find you. Curiosity is a dangerous freedom, Ruth. It leads to the unmaking of the self.'

They sat for a long time in the dim light, the clove smoke a grey haze in the air, the mountain a silent, stony witness to their arrival. Ruth felt the somatic reality of the secret, a deep, resonant vibration that matched the pulse in her own throat. She was no longer just between two worlds—she was catching the same ghoema beat of resistance that she had heard in the mission school, a realization that the most important grammar she would learn was the language of her own refusal to be erased. 

The secret became a source of isolation, too. As Ruth walked back toward the university residence, the salt air cold against her face, she felt a new, sharper kind of distance from the other students in the residence hall. She saw them laughing in the quadrangle the next morning, their lives a series of unexamined transitions, and felt a wave of pity for their safety. They didn't know the names for their own hungers. They were guests in a world they hadn't had to choose, inhabiting a Stable Geography that they mistook for the whole world.

Ruth climbed onto the tram, the wooden seats cold against her legs. She looked back at the lights of Gardens, a small, bright constellation of resilience nestled against the mountain. She felt the seductive power of the city, the way it offered both self-invention and the cost of witnessing. She was no longer just a scholarship girl; she was a woman whose desire was a political act, a dangerous freedom that would eventually force her to choose between the safety of her curated life and the costly honesty of the world she had begun to name. 

The tram vibration hit her first—a rhythmic, industrial clatter that felt established and indifferent. It sounded like the Mechanical, Bored Precision of the schedules, a vibration of paranoia that was as much a part of the city as the granite arches. She watched the city pass through the vibrating glass—the high, granite facades of the banks, the iron railings of the shops, and the Europeans Only signs that marked the boundaries of the state's boxes. The signs were fixed to the pillars with a Mechanical, Bored Precision that made their violence feel domestic.

She was returning to the university's Stable Geography, to the high vaulted ceilings and the cedar-scented halls. But she was carrying a Dark Grammar in her heart, a realization that her Dangerous Freedom was not a thing to be feared, but a thing to be claimed. The mountain followed her, its face dark and ancient, a silent witness to her refusal, a reminder that the most important thing she would learn was the language of her own desire. 

When she reached the residence, the morning was already arriving with a sharp, coastal glare. The rising bell rang—not the low, brassy clangour of the residence, but a distant, mechanical ring that seemed to be coming from the very heart of the mountain. Ruth move with a synchronized, bored efficiency, her fingers tracing the edge of her scholarship papers as she prepared for the day. Every movement was a performance of her own Double Life, a realization that the most important thing she would learn was not the grammar of the dead, but the language of her own hunger. 

She opened her Latin notebook, the ink dry and final on the page. *Arma virumque cano.* I sing of arms and the man. It was a language of conquest, of order, of the state. But as she sat in the silent room, she felt a different rhythm—the Ghoema beat of the District, the salt air of the Bo-Kaap, and the scent of clove from the Gardens flat. She wasn't singing of arms or men anymore. She was singing of the unburied, of the unnamed, and of the hunger that the state could not parse.

She began to write, her pen scratching against the paper with a new, fierce energy. She wasn't translating Virgil; she was translating her own life. She used the Dark Grammar of the District to name the exclusionary laws of the university. She used the Ghoema soul of the Bo-Kaap to describe the rigid, administrative order of the residence. It was a somatic act of refusal, a choosing of the unmade self over the curated identity.

She looked at the mountain, visible through the high window of her room. It was no longer just a rock; it was a witness. It had seen the siding, the drought, the camps, and the removals. It had seen the names for hunger and the names for loss. And now, it was seeing Ruth.

'I see you,' Ruth whispered, the words catching in the air. 

The university was a sanctuary of the dead, but the city was a theatre of the living. Ruth chose the living. She chose the dangerous freedom. She chose the names that didn't fit the syllabus. And as the morning light filled the room, she knew that the passing would be a series of tests she was finally ready to pass. Every door she walked through revealed the same wall, but she was learning how to find the gaps. She was learning how to breathe in the Draft.

# Chapter 12: Names for Hunger

She had slept badly, in the specific way of a person who had not slept much rather than not slept at all, and woken before the bell with the brown Tibullus on the desk where she had left it the night before, beside the still-closed notebook.

She lay for a moment looking at the ceiling. The room was cold. The condensation on the window had set during the night into a film that still held the shape of the wind off the rock face. She got up, washed at the basin, dressed in the same dark dress because the other was still at the laundry, and put the brown volume into her satchel without opening it.

Breakfast in General Botha Hall was the usual subdued machinery of Friday morning: porridge, the tea urn, the smell of margarine on toast that had been kept warm too long, twenty-three girls eating with the forward postures of people working through it before chapel. Miriam was at the far end of the long table with two of the medical scholarship girls. Ruth took a place at the near end. She drank the tea. She read the Cape Times leader without registering what it was about, the page held flat against the salt cellar.

She was on her way out to the corridor when she met Miss Pryce at the door.

Miss Pryce was always already in the corridor at this hour. It was something Ruth had observed without making a study of it: the warden's mornings began, by some private calendar, before the breakfast bell, and by the time the girls were leaving the dining hall she was already in motion through the building, a clipboard in one hand, the keys in the other, doing the rounds that constituted, in her view, the moral architecture of the residence.

'Miss MacLeod.'

Ruth stopped.

Miss Pryce was sixty or near it, dark skirt, dark jacket, iron-grey hair set close to the head, the close-set pale eyes that did their measuring quickly and registered nothing they found surprising. She held the clipboard against her chest with a flat hand. The other hand held the keys against her side without their making a sound.

'Good morning, Warden.'

'Good morning. Are you well.'

'Yes, thank you.'

'You look tired.' Miss Pryce said it kindly, and the kindness sat exactly where the consideration would have sat in any other woman, and was therefore not different from consideration in any way one could prove. 'Are you sleeping well in four-twelve.'

'Mostly.'

'I find that scholarship girls, in their first year, sometimes underestimate the demands of the term on the body.' She made an adjustment to the clipboard. 'I noticed that you came in late on Thursday evening. Twice last week. Once the week before. I do not, as you know, keep a record. But I do notice.'

Ruth held her satchel against her hip. The brown Tibullus was inside it, unopened, against her ribs.

'I had been at a friend's.'

'Of course.' The clipboard shifted. 'A scholarship girl's social life is not the warden's concern, except in so far as it bears on her work. I trust you are managing your work.'

'I am.'

'Good.' Miss Pryce held her gaze for a moment longer than was social and then released it. 'There is a card on the noticeboard about the Sunday tea. Eleanor Stratford-Reid is convening it. I should be pleased to see you there. The girls who do well in the residence are the ones who allow the residence to do well by them. Do you understand me, Miss MacLeod.'

'I understand you, Warden.'

'Good. Have a fine day.'

She moved on down the corridor without waiting to see Ruth move.

Ruth stayed by the door for a moment with the satchel held against her hip. Her pulse was steady. Her hands were steady. She looked at the brass strip along the threshold of the dining-room door, at the way it had been polished smooth in the centre by years of girls stepping over it, and she filed the conversation in the place where she filed things she had not yet finished understanding.

The light along the corridor was the cold morning light that came through the high window at the far end. The high window held the rectangle of the Atlantic and a fragment of the sky. Ruth walked the length of the corridor and went down the stairs.

The walk to the Jagger Library at this hour was through the upper paths of the Hiddingh campus, through the avenue of pines that ran along the residence drive and down towards the gymnasium, past the tennis courts, the grass already in late-summer brown along the side furrow. The mountain above her was clear today, no cloud on the rock face, the light very clean. The pines smelled of pine and of last week's rain in the lower needles. She walked with the satchel against her side and did not think directly about Miss Pryce. The conversation was already at work somewhere below the level of thinking, the way a small splinter does its work in a finger before it announces itself.

The Jagger was empty at this hour. Friday morning, before the ten o'clock lectures, was the only time it was. Ruth went up to the second-floor reading room, took her usual carrel along the western wall, and laid the brown Tibullus on the desk beside the notebook.

She opened both.

The brown volume was the Lobeck edition with English notes. It opened by use to the second elegy of the third book, the page softened by other hands. Marathus on the boat. The edge of the leaf was faintly grey at the corner where many fingers had held it.

She read the lines slowly, in Latin, without translating. She read them again, translating. She read them a third time, and translated the relevant passage onto the right-hand page of the notebook in her own hand.

The passage was the one Clara had named.

She wrote it small and neat. Her translation took twenty minutes. She did not paraphrase. She did not soften. She wrote what the lines said, in the order they said it, with the line breaks held to the Latin where they could be held. When she was done she read what she had written, and then read the Latin against it, and then sat for a moment with the pen held above the paper without touching it.

The poem said what she had said to Clara. The poem said it more directly than she had said it. The boat was a boat. The boy was a boy. The grief was grief that had not been arranged. The lines did not behave themselves.

She closed the brown volume.

She did not close the notebook.

The Jagger had a small section of modern poetry along the upper gallery, donated by an emeritus professor whose name was not on the labels but whose tastes were. Ruth had walked past it twice in her first weeks at UCT without going up. Today she went up. The section was four shelves long, the books mostly hardback with paper jackets that had begun to fade in the southern sun. She moved along them, reading spines, until she found a slim hardback bound in a faded green cloth: *Poems by C. P. Cavafy. Translated from the Greek with introduction and notes by John Mavrogordato. Hogarth Press, 1948.*

She took it down and carried it to the carrel.

She sat for a moment with both books open in front of her, the brown Tibullus on the left, the green Cavafy on the right. The light from the high western window fell across the page in a clean rectangle.

She read the Cavafy poem about the windows. She read it twice in the English. The English fell short in some places, but the shape held. In the dark rooms where I spend oppressive days I walk back and forth searching for the windows. It will be a great relief if a window opens. But the windows are not to be found, or I cannot find them. And it is better, perhaps, that I do not find them. Perhaps the light will be a new tyranny.

She held the book in her hand without writing anything.

Then she wrote two lines under the Tibullus translation, in the same neat hand, and underlined the second.

*The hunger does not require a house. It requires a window.*
*Perhaps the light will be a new tyranny.*

She did not write anything else.

She held the green Cavafy in her hand and put the brown Tibullus on top of it and sat for a moment looking at the high window of the reading room, at the rectangle of mountain it framed, and the rectangle of sky above the rock face. The shape of the window was the shape of the rectangle. The shape of the rectangle was the shape of the page.

She filed this without writing it down. There was no need to write it down. It would not leave.

At a quarter to ten she closed the books and put the green Cavafy back on the upper gallery and went down the stairs of the Jagger. The stairs were stone and the descent had a small echo. She came out into the morning sun on the steps of the library.

Arbuthnot was on the steps coming up.

He did not see her at first. He was reading a typed sheet, holding it close to his face, the silver-framed spectacles low on his nose. The black gown carried its scent of pipe smoke and old wool into the morning air. He came up two steps before he registered the figure descending and stopped.

'Miss MacLeod.'

'Sir.'

'Coming from the library.'

'Yes.'

'On a Friday morning before lectures.' He said this without inflection, as if confirming a small piece of practical information. 'Did you find what you went up for.'

'I did.'

'Excellent.' He looked at her directly for the first time. The gaze was the gaze of a man for whom looking was a tool, and the tool had been set, briefly, against her. 'I had a thought yesterday that I should have given you in the lecture and did not. The Tibullus you mentioned in the tutorial. The third book is contested. Some of it is not Tibullus at all. The Marathus poems, however, are Tibullus, on the best evidence. The textual question has historically been used to dispose of him. I should not, were I you, allow myself to be disposed of by a textual question.' He nodded once and continued up the steps.

She did not turn.

She held the satchel against her hip and walked the rest of the way down the steps and out onto the lower path that ran along the front of the Hiddingh buildings.

The city below was loud at this hour, the ten o'clock movement, the trams and the delivery vans on Long Street and the harbour drays on Strand. She did not yet know whether she had a lecture at eleven or whether she had cancelled it from herself in advance. She walked down through the gardens of the Company's Garden, past the squirrels around the oak trunks, past the old grey palms and the new beds, towards the lower city.

She walked until she came to the bottom of Adderley and crossed Strand under the overhead tramwires and went on towards the Foreshore.

She had not planned this. She did not plan it as she went, either. She walked because the other thing she might do was to go back to the residence and sit in Room 412 with what Miss Pryce had said still working on her, and the residence was not where she wanted that work to be done. The Foreshore was where she went instead, because the Foreshore was the part of Cape Town that was being made and unmade at the same time, and the Foreshore did not pretend that anything was settled.

The new docks works were enormous. Concrete piers were going down into the water along the line of the old shore. The reclaimed land was a flat grey expanse, fenced, with cement mixers and pile-drivers at intervals. The cranes were the colour of new metal. The signs along the fence said ZUID-AFRIKAANSE SPOORWEË EN HAWENS in white letters on dark green and below them the same in English. Beyond the fence the harbour proper held its freighters, two grey navy vessels in for refit, the masts of the fishing fleet against the further breakwater.

The light off the concrete and the water was hard, the kind of light that flattened detail.

Ruth walked along the inland side of the fence. The wind was up. The wind off the harbour at this hour smelled of diesel and tar and the cold green of the Atlantic.

A figure was on a ledge of the fence twenty yards along, bent over a tripod. The tripod had the heavy three-legged set of a press camera and the figure was working with the back of the camera, the cloth over her head and shoulders.

Ruth stopped walking.

The cloth came off. Hannah straightened and stood for a moment with her hand on the camera, looking at the cranes. She had a leather satchel slung across her body and her hair was pulled back under a flat cap that was, on her, neither masculine nor not. Without the lamplight of the Brownlow Road flat she looked younger, perhaps thirty, the line of her face cleaner against the harbour light.

She turned her head before Ruth could decide whether to walk on.

Hannah looked at her for a moment without expression. Then she lifted her hand from the camera in a brief acknowledgment.

Ruth crossed the few yards of paving to the fence.

'I am sorry to interrupt.'

'You are not interrupting. I am between exposures.' Hannah's voice was the same as it had been on Thursday: low, laconic, working. She did not seem startled by Ruth's appearance and did not arrange her face for it. 'You are walking.'

'I am.'

Hannah looked back at the cranes for a moment. 'They want a series. The Department wants twenty plates of the new harbour works for a publication next year. They will be useful for the tourist board and for the parliamentary report. The light is impossible. There is too much sky and too much concrete and not enough between them.' She moved an adjustment on the front of the camera. 'I have been here since seven.'

'You were going to do this on Thursday.'

'I was. I came yesterday and the light was wrong and I came back.'

Ruth stood at the fence with the satchel still against her hip. The brown Tibullus was inside it, beside the green Cavafy she had not, in fact, put back upstairs in the Jagger after all but had carried out of the reading room without thinking and would have to take back this afternoon. The strap of the satchel cut into her shoulder.

'May I watch.'

Hannah looked at her again, briefly and entirely. 'You may. Do not stand in front of the camera.'

Ruth stepped back two paces and stood against the fence. The metal of the fence was cold through the wool of the dress.

Hannah went under the cloth again.

Ruth watched her work. The work was slow. There was a long composition under the cloth. Then Hannah came out and made an adjustment to a dial on the side of the camera and put a wooden plate-holder into the back of it and removed the front cover and moved a cable and held very still and pressed the cable and then stood back. She did this with the calm of a person whose hands had done it perhaps a thousand times. She replaced the cover, turned the plate, repeated the operation. Two exposures.

Then she took the cloth off entirely and lit a cigarette.

'You walked here from the university,' she said.

'Yes.'

'On a Friday morning.'

'Yes.'

Hannah looked at her with the directness that had been in the room on Thursday. 'I am not going to ask you why.'

'Thank you.'

'But I am going to say that the harbour is a public place, in a way the flat is not, and a girl walking along the fence is also a person someone might notice. Do you understand me.'

Ruth said that she did.

Hannah looked at her for a moment longer and then turned back to the camera. 'There is a tea room on St George's Mall that does an acceptable cup. They have a back table that is quiet. I am going to be done here in twenty minutes. If you wanted to walk back into town in twenty minutes I would not mind the company.'

It belonged to none of the registers Ruth had a name for. Not the warmth of Mrs Lawrence's welcome. Not Clara's watchful attention. Not Sarah's measuring glance over the water carafe in the Cohens' dining room. The working laconic kindness of a woman who had decided, for reasons of her own, to extend a piece of practical care.

'I would like that.'

'Good. Stand somewhere out of the wind. There is a wall around the corner.'

Ruth went around the corner and stood against the wall in the lee of the wind. The wall was warm on the side that faced the morning sun. She put her hands flat against the brick and felt the warmth go into her palms.

She thought of Miss Pryce's *I noticed that you came in late on Thursday evening*. She thought of the conversation as a piece of information that had now been delivered. The information was that she was being read. The reading had been going on for some time. She would not be able to undo the period during which she had assumed she was not being read.

She thought of *Eleanor Stratford-Reid is convening it. I should be pleased to see you there.* She thought of the verb *convene*, which Miss Pryce had used precisely. A girl convened a Sunday tea the way an admiral convened a council. The verb was the architecture.

She thought of Arbuthnot's *I should not, were I you, allow myself to be disposed of by a textual question.*

She stood against the warm wall with her hands flat on the brick and let the three things sit in her without arranging them.

When Hannah came round the corner, the camera bag over her shoulder and the tripod folded under her arm, she took the bag without a word from Ruth's offered hand and gave Ruth the tripod.

'It is not heavy,' Hannah said. 'But two are easier than one.'

They walked back into the city along Riebeek Street. The streets were full now. The trams were running every five minutes. A delivery cart full of crates of fruit went past, the man on the box smoking and not looking. Two women with shopping baskets stepped off the pavement to pass a bench and stepped back on, the older one murmuring something to the younger that ended in a brief shared laugh.

Ruth carried the tripod.

At St George's Mall the tea room was a long narrow place with marble-topped tables and the smell of coffee being ground. The back table was empty. Hannah set the tripod against the wall and the camera bag onto the chair beside her and sat down and did not say anything until the waitress had come and they had ordered, two coffees and a plate of biscuits.

'Are you all right,' Hannah said then.

'Yes.'

'Tell me anyway.'

Ruth held her hands around the cup the waitress brought before she answered. 'The warden spoke to me this morning in the corridor.'

'The warden of the residence.'

'Miss Pryce.'

Hannah took a biscuit and did not eat it. She set it on the saucer beside her cup. 'Tell me what she said.'

Ruth told her. She told her precisely. She did not interpret. She gave the words in the order Miss Pryce had given them. The information about the Sunday tea. The verb *convene*. The reference to Eleanor Stratford-Reid. The two phrases she had used about the residence doing well by girls who allowed it to. The line *I do not, as you know, keep a record. But I do notice.*

Hannah listened with her fingers around the cup. She did not write any of it down. She looked at the table once in the middle of Ruth's account and then back at Ruth's face.

'Do you understand what she was telling you.'

'I understand that she was telling me she had noticed.'

'Go further than that.'

Ruth thought for a moment. 'She was telling me she had a record without keeping one. She was telling me Sunday tea is the correction. She was telling me the consequence of not attending Sunday tea would not be Sunday tea.'

Hannah looked at her steadily. 'You read it correctly.'

'What is the consequence.'

Hannah stirred her coffee. The teaspoon made the repetitive sound a teaspoon makes against a cup. 'The Warden writes a conduct endorsement at the end of every term for every girl on a scholarship. The endorsement goes to the scholarship board. The board does not read it for content. It reads it for tone. A neutral endorsement is a green light. A warm endorsement is a recommendation for renewal with prejudice in the candidate's favour. A cool endorsement is an instruction. The board does not investigate. It acts on the warden's word. This has been the system at the women's residences in this country since before the war.'

Ruth held the cup against her palm.

'How do you know this.'

'I had a friend at Stellenbosch in nineteen-forty. She was a scholarship girl. She did not attend the Sunday teas.'

'And.'

'And she was withdrawn from the scholarship at the end of her second year, with a citation that referred to social conduct. The citation did not mention the teas. The citation did not need to.' Hannah took the biscuit she had set on the saucer and broke it in half and ate one half. 'She is a sister at Stellenbosch hospital now and is a perfectly happy person. But she did not finish her degree.'

Ruth did not say anything.

'I am not telling you this to frighten you.'

'Yes you are.'

Hannah looked up. The directness was not, this time, working laconic kindness. It was something more attentive.

'Yes,' she said, 'I suppose I am. But I am also telling you because Judith brought you to the flat on Thursday and I would prefer not to read about a clever girl from the north having her scholarship withdrawn for reasons no one will name.' She took the second half of the biscuit. 'You should attend the Sunday tea. You should attend all of them this term. You should stand near Eleanor Stratford-Reid and you should be observed standing near her. You should be precise about your collar and your shoes and your vowels. You should not, in this term, miss a chapel.'

'I do not attend chapel.'

'Begin attending. Sit in the third row from the back and read your Latin during the homily. The third row is where the scholarship girls who are managing the residence sit. The warden knows this.' Hannah looked at her. 'I am sorry, Ruth. I would like to tell you something different.'

Ruth did not say anything for a moment.

'Hannah.'

'Yes.'

'Why have you told me this.'

Hannah was quiet for a moment. She turned the cup once on the saucer.

'I told you on Thursday I do not photograph people any more.'

'Yes.'

'I photographed people for a publication in Johannesburg between forty-three and forty-six. I photographed mineworkers, mainly. Black mineworkers on the Reef. Strikers in forty-six. The photographs got the publication closed. They did not, however, get me into trouble of any consequence, because I am the sort of person I am. The photographs got two of the men in them killed. Not because of my photographs. They would have died anyway. But the photographs were among the things that were used in the trial. I did not know they would be used. I should have known. I did know, and I let myself not know, which is the same thing.' She set the cup down. 'I have made a decision since to be careful with people I become aware of. I am aware of you.'

Ruth held very still.

'Thank you,' she said.

'You do not need to thank me. I am asking you to be careful.'

'I will be careful.'

'Good.'

They sat for a while without speaking. The coffee was cooling. The biscuits had not been touched after Hannah's first one. Outside the tea-room window a tram ground past on its way down to the Foreshore and a man on a bicycle wove between two cars and disappeared up Adderley.

'There is one further thing,' Hannah said eventually. 'About the flat.'

'Yes.'

'You should not come for some weeks.'

Ruth looked at her.

'It is not because Clara would not want you to come. She would. But the warden has noticed your Thursday evenings and a Thursday evening in Brownlow Road is something the warden may, in time, attach a name to. Not now. In time. Clara will not mind. Clara would mind more if you came and lost your scholarship for it. The Tibullus may stay with you. Read it. Write to her about it through me, if you want. I see her on Tuesdays.'

She said this without inflection, without softening, as a piece of practical information.

Ruth nodded once. The nod did not feel like agreement, exactly. It was the gesture a person makes when they are receiving a thing that is being given to them and have understood that the gift is also the cost.

'I understand.'

'Good.' Hannah picked up the bill the waitress had left. 'My coffee. You may pay for the biscuits if you want a record of independence. Otherwise I will pay for them and you will owe me biscuits at some indeterminate future point.'

'Biscuits.'

'Biscuits. Now go back to your library. You should be seen in the library this morning. The librarians know who comes in and who does not. They are not, for our purposes, on our side or against us. They are inventory. But inventory matters.'

Ruth left the tea room ahead of Hannah and walked back up Adderley alone, the tripod-less satchel light against her side. The brown Tibullus and the green Cavafy were inside it. The two books knocked against each other faintly with each step.

She went back to the Jagger. She returned the green Cavafy to the upper gallery, replacing it precisely where it had been. The slot held the shape of the absence for the few seconds in which she set the book back, and then the absence was filled and the gallery was as it had been.

She went down to her carrel. She opened the brown Tibullus. She opened the notebook. She read the passage she had translated that morning. She read the two lines she had written underneath. She read them again. Then she turned to the next blank page of the notebook and began, in the same neat hand, a new translation of an entirely different elegy, the first elegy of the first book, which she had chosen for no reason except that it had nothing to do with Marathus.

She worked through the morning. The Latin held her. The work held her. The work was the only thing in the room that did not depend on what other people had decided about her.

At lectures that afternoon she sat in her usual place. Arbuthnot did not look at her. He read Sophocles, the passage on Antigone refusing to leave Polyneices unburied. He read it in Greek and in English and then closed the book and said that the question for the afternoon was not whether Antigone was right but whether the polis was capable, in any structure that had ever existed, of accommodating a being who was prepared to die for the unburied. He asked the question without asking it of anyone in particular. He took notes from the row of doctoral students at the back. Ruth wrote nothing in her notebook. She listened.

She walked back up to the residence in the late afternoon along the path through the pines, the satchel against her side. The wind had not come up. The mountain was clear in the early-evening light, the rock face holding the last gold of the sun, the lower slopes already in shadow.

She passed Miss Pryce on the residence drive. Miss Pryce was speaking to one of the under-wardens, the clipboard against her chest. Miss Pryce looked up as Ruth passed, and Ruth said, 'Good evening, Warden,' and Miss Pryce said, 'Good evening, Miss MacLeod,' and they both moved on.

In Room 412 Ruth took off her coat and hung it on the hook on the door and sat on the edge of the bed.

She took the brown Tibullus out of the satchel and put it on the desk. She sat for a moment with her hands in her lap.

She wrote a letter then, on a single sheet of the residence's writing paper, headed *General Botha Hall, University of Cape Town,* in her neat hand. She did not address it. She wrote three lines.

*The window opens. The light may, as you said, be a new tyranny. I am attending Sunday tea this week.*

She did not sign it.

She folded it once and slid it into the brown Tibullus at the page of the second elegy of the third book and closed the cover.

She sat for a while looking at the volume on the desk. The light along the western wall had turned the colour of clean brass. The mountain was beginning to lose its definition above the residence roofline. The pines along the drive were moving in the slow, dry agreement of evening.

She did not light the lamp.

She picked up the green silk square from the desk, the one Judith had lent her on a Friday evening at the Cohens' three weeks before and that Ruth had not yet returned, and folded it once on her palm, and then folded it again, and held it in her lap.

She sat in the gathering dark with the square in her hand and did not move.

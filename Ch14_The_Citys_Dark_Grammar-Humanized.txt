# Chapter 14: The City's Dark Grammar

The note came through the porter's pigeonhole on Tuesday morning. It was a single sheet of buff card folded once, addressed in a hand Ruth recognised from the back of one envelope she had received at Brownlow Road in October.

*Tuesday afternoon. Three o'clock. The corner of Caledon Square nearest the bus rank. H.*

There was nothing else.

Ruth read the note twice in the porter's lobby and put it into the inner pocket of her tweed jacket and went up to her tutorial. She translated the passage of Cicero set for the morning. She wrote four lines of it in pencil and four lines in ink. She handed in the fair copy at half past eleven and she walked back across the upper field to the residence and ate at the long table with twenty other girls and Miss Pryce nodded to her from the head of the room. She went up to Room 412 and changed her stockings and brushed her shoes and put on the heavy grey jersey under her tweed jacket because the wind in town was from the south-east today and the south-east wind was cold even at three o'clock in April.

She went down to the residence drive at twenty past two. She walked to the tram. The tram was empty at that hour. She rode it to the lower stop on Plein Street and walked the four blocks to Caledon Square and stood at the corner Hannah had named, near the bus rank, with the leather satchel on her left shoulder and her hands in her coat pockets.

Hannah was already there. She was leaning against the iron railing of the bus rank in the brown coat and the flat grey cap, the camera bag over her left shoulder, a packet of cigarettes in her right hand. She did not wave. She did not move from the railing as Ruth approached. She nodded once.

'You came.'

'I came.'

'Walk with me.'

Hannah pushed off from the railing and turned and began walking. She walked at the unhurried angle of a woman who had business and was not concealing it. Ruth fell in beside her. They walked past the bus rank and across the lower end of Caledon Square and up the rise toward Buitenkant Street, and Hannah did not speak until they had crossed Buitenkant.

'Sunday tea,' she said.

'I went.'

'Eleanor.'

'Yes.'

'And what did Eleanor want.'

Ruth did not answer at once. She watched the kerb. The kerb at the corner of Buitenkant and Roeland was fitted with an enamel sign that read CARS PARK ONE WAY in a colour that had once been cream. The enamel had been chipped at the lower left.

'A walking party. The mountain. Saturday.'

'You went.'

'Yes.'

Hannah glanced at her then. The glance read her face for two seconds, no longer, and then went back to the pavement.

'Up?'

'To the upper plateau. We came down by the cableway.'

'Did you fall.'

'No.'

'But you froze.'

Ruth did not answer. They had crossed Roeland and were walking up the slow rise toward the lower end of Tennant Street and the eastern edge of the District. The southerly was at their backs and the wind moved the dust along the kerb in short pale spirals.

'I have a fear of heights,' Ruth said. 'I did not know how bad it was.'

'They saw it.'

'Yes.'

'Constance?'

'She walked back across the plateau and walked in front of me.'

'Vivienne?'

'Vivienne said something kind once.'

'Don't bank on it.'

'I have not.'

'Good.'

They walked. The houses along Tennant Street were smaller now and the shopfronts began to repeat in two languages. There was a butcher with hand-painted Hebrew on the upper window and a fruit-and-vegetable seller with Arabic on the awning and a tobacco kiosk with English and Afrikaans painted in alternating columns down the doorpost. The men outside the tobacco kiosk wore caps. The women carrying parcels along the pavement wore long pale skirts and headscarves of three different fabrics and the children running between them had not been told about the wind.

'I am taking you to a tailor,' Hannah said. 'Mr Adams. He has done a jacket for me last winter. He has a print I made of a doorway on Wale Street that he asked me to bring.'

'A working visit.'

'A working visit.' Hannah did not look at Ruth as she spoke. 'But I want you to come in with me. He likes a quiet visitor and he is having a difficult week. There are reasons I will tell you afterward. While we are inside, you will sit in the chair by the window and not speak unless he speaks to you. If anyone else comes into the shop while we are there, you will go on sitting in the chair. You will not speak to whoever it is. Whatever I say, you will not contradict.'

'Why.'

'Because Mr Adams is on a verification list and the verification clerks are working Tennant Street this fortnight, and your face is a clean white face that does not belong here, and the clerks are going to read it.'

Ruth slowed half a step and made herself not slow.

'Will they come today.'

'They might. They might not.'

'Hannah. Why are you taking me with you.'

Hannah did not answer at once. They had turned off Tennant into a narrower street whose name Ruth missed because she was watching the upper line of the buildings, and the upper line of the buildings had washing strung across it on lines from window to window, and the washing in the southerly was beating against itself like a hundred low flags.

'Because you have been to the Cohens' dining room,' Hannah said, 'and you have been to the upper plateau on Saturday with Eleanor Stratford-Reid, and you have been to a salon in Brownlow Road in October, and I want you to see the third room in the city that you are walking through every day without seeing it. The room you are about to enter is the room that pays for the other two.'

'I have been to District Six before. With Judith. In February.'

'Yes. With Judith. On a Saturday afternoon. In the music. I know.'

'How.'

'Clara mentioned it.'

The street narrowed further. They turned again, into a flagged side-lane with a single washing line low across it and a shrine to the Virgin let into the wall of the corner house, and at the end of the lane was a doorway with a hand-painted board above it, in faded gilt, that read *J. ADAMS, TAILOR, ALTERATIONS*. Below the gilt board was a smaller card, recently affixed, that read *Family Tailoring. Cape Suits. Repairs.* in three lines of black ink.

Hannah pushed the door. A bell rang.

The shop was a single long room. The front section, lit by the window onto the lane, contained a low table for laying out fabric, two wooden chairs, and a glass-fronted display case in which three suit jackets were arranged on padded hangers. The rear section, separated by a curtain of striped cotton, was the workroom. Through the half-open curtain Ruth could see the foot of a treadle sewing machine and the lower edge of a cutting table.

Mr Adams came through the curtain wiping his hands on a strip of muslin. He was a man of perhaps fifty, slight, in a grey waistcoat over a white shirt with the cuffs rolled. His face was lined around the mouth in the manner of a man who had been polite to other people's children for a very long time. He saw Hannah and his face moved in the way faces move at the entry of a person they had been waiting for.

'Miss Hannah.'

'Mr Adams.'

'You bring it.'

'I bring it.'

He did not come forward. He stayed at the curtain. There was a stillness in the shop that Ruth had not registered until that second. The stillness was the stillness of a man who was listening for the bell again.

'This is Ruth,' Hannah said, in the same even voice. 'A friend who walked over with me.'

Mr Adams nodded to Ruth. 'Miss.'

'Mr Adams.'

'Sit by the window.'

Ruth sat in the chair by the window. The chair faced the lane. From the chair she could see the upper end of the lane and the slice of Tennant Street beyond it. She put the satchel against her ankle. She set her hands on her knees.

Hannah came forward to the low table and opened her camera bag and took out a flat manila envelope. She undid the brass clasp and slid out a single photographic print, sepia, perhaps eight inches by ten. She laid it on the low table.

Mr Adams crossed the room. He stood at the table and looked at the print. He did not pick it up.

'My door,' he said.

'Your door.'

'Three weeks ago.'

'Three weeks.'

'You have made it look very dignified.'

'It is very dignified.'

'It was not, that day.' Mr Adams said this without bitterness. He said it as a man might note the exact time on a clock. 'But you have made it so.'

'I would like you to keep the print whatever happens. If the address moves, I can make another, of whatever door you stand in next.'

'Yes.'

He picked up the print then and held it at arm's length and looked at it and laid it back on the table.

'Miriam will want to see it. She is upstairs with the boy. He had his medicine half an hour ago.'

'I will not stay long.'

'No, no. Stay long enough for Miriam.' He went to the foot of a flight of dark stairs at the rear of the shop and called up softly. *Miriam. Miss Hannah is here. With the print.* There was a pause and the interior sound of a chair being moved on a wooden floor and then a woman's voice answering in a language Ruth did not recognise at the level of meaning but recognised at the level of shape. Mr Adams turned back. 'A minute.'

'A minute is kind.'

He came back to the table and stood in front of the print again, hands folded behind his back. He did not look at Ruth. The not-looking was a courtesy. She was a stranger he had agreed to admit at Hannah's word, and the courtesy was that he would not require her to perform anything for him while she sat by his window.

The bell on the shop door rang.

Mr Adams's hands separated behind his back and refolded.

The two men who came in wore jackets but not coats. The first was perhaps thirty, stocky, with a clipboard and a fountain pen in his upper jacket pocket. The second was a constable in the dark blue uniform of the South African Police, with the cap held under his arm and the lanyard of his whistle visible across his chest. They were not moving as men who had hurried.

'Mr Joseph Adams.'

'I am Mr Adams.'

'I am Mr Pretorius from the City Council. I am visiting the addresses on the verification list this week. This is Constable du Toit. We will not take long.'

'Yes.'

'Your wife is at home today.'

'My wife is upstairs.'

'And the children.'

'My son is upstairs. My daughter is at her work.'

'Where does your daughter work.'

'At the Bonnie Tea Room on Castle Street.'

'Castle Street is not in the District.'

'No, sir.'

'Then she is registered at this address but she is at present at the tea room.'

'Yes, sir.'

Mr Pretorius opened the clipboard. The clipboard had a single sheet of foolscap on it, divided into columns. He held a fountain pen in his right hand. He did not write.

'And your son's name.'

'Daniel Adams.'

'Your son is registered at Buitenkant Street.'

'Yes, sir.'

'But you have just told me he is upstairs.'

Mr Adams paused. The pause was very brief. The pause had inside it the administrative reckoning that Ruth's body had done on the upper plateau of the mountain, the reckoning of a man finding the precise place he could put his foot.

'My son is unwell. He has come to stay with us this week so that his mother can manage his medicine.'

'I see.' Mr Pretorius made a small note on the foolscap. 'He has not changed his registered address.'

'He is staying here for a fortnight, sir.'

'You understand, Mr Adams, that the verification is concerned with where a person is *currently resident*, not where they have a card.'

'Yes, sir.'

'May we come up.'

'My wife.'

'We will be brief.'

There was a pause then in which the room moved without moving. Hannah, at the low table, had not turned. Her right hand was on the manila envelope and her left hand was on the lid of the camera bag. She was not photographing. The camera was inside the bag and the bag was closed.

Ruth, in the chair by the window, did not move.

Mr Pretorius looked at her.

He looked at her for not more than two seconds. He took in the wool dress under the jersey, the tweed jacket, the school shoes, the pale face, the satchel against the ankle. He took in the not-belonging of her in the room. He did not address her. He looked at Hannah.

'Madam.'

'Mr Pretorius.'

'You are.'

'Hannah Roth. I am the photographer. I am here to deliver a print Mr Adams commissioned at the end of last month. I have a contract with the Department of Railways and Harbours. My credentials are in the bag if you require them.'

'And the young lady.'

'My assistant.'

There was no pause in Hannah's voice. The word *assistant* came out of her in the unhurried way she had walked across Caledon Square. Ruth understood with the precision the upper plateau had taught her that she was not to react to the word.

'Your assistant.'

'Yes.'

'Her name.'

'Miss MacLeod.'

'Miss MacLeod.' Mr Pretorius wrote nothing. He was a man who used his pen sparingly. 'And you are working for the Department of Railways and Harbours in this lane on Tennant Street.'

'No, sir. I am delivering a print to Mr Adams. We were on our way back from the Foreshore.'

'You take photographs of doorways.'

'I take photographs of buildings the Department wishes to record. Sometimes I do private commissions on the same afternoon.'

'I see.' Mr Pretorius closed the clipboard. He kept it in his hand. 'We will go up, Mr Adams.'

'Yes, sir.'

The constable went first. He had not spoken. He went through the rear of the shop and up the dark stairs with the soft tread of a man who had been told all day to be a soft tread. Mr Pretorius followed him. Mr Adams, after a moment, followed Mr Pretorius. The shop was empty of them in five seconds.

Hannah did not turn. She did not speak. She stood at the low table with her hand on the manila envelope and the other hand on the lid of the camera bag, and she counted, Ruth could see, in the angle of her shoulder, the count of the seconds between the closing of one door at the top of the stairs and the opening of another. She counted to, perhaps, sixty.

Then she said, very quietly, without turning, 'Stay sitting.'

'Yes.'

'When they come down, do not look at them. Look at the print.'

'Yes.'

'Whatever happens to the boy, do not stand up.'

'Yes.'

Above them, on the upper floor, there was the sound of a man's voice asking a question in a flat administrative tone, and the higher sound of a woman answering, and the lower private sound of a third voice that Ruth understood after a moment was the voice of a young man who was sick.

The exchange went on for, perhaps, four minutes.

Then they came down. The constable first, then Mr Pretorius, then Mr Adams, and behind Mr Adams, walking with effort, holding the rail of the stair, a young man of perhaps nineteen in a thin shirt and trousers, with the hectic colour high under his eyes that Ruth had last seen at the convent in the year she nursed Anna through the winter pneumonia. He was wearing a heavy brown overcoat that did not belong to him.

'Mr Adams,' Mr Pretorius said, on the threshold of the front of the shop. 'Your son is to come with us to the Caledon Square office for the verification of his current address against his registered address. It will not take long. He will be home this evening.'

'He is not well, sir.'

'He will be made comfortable. He will sit. We will not detain him beyond what the verification requires.'

'Sir, I would request that I come with him.'

'You may walk down with us. You may not enter the office. He will be brought out to you at the door. You may then bring him home.'

'Sir.'

'Mr Adams, this is the procedure. It is not a matter on which we have leeway.'

'Yes, sir.'

The constable opened the door of the shop. The bell rang. Mr Pretorius stepped out first. The young man with the hectic colour stepped out behind him, and the constable closed in behind the young man, and Mr Adams came last, and the bell rang again as the door closed, and the lane outside took the four of them in the pale afternoon light and they went up toward Tennant Street with the exact unhurried pace at which Hannah and Ruth had come down.

Hannah did not turn until the bell had finished swinging.

She turned to Ruth.

'Stay there.'

She went to the foot of the stair and called up softly. *Miriam. They are gone. May I come up.* The woman's voice answered. Hannah crossed the shop and went up the stairs. Ruth heard Hannah's voice on the upper landing. She heard the woman's voice answer in the same language Ruth had not at first recognised, and she understood it now to be Yiddish, or a Cape Yiddish with English in it, or some plait of Yiddish and Kaaps the precise grammar of which Ruth would not learn that afternoon. She heard the two women not weep and not raise their voices. Hannah was up there for, perhaps, six minutes.

Ruth sat in the chair by the window. The lane outside was empty. The slice of Tennant Street at the upper end was empty for one minute and then a woman walked across it with a string bag and then it was empty again. The print of the door was on the low table. From where Ruth sat she could see the print at an angle. The print showed the doorway of the shop she was sitting inside, taken in the late morning, the gilt board catching the light, and Mr Adams standing in the open doorway in his grey waistcoat with his hands folded in front of him, looking out, not at the camera. The print had been made by a person who had wanted to give a man back his door before the door was taken from him.

Hannah came down. Her face had not changed. She picked up the camera bag.

'We will go.'

'Yes.'

'The print stays here.'

'Yes.'

She crossed the shop and laid one hand on Ruth's shoulder for not more than a second, in passing, and went out into the lane. Ruth stood up. She picked up the satchel. She followed Hannah.

In the lane the light had begun to go thin in the southerly. They walked back the way they had come, up the lane, into Tennant Street. Hannah did not turn down toward Caledon Square. She turned up, away from the District, toward the higher streets along the ridge.

'Where are we going.'

'A tea room I know on the upper end of Roeland. We will sit. I will tell you what just happened.'

'Yes.'

They walked.

The tea room on Roeland was a long narrow room above a bookbinder's, reached by a wooden stair that smelled of old paper. There were six tables. Three were occupied. The waitress was a coloured woman in her fifties who knew Hannah by face, and Hannah took the table in the corner that did not face the door, and the waitress brought a pot of black tea without being asked. It was four o'clock. The light through the upper window had the same brass quality the residence wall had at this hour.

Hannah poured the tea.

'Joseph Adams is on the verification list for the third time this year,' she said. 'The verification list is the pre-removal census. The Group Areas zoning for District Six has not been gazetted yet. The verification is the preparation. They are mapping who is where, who is registered where, who is sleeping where, which family has a son with a card at one address and a body at another, which household register does not match the floor plan they have on file at Caledon Square. They are making a map. The map will be used.'

'When.'

'A year. Two. Five. They have not announced. The announcement will not be a surprise to anyone who has been keeping the verification card.'

'Daniel.'

'Daniel will be home this evening. They will make him sign that he is currently resident at Buitenkant Street and not at Tennant Street. He will sign because he has tuberculosis and they will be kind to him at the Square in the way men of that kind are kind. They will give him a glass of water and a chair. He will sign and he will come home and Joseph and Miriam will move him back to Buitenkant Street tonight, where he does not have a person to give him his medicine in the morning, and Miriam will go up to him every day on the bus until he is well enough to come back, by which time the next verification visit will register him as resident at Buitenkant Street and the household register at Tennant Street will read three persons instead of four.'

'And if Joseph keeps him at Tennant Street.'

'Then on the next verification visit there will be a question about why the boy is at Tennant Street and not at Buitenkant Street, and the question will be put to Joseph, and Joseph will say that the boy is unwell, and the clerk will write down that the boy is unwell, and then the next time, the clerk will ask whether the boy is still unwell, and the time after that the question will be put to Daniel directly, and Daniel will not have an answer that the clerk can write on the form, and Daniel's card will be cancelled.'

'Cancelled.'

'A man without a card cannot work.'

Ruth set her cup down. The cup was full. She had not drunk from it.

'Hannah.'

'Yes.'

'You said *the room that pays for the other two*.'

'I said it.'

'I had been there before with Judith.'

'I know.'

'And I had heard about it from Sarah.'

'Yes.'

'Sarah lives.'

'Three streets up from the lane we sat in. Above a butcher who is also on the list.'

'David Cohen.'

'Yes.'

'He knows.'

'He knows. He has the analysis. He cannot make himself act on it. You have heard him reach for the wine glass at his father's table.'

'Yes.'

'It is not a moral failure on David's part that interests me. It is a structural fact. The Cohens cannot act because they are a Jewish family in Cape Town in 1949 and the dining room they sit in is a dining room in a building whose lease, whose rent, whose freedom from interference, all depend on a series of administrative continuities at the City Council that any of them could disturb by signing the wrong petition. Sarah cooks in the kitchen of a family that has the analysis and does not act because the family is one signature away from being on a verification list of its own.'

'No.'

'Yes.'

'Hannah.'

'Yes.'

'What does Sarah do.'

'Sarah does not have the choice you have. Sarah cooks. She raises a younger sister in Tennant Street who does not have tuberculosis. She is alive to her community and she is alive to the moral cost of cooking in the Cohens' kitchen and she does not come apart because she has had the practice that a woman of fifty-seven in Cape Town has had. She tells the truth in the kitchen and not in the dining room because in the dining room there is no use for it. She is not a saint. She is also not a victim. She is what most of the people in this city who hold the city up against the falling of it are. She is functioning.'

'I understand.'

'No. You almost understand. You will understand by Christmas.'

'Why do you bring me here.'

Hannah took a sip of her tea. She set the cup down.

'Because you have a habit, Ruth, that I noticed at Brownlow Road in October and I noticed again this afternoon when Mr Pretorius looked at you. Under pressure you become exact. You go very still and you become exact about a thing that is not the pressure itself. The wear on a kerb. A buckle on a strap. The weight of a print on a table. It is a useful habit. It is also a habit that has no meaning if you only ever use it on yourself.'

'I do not understand what you are asking me to do.'

'I am not asking you to do anything. I am asking you to look. The habit looks. The exactness is an instrument. If you keep it for the saucer in your hand at Sunday tea and the path under your foot on the upper plateau, you will go through the next four years a fine classicist and a closed person. If you turn the same exactness on Mr Adams's doorway, on Mr Pretorius's clipboard, on Daniel's overcoat that did not belong to him, then in five years you will be a person of some use in this country. That is the only thing I am asking.'

'I am eighteen.'

'You are nineteen now, surely. What month were you born.'

'November.'

'Eighteen. Then by Christmas you are nineteen. There is time.'

Ruth picked up the cup. The tea was cooling. She drank a sip.

'The overcoat.'

'Yes.'

'It was Joseph's.'

'It was Joseph's. He would have put it on Daniel before the men came down the stairs. He did not want the constable to take Daniel out of the house in a thin shirt. The overcoat was the only piece of dignity the men did not have authority to remove.'

'I noticed it.'

'I saw you notice it.'

'When.'

'When they were on the threshold and Mr Pretorius was making his announcement. You did not look up. You looked at the lower edge of the coat. The exactness, again. It is your instrument. Use it.'

The waitress came. Hannah paid. They went down the wooden stairs and out into Roeland Street and the wind had dropped along the upper ridge and the sun was coming through low between the buildings.

'Tram?'

'Tram.'

'Hannah.'

'Yes.'

'When do I see you again.'

'Tuesday in two weeks. The same corner.'

'And in between.'

'In between you sit at the Sunday tea and you nod to Miss Pryce and you write your Cicero and you do not come down to Tennant Street alone. You are not yet of any use down here on your own. You would only put Joseph at risk.'

'Yes.'

Hannah looked at her. The look was the look she had given Ruth in the cafe on St George's Mall in October, and it was not a look that asked anything of Ruth, and it was not a look that promised anything, and Ruth understood for the second time in two months that the look was what Hannah had instead of what other people had as warmth, and that it was, in its own way, warmth.

'Go,' Hannah said.

'Yes.'

The tram up to the residence was full at five o'clock with secretaries from the lower offices and two men in suits who had been at a meeting on the Foreshore and a girl in a school blazer who was reading a French textbook under the strap. Ruth stood at the rear platform with the satchel against her hip and she did not look at the city as it went past. She looked at the metal of the rear platform. The metal of the rear platform was painted dark green and the paint had been worn down to the steel along the inside edge of the rail by the hands of the conductors.

She watched the wear pattern.

She made her hand soft against the rail and felt the wear pattern under the heel of her hand.

In the residence Miss Pryce was in the lower hall at half past five. She was not at the warden's desk. She was standing at the long window beside the desk, with her back to the door, her hands folded behind her at the small of her back, looking out at the residence drive. She turned as Ruth came in.

'Miss MacLeod.'

'Warden.'

'You are returning late.'

'Yes, Warden.'

'You have been in town.'

'Yes.'

'For a purpose.'

'Tea with a friend.'

'Which friend.'

The question came out of Miss Pryce in the same voice in which she announced the linen at the long table. The voice was of a woman who was confirming a small detail on a document she already had open.

Ruth's hand on the strap of the satchel did not move.

'Miss Roth.'

'I do not think I have met Miss Roth.'

'She is a photographer. She works for the Department of Railways and Harbours.'

'How interesting.' Miss Pryce's hands did not unfold from behind her back. 'You met her where.'

'She is a friend of a friend.'

'Yes. That is, of course, how one meets people.'

'Yes, Warden.'

'Dinner is at six, Miss MacLeod.'

'Yes, Warden.'

'Good evening.'

'Good evening.'

Ruth went up the stairs.

In Room 412 she did not sit on the bed. She sat at the desk. She took the brown Tibullus out of the satchel and set it on the desk and opened it to the page of the second elegy of the third book where the folded sheet of writing paper lived. She unfolded the sheet. She read the four lines that were already on it.

*The window opens. The light may, as you said, be a new tyranny. I am attending Sunday tea this week.*

*I went up the mountain on Saturday and could not move at the top.*

She picked up the pen.

She did not write at once. She held the pen above the paper for some seconds.

Then, in the same neat hand, she wrote a fifth line.

*This afternoon I sat in a tailor's shop on Tennant Street and watched a man's son taken away in a coat that did not fit him.*

She read the fifth line.

She did not sign it.

She folded the sheet again along the same fold and slid it back into the Tibullus at the page of the second elegy of the third book and closed the cover.

She picked up the green silk square from the desk where it had lain since Sunday evening and folded it once on her palm and folded it again and laid it across her knee.

The light along the western wall had gone the colour of clean brass, as it had done on Friday and on Sunday and would do on every clear weekday evening of the autumn. The mountain was beginning to lose its definition above the residence roofline. The pines along the drive were moving in the slow dry agreement of evening.

She did not light the lamp.

She sat with the square folded on her knee and her hand on the cover of the Tibullus and she thought of Daniel's overcoat, of the lower hem of it that had been three inches too long for him and that he had not turned up at the wrist because he had not had the time.

She kept her hand where it was.

# Chapter 15: Men After War

The card came in the second post on Thursday. The hand on the envelope was the same authoritative hand that had written the first invitation in March, and the card inside had the same ivory weight, the same faint impression of the floral and dry scent that Ruth had not, the first time, been able to name and that she now placed as the dry sachets women of a certain Cape Town household kept at the back of a drawer of writing paper.

*Mrs Audrey Lawrence at home Saturday afternoon. Half past three. Quiet sherry and a few friends. Bellevue, upper Hof Street.*

Below the printed line, in pencil:

*Ruth dear. A small gathering. Thomas has been a little unwell. It would do him good to see a fresh face. AL.*

Ruth read the pencil twice. The pencil was the chapter. The card was the cover.

She read it a third time and put the card on the desk beside the brown Tibullus and looked at it and went down to dinner.

\*

On Saturday morning the southerly had dropped and the air over the residence was the still warm air of a clear autumn day that was not yet asking the body to brace. Ruth wore the navy wool dress because the navy wool dress did not register strain and she put on the dark grey jersey under the jacket because Mrs Lawrence's drawing room had been cooler in March than the day outside it. She walked to the tram. She rode it to the lower stop. She walked the slow rise up Hof Street through Oranjezicht under the high pale light that came down between the upper houses on the slope, and the houses stepped up in their walled gardens with the assured spacing of a street that had been laid out in 1904 and had not been required to argue for itself since.

The gate at Bellevue was open. The drive was raked. The front door had a bell-pull of polished brass that Ruth did not quite touch before the door opened, because Mrs Lawrence had been at the morning-room window and had seen her come up the drive.

'Ruth dear.'

'Mrs Lawrence.'

'Come in. The others are here. We are in the long room.'

Mrs Audrey Lawrence was in pale grey wool, pearls at the throat, hair set that morning. The house behind her was warm and smelled of beeswax and a mild smoke of cedar. The corridor light caught on the polished walnut of the hall stand and on the silver salver that had nothing on it. The drawing room was at the rear of the house, the long room with the southern light coming in from the garden, and through the half-open door Ruth could hear the sound of a male voice in the lower register of social courtesy and a woman answering it in the upper register of polite agreement.

In the drawing room there were five other guests. Mrs Rawlings was on the sofa, in lavender, with a sherry glass at her right hand and the leather bag Ruth had seen her with in March on her lap. Beside her sat a woman Ruth had not met, perhaps sixty, in a brown tweed suit with a pheasant brooch at the lapel. By the window was a man in his late fifties, in a dark blue blazer with regimental buttons, the two centre buttons done up, the lower one not. He was holding a sherry glass at the angle of a man who had been standing for some time. Beside him was his wife, in fawn, who looked up as Ruth came in and gave her the brief, considered, not-unkind appraisal of a woman who had been in this room before and would be in it again. There was a fifth guest, a younger woman in her thirties, on the further window seat, alone, with a book she had set face down beside her on the cushion. The fifth guest was wearing trousers, which was the most distinctive fact in the room.

'May I introduce Miss MacLeod,' said Mrs Lawrence. 'Ruth is reading classics at the university. A scholarship girl. Her father is from the Letaba.'

The room registered her with a small collective adjustment of cuffs and ankles.

Mrs Rawlings extended a hand. 'Ruth. I was hoping to see you again. The Schumann series begins on the eleventh. You will receive a card.'

'Thank you, Mrs Rawlings.'

'This is Mrs Whitechurch. Eveline. And Major and Mrs Penn-Carteret. And on the window seat is Miss Helen Tindall, who has come down from Johannesburg this week and who is, we are determined, going to be persuaded to play for us before tea.'

'Helen will not play,' said Helen, without lifting her face from her hands. 'Helen has played quite enough in 1949 already.' She lifted her face then and looked at Ruth and her face was striking, a long pale face with very dark eyebrows and the same carriage in the throat that Hannah had at the camera bag. The carriage said: *I have decided not to do something and the decision is firm.* 'How do you do, Miss MacLeod.'

'How do you do.'

'Sit by me. The sofa is taken.'

Ruth went to the window seat and sat at the further end of it.

'Sherry,' said Mrs Lawrence, with the small composing movement she made before going to the sideboard, 'or tea. We have both.'

'Tea, please.'

'Tea. Eveline?'

'Sherry.'

'Major?'

'I have one, thank you.'

The teas and sherries were poured. Mrs Lawrence handed Ruth her cup, and the cup was the cup Ruth remembered from March, white with a thin gold rim and a repeating leaf inside the saucer. The conversation gathered on the topic of the rains.

It went on for forty minutes.

Major Penn-Carteret had a brother on a farm at Greyton. The rains had not yet come at Greyton. Mrs Whitechurch's sister at Caledon had rain on Tuesday but only an inch. Mrs Rawlings's gardener had said that the squills were up early. Helen Tindall did not contribute. She picked up the book she had set face down and turned it once in her hands and set it face down again. Mrs Lawrence kept the room moving with a series of administrative inquiries about jam, recipes, and a christening at St George's Cathedral.

Thomas was not in the room.

Thomas was not mentioned.

The house had the upper-floor stillness of a house in which a person is being not-mentioned.

\*

At a quarter past four Mrs Lawrence rose and went to the door of the drawing room and stood there for a moment with her hand on the architrave, as a woman might stand at the door of a sickroom listening for whether the breath inside it had altered. She said quietly, to no one in the room, 'Excuse me a moment,' and went out.

The conversation in the drawing room continued on the topic of the squills.

She came back four minutes later and said, in the same composed administrative voice, 'Thomas will join us in a moment. He has had a long week.'

Major Penn-Carteret said, 'Of course,' in the precise cadence of a man who had said the same word in the same room about the same matter at least once before.

There was a pause then in the conversation that lasted, perhaps, twenty seconds. The pause had inside it the quiet realignment of every guest in the room. Mrs Rawlings set her sherry down without drinking. Mrs Whitechurch's hand moved to the pheasant at her lapel. Mrs Penn-Carteret looked at her husband and looked away. Helen Tindall on the window seat picked up the book and held it in her lap with both hands, and the holding-with-both-hands was the precise movement that told Ruth that Helen Tindall had been at Bellevue in this register before.

The door of the drawing room opened.

Colonel Thomas Lawrence came in.

He was in a tweed jacket and grey flannels and a blue shirt with the collar unbuttoned. He had not put on a tie. His hair, which Ruth remembered from March as iron-grey and combed flat, was longer at the back of the neck than it should have been and had not been combed flat that morning. He was carrying a sherry glass that he had not been given by his wife. The colour in his face was the colour of a man who had been outside in the wind for some hours and had not yet come back to interior temperature, and the colour in the eyes was the colour of a man who had begun the week with a degree of disorder that a Saturday afternoon at home would not, in the course of the next two hours, fully restore.

He stood in the doorway. He looked at the room.

'Audrey,' he said.

'Thomas. You know Henry. And Eveline. And Mrs Rawlings, of course.'

'Of course.' His voice was the voice Ruth remembered, the courteous instrument of a man trained from boyhood to enter a room well, but the instrument was being played by a hand that was a degree less steady than the hand that had played it in March. 'Mrs Rawlings.'

'Colonel.'

'Eveline.'

'Thomas.'

'Henry. Margaret.'

'Tom.'

He looked at the window seat. He saw Helen first.

'Helen.'

'Thomas.'

'You came.'

'I came.'

'Audrey said you would not.'

'Audrey was incorrect.'

He looked then at Ruth. The looking was not a recognition. The looking was a registration: a young woman in the further corner of his drawing room, in a navy wool dress, whom he had not been told about. He looked at her for two seconds and then looked at his wife.

'This is Miss MacLeod, Thomas. Ruth. From the university. You met her in March.'

'Of course. Miss MacLeod.'

'Colonel.'

'How is the Latin.'

'It is well, sir, thank you.'

'And the Greek.'

'The Greek is harder.'

'It is. It is. It always was.' He nodded as though to himself. He came into the room. He did not go to the chair beside the fire. He went to the further window and stood with the sherry glass at his side and looked out into the garden. The garden was a lawn enclosed by a wall and a row of Mediterranean cypresses, and beyond the cypresses the upper line of the suburb stepped down toward the city.

The conversation in the drawing room did not resume. Mrs Lawrence made a very brief administrative gesture with her right hand to Mrs Rawlings, which Ruth did not at first read, and Mrs Rawlings, after a half-beat, said, 'Audrey, you must tell me how you got the marmalade quite that colour. I have been at it for years.'

'Oh, the marmalade.' Mrs Lawrence's voice came out exactly composed. 'It is a question of the temperature of the second boiling. I will fetch the recipe.'

'No, no, sit. Tell me.'

The marmalade conversation began.

Thomas did not turn from the window. His back, in the tweed jacket, was a back Ruth had not seen until that minute. The set of the shoulders at the upper end was the set of a man holding his own weight up by an act of conscious application. The lower line of the jacket below the shoulders had begun to lose the line a tailor would have put in it. The hand at his side that held the sherry was steady. The other hand was in the pocket of the flannels and the pocket was slightly distended, as though a hand pressed down through cloth.

He stood at the window for, perhaps, four minutes. The marmalade conversation went on.

Then he turned. He came back into the centre of the room. He set the sherry glass on the mantelpiece. He looked at the clock on the mantelpiece, a French clock in gilt and enamel that did not chime, and then at the room.

'I have been at the Shellhole,' he said.

Mrs Lawrence did not answer at once. Then she said, in the same exactly composed voice, 'Yes, dear.'

'They had Smithers down from Pretoria. They had a small lunch. I stayed on.'

'Yes, dear.'

'It went a little late.'

'Yes.'

He did not look at his wife. He looked at the room generally, at no one in it.

'There was a man at the table, Henry, named Carmichael. From the Sixth South African. He had been with us at Sidi Rezegh and again at El Alamein. I had not seen him since 1944. He has lost a leg below the knee since. He carries it well. He laughs at the appropriate moments. He is not, I should say, a man who has made his peace with what was done to us, but he is a man who is alive, which is more than one can say for the rest of the table that lunched at Sidi Rezegh, and we drank to them, as it is our custom.'

'Of course you did,' said Major Penn-Carteret, very quietly.

'It is our custom. We drank to them.' Thomas paused. The pause had inside it the same quality the pause in Joseph Adams's shop had had, the reckoning of a man finding the precise place he could put his foot, and Ruth heard it across the drawing room and took the cup from her saucer and held it in both her hands. 'It is our custom and it is, I think, the only piece of useful work the Order does. The rest of it is ceremony. The drinking to them is the thing. We do it because the country, which sent us, did not afterward remember that it had sent us, and we are required to remember on its behalf.'

'Thomas,' Mrs Lawrence said.

'A moment, Audrey. I am not making a scene. I am making a statement of fact in a drawing room of friends.'

'Of course you are not making a scene, dear.'

'I have come back from the Shellhole and I have come back the long way, by the lower end of Long Street, because I was not yet equal to the upper streets, and I have walked through the city that I came back to in 1945 and I have seen, as I always see when I take the lower route, that the city does not in any way acknowledge that it sent any of us. The city was perfectly composed when we left and the city is perfectly composed now. The composure has always been the city's strongest characteristic. I find myself, on a Saturday afternoon in April 1949, unable to walk into a drawing room of perfect composure without saying so. I beg your pardon.'

There was a silence in the drawing room of perhaps three seconds.

Helen Tindall stood up.

She crossed the room. She crossed it without hurry. She went to Thomas at the mantelpiece and took the sherry glass off the mantel and put it in his hand and closed his hand around it.

'Thomas,' she said, 'that is well said. Drink your sherry.'

'Helen.'

'Drink it.'

He drank it.

She took the glass from him then and set it back on the mantel. She did not look at Audrey Lawrence and she did not look at the rest of the room. She put one hand briefly on Thomas's elbow and the other hand briefly on his upper arm, in the careful firm steady contact of a person who had handled a horse in trouble, and stepped back.

'I find,' she said, into the room generally, 'that I would after all like very much to play. Audrey, may I.'

'Of course, Helen dear.'

Helen went to the piano in the corner of the long room that had not been opened all afternoon. She lifted the lid. She sat down. She played eight bars of something Ruth did not recognise as a piece but recognised as a structure, a slow descending phrase in a minor key that did not resolve at the eighth bar and did not require to. She played it twice. Then she lifted her hands and closed the lid and rose and came back to the window seat and sat down beside Ruth without comment.

Mrs Rawlings said, 'Helen, that was lovely.'

'It was Schumann,' Helen said. 'A piece he did not finish.'

The marmalade conversation resumed at exactly the place it had left off. Thomas stayed at the mantelpiece for, perhaps, another two minutes, and then went back to the window and stood there looking out at the garden until the long afternoon shadow had begun to come up the wall of cypresses and the light in the drawing room had begun to go warmer, and at five o'clock Mrs Lawrence said, *Shall we have fresh tea*, and Mrs Whitechurch said, *Do let us*, and the gathering reorganised itself around the tea tray as though it had been a gathering about marmalade all afternoon.

Thomas drank tea. Thomas spoke to Mrs Rawlings about the squills. Mrs Lawrence sat beside her husband on the sofa for the next half hour with her hand resting loosely on the cushion between them. She was not touching him. The hand was available to be touched if he should choose to take it, which he did not, and the not-touching was the day's most complete administrative achievement.

\*

At a quarter to six the guests began to leave. The Penn-Carterets went first, in their car. Mrs Whitechurch went with Mrs Rawlings, who would drop her on the way to the Schumann committee meeting at six. Helen Tindall picked up her book and her shawl and stood at the door of the drawing room and looked at Audrey Lawrence with a long quiet look that contained a number of things Ruth could not at that distance read except to know that some of them had to do with March 1944 and some of them with the year before, and Audrey Lawrence said, 'Helen, will you stay to supper,' and Helen Tindall said, 'I will not, Audrey, but I am very glad I came,' and they kissed, briefly, on the cheek, and Helen went out.

Ruth was last.

'Stay a moment, Ruth dear,' said Mrs Lawrence. 'The car will run you down. Thomas will see to it.'

'I am very happy on the tram, Mrs Lawrence.'

'Thomas will see to it,' said Mrs Lawrence, who was looking not at Ruth but at her husband, in the tone of a woman who was placing an administrative trust in a hand that needed to be trusted with a small administrative thing. 'Thomas, you will walk Ruth to the car.'

'Of course, Audrey.'

He walked Ruth out of the drawing room and down the corridor and through the hall and out onto the gravel of the front drive. The afternoon had begun to cool. The light along the upper line of the cypresses had gone the colour of clean brass. The car was at the further end of the drive, the engine ticking, the driver in his cap behind the wheel, reading the racing pages.

Thomas did not call to the driver.

He stopped at the lower end of the drive, where the cypresses shaded the gravel and the house was at their backs, and he turned to Ruth.

'Miss MacLeod.'

'Colonel.'

'I should like to walk with you to the lower gate. The car can pick you up at the lower gate. It is two minutes.'

'Yes.'

They walked. The gravel had been raked that morning and a pattern of long even stripes ran the length of the drive. The lower gate was at the bottom of a slight curve, where a hedge of plumbago broke the line of the wall. They walked at the pace of a man who had been on his feet for some hours.

'I beg your pardon, Miss MacLeod.'

'Colonel, you do not have to.'

'I do. Audrey will not require me to. That is why I do.' He paused. 'I came into the room at an inappropriate altitude. You have been very kind to attend my wife on a Saturday afternoon. I did not want you to leave the house thinking that the man in the doorway was, in any way, the man my wife had hoped you would meet.'

'I do not think anything that has not been thought.'

He smiled then, a brief, exact smile that was the first smile Ruth had seen on him all afternoon. 'That is admirably said.'

'It is not admirable. It is true.'

'It is both.'

They came to the lower gate. The car had begun to move down the drive at a slow tick to meet them. Thomas put a hand on the wrought iron of the gate and rested it there.

'Miss MacLeod, I am to give you a piece of advice that you have not asked for and that I have no right to give. May I.'

'Yes, sir.'

'My wife will write to you again next month, and again the month after that, and you will go on receiving these invitations as long as my wife judges that they are the correct social currency for a young woman in your position to be seen to be cultivating. They are not. The correct social currency for a young woman in your position is nothing of the kind. The correct currency is the work. You have the work. You should go on with the work. You should go on coming here when it suits you and not when it does not. You should not feel that it is required of you. It is not required of you. It is required of nobody.'

'I see.'

'I should not say it. It is unhelpful to my wife. I say it because once a year I come back into a drawing room from somewhere I should not have been and I am made to walk a young woman to a car and I have, on those afternoons, a clean piece of the day in which I am useful to someone who is not Audrey, and I take it.'

He took his hand off the gate. The car had stopped. The driver had got out and was opening the rear door.

'Thank you for the afternoon, Colonel.'

'Thank you for the afternoon, Miss MacLeod. The Greek is harder. It is. Keep at it. The Greek was the only thing in 1944 that I read for the discipline of the alphabet, and the alphabet was, in those weeks, the only thing that was not breaking. Go.'

He nodded to the driver. He turned and walked back up the drive without looking at the car.

Ruth got in.

The door closed. The car descended Hof Street at the slow pace of a Saturday afternoon. Ruth sat in the back seat with her hands in her lap and the cup-and-saucer afterimage still on her palms, and she looked through the side window at the houses stepping down the slope, at the walled gardens, at the upper windows of Bellevue receding, and she did not turn to look back when the house was no longer visible from the lower bend.

In the residence Miss Pryce was not in the lower hall. The under-warden was at the desk, signing a delivery slip. She looked up.

'Miss MacLeod. Visitors today.'

'Mrs Lawrence's afternoon, Miss Burrows.'

'In Oranjezicht.'

'Yes.'

'How nice. The book was returned for you this afternoon. It is in the porter's pigeonhole.'

'Thank you.'

Ruth went to the porter's lobby. In the pigeonhole there was a single book, wrapped in brown paper, with a card paper-clipped to the wrapping. The card was unaddressed. It read, in a hand Ruth had seen only once before, on the back of an envelope at the Cohens':

*Ruth. I have had this from a cousin. It is a translation that has not yet got out into the shops. He thought you might like to read it. You may keep it. J.*

She unwrapped the book in the porter's lobby. It was a slim volume in dark cloth, *The Greek Anthology, Book V,* in an English version Ruth had not seen before, privately printed in Edinburgh in 1948, the print run short enough that the paper still had the rough edge of a deckle. She held it in both hands. She wrapped it back into the brown paper and went up the stairs.

\*

In Room 412 she did not sit on the bed. She put the wrapped book on the desk beside the brown Tibullus. She took off the tweed jacket and hung it over the chair. She took off the heavy grey jersey and folded it on the bed.

She unwrapped Judith's book. She set it on the desk to the left of the Tibullus. She did not yet open it.

She took the brown Tibullus and opened it to the page of the second elegy of the third book and unfolded the sheet of writing paper. She read the five lines.

*The window opens. The light may, as you said, be a new tyranny. I am attending Sunday tea this week.*

*I went up the mountain on Saturday and could not move at the top.*

*This afternoon I sat in a tailor's shop on Tennant Street and watched a man's son taken away in a coat that did not fit him.*

She picked up the pen.

She wrote a sixth line.

*This afternoon a man came back into his own drawing room from the Shellhole and asked the room to remember on the country's behalf what the country had not remembered, and a woman in trousers played eight bars of unfinished Schumann.*

She read the sixth line.

She did not sign it.

She folded the sheet again along the same fold and slid it back into the Tibullus at the page of the second elegy of the third book and closed the cover.

She picked up the green silk square from where it had lived on the desk since Sunday before last and folded it once on her palm and folded it again and laid it across her knee.

The light along the western wall had gone the colour of clean brass. The mountain was beginning to lose its definition above the residence roofline. The pines along the drive were moving in their slow dry agreement of evening.

She did not light the lamp.

She sat with the square folded on her knee and her hand on the cover of the Tibullus and she thought of Thomas at the lower gate with his hand on the wrought iron and the brief exact smile and the alphabet that had been the only thing not breaking, and she thought of Lena at the kneading board with her back to the room and the rhythmic thud of flour on wood, and she did not, for the first time, find the two thoughts incompatible.

She kept her hand where it was.

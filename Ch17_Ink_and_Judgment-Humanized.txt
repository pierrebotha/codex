CHAPTER 17

Ink and Judgment


I

The letter came on the Friday with the second post.

Davies handed it to her at the porter's box at five past six, one minute before Audrey's Friday telephone window was due to open, and held the call slip in his other hand for the second of recognition. He looked at the envelope, looked at her, and gave her the envelope first.

'This came at four. From Letaba district. Will I hold the call.'

'Please.'

'I'll tell Mrs Lawrence in two minutes you'll come down. Take the call in the box when you're ready.'

She took the envelope upstairs. She did not look at it on the stairs. In Room 412 she did not light the lamp. She sat at the desk and put the envelope flat on the blotter and looked at it. Lena's hand on the address, the small square upright capitals Lena had used for parcels for as long as Ruth had been able to read. The stamp was the new King's head, a violet-and-grey three-penny that had come into circulation in March. The postmark was Letaba 28 IV 49 and the rural depot stamp she did not recognise, indistinct.

She turned the envelope over. There was no sender on the back. Lena had never written a sender on the back. The flap was sealed with the careful pressure Lena gave a flap, the small ridge of flour on her forearm at the kneading board having been wiped on her apron before she touched the gum.

She opened it with the brass paperknife. The letter inside was on a single half-sheet of the lined writing paper Lena bought from the general dealer at Tzaneen, the paper Lena used for an order to the co-operative. The hand was Lena's. The ink was the violet-blue of the bottle that had stood, when Ruth was a child, on the shelf above the wireless.

*Ruth,*

*William wrote to me on the 22nd. He says he has not heard from you since Easter. He is concerned. I told him I have not heard from you since November, when you left. I did not tell him how you left. He is your brother and he does not need to know how you left.*

*If you intend not to write to him either, you should at least let him know that. He is a young man of seventeen with a year of school to finish. He should not be left wondering at the post.*

*I do not need a letter back. I do not require one. I write on William's account.*

*Lena*

She read it once. She read it again. She put it back into its envelope. She set the envelope on the right side of the blotter, square to the desk edge.

She went down to the porter's box.

'Mrs Lawrence is waiting on the line, Miss MacLeod.'

She lifted the handset.

'Aunt Audrey.'

'Ruth. Forgive the lateness. I was held up.'

'It's not late.'

'Thomas is at Bellevue. He is well. He is reading. He has shaved.' A small considered breath. 'I shall not keep you. I rang chiefly to say thank you again, and to tell you that the doctor came on Wednesday afternoon and was satisfied. Have you had any complications at the residence on account of the day off.'

'No.'

'Good.' A small pause. 'Are you well, Ruth.'

'Yes.'

'You are not well. But you are managing, and that is the sentence we shall agree on if anyone enquires.' Audrey, briefly, almost laughed at her own formula. 'Sunday tea. Bring whatever it is. We shall not speak of it.'

'Eleanor said the same thing on Wednesday.'

'Eleanor and I are alike in that. It is a Stratford trait.'

'Yes.'

'Friday again at six.'

'Yes.'

She rang off. Davies looked up from his ledger.

'Anything I can do, Miss MacLeod.'

'No. Thank you, Mr Davies.'

She went up the stairs at the pace of a person who had been holding her own elbow at the porter's box.


II

She did not draft the reply that evening. She sat at the desk and watched the brass-coloured western light move down the wall to the level of the skirting board and watched the mountain above the residence roofline lose its definition and watched the pines move in their slow dry agreement of evening, and did nothing. The silk square was open on the desk under her left hand where it had been since Wednesday, the green of it gone deeper in the failing light. The Tibullus letter was inside the brown cover at eight lines. The Greek Anthology was on the right corner of the desk, still unopened. Lena's letter was on the blotter to the right of her right hand.

She drafted the first reply on the Saturday morning.

*Lena,*

*Thank you for your letter. I shall write to William this week. I did not intend silence. I did not intend any of it.*

She read this at the level of the third line and stopped. *I did not intend any of it* would, in Lena's reading, mean the leaving as well as the silence, and that was not what she meant. She struck the line through. She struck the whole through. She tore the paper in half and put the halves in the wastepaper basket.

She drafted the second on the Saturday afternoon.

*Lena,*

*I shall write to William this week. He will have a letter from me by the end of the month. You did not need to be the one to remind me. You were right to be.*

She read this. *You did not need to be the one to remind me. You were right to be* was a sentence that wanted to do two things at once and would do, in Lena's reading, neither. It was the kind of sentence that the year's tutorials had taught her not to write in Greek. She struck it through. She tore the paper. She put the halves in the basket.

She walked back from the basket to the desk and stood at the window. The mountain was the same shape it had been at six in the morning. The light on the stone of the residence wall was the dry brass-coloured light of the Cape autumn at four in the afternoon.

She knew what was wrong with the sentences. She had been trying to write to Lena in English. She had been trying to write to Lena in the English of the residence and the seminar room and the Stratford-Reid drawing room because the English was the language her body now sat inside, the language that ran in her sentences before any other, the language she now read in and dreamed in and wrote tutorial papers in. The English was Pa's language. The English was Pa's classroom at the Merensky Farm School and Pa's brief plain register at the kitchen door and the Latin that ran underneath the English in the way the Latin ran underneath everyone Pa had taught for thirty years. Lena did not have any of this. Lena had the slow even Afrikaans of the lower Soutpansberg farms her grandmother had stitched the dark wool shawl in. Lena had the kitchen Afrikaans of the bread and the basin and the spoon. Lena had no English of her own except the English she had to use when a doctor or a clerk or a tax inspector required it, and which she had used at those moments without complaint and without intimacy.

Ruth had been writing to Lena in the language Lena did not have. The leaving was the leaving from one language into the other. The leaving could not be explained in the language she had left for. She would have had to write the letter in Afrikaans for Lena to be able to receive it as a letter from her daughter and not as a letter from a stranger using a register Lena recognised but did not own.

She did not know if she could still write in Afrikaans. She had not written in Afrikaans for the eighteen months. She had written tutorials and prize verses and a brown Tibullus and a great many letters to people who were not Lena. She had read Lena's letter in Afrikaans without effort. She had read it as her own language. But the writing was a different muscle. She did not know if the muscle had survived the eighteen months at the desk in Room 412.

She did not, on the Saturday afternoon, attempt the third draft. She walked down through Government Avenue to the Botanical Gardens and along the avenue of camphor trees as far as the company gardens fish pond and stood at the rail of the pond looking at the koi for a longer time than the koi required and walked back. She had no thought in her head she could afterwards have described.

On the Sunday morning she drafted the third. She drafted it slowly. She wrote it out twice. The second writing-out did not require any change.

*Lena,*

*Thank you for your letter. I shall write to William this week.*

*I am sorry I left as I did. I am not sorry I left. I do not know how to put those two sentences together in a letter and have them say what I mean, so I shall let them stand as they are.*

*You said you did not need a letter back. I have written one anyway, on my own account.*

*Ruth*

She read it twice. She folded it. She put it in an envelope. She addressed the envelope to Mrs L. MacLeod, *Letaba Estates*, P.O. Mooketsi, Letaba District. She put a three-penny stamp on it from the strip in the desk drawer. She set the addressed envelope on the desk to the right of the blotter and did not, that morning, take it down to the pillar box at the corner of Government Avenue.

She went up to Sunday tea at Eleanor's.


III

The common room at Sunday tea was a room of the easy female afternoon whose conventions Ruth had now spent six weeks learning, and the conventions were that Eleanor had her chair by the fireplace whether the fire was lit or not, and Connie had her chair at right angles to Eleanor with the long legs crossed, and Vivienne sat on the window seat with the pale grey wool of her sleeve against the pane, and the others, Marion Eddowes-Black, Penny Liversidge, Beatrice Ankarcrona who was Swedish and read English, came and went as the afternoon moved. The tea things were brought up by the kitchen at quarter to four. Eleanor poured. The cigarette case lay open on the low table beside the milk jug, brushed brass with the gold-tooled interior, and the small monogrammed signet on the lid caught the window light at the angle Eleanor preferred for it.

Ruth came in at five past four. She sat in the further armchair, the one beside Vivienne's window seat. Connie had been in the middle of an account of her brother's regiment having had its post-war silver returned on the wrong train and the room was laughing in the controlled common-room way. Ruth received a cup. She drank it. She did not speak.

After perhaps twenty minutes Eleanor turned slightly toward her and said, in the easy register she used for the others, 'MacLeod. Walk on the mountain on Saturday. Connie and Hugh and Robin and a Miss Wallis-Hewitt I should like you to meet. We thought the Saddle path. Gentle. A picnic.'

'Yes. Thank you, Stratford-Reid.'

'Good.'

Eleanor turned back to Connie. The conversation moved on. Ruth was not asked anything else.

When the tea was done and the others were going down, Eleanor stayed standing by the low table putting the cups onto the tray for the kitchen, and Ruth waited for the room to clear, and went over.

'Stratford-Reid.'

'Yes.'

'I have had a letter from my aunt.'

'The one in Oranjezicht, or the other one.'

'The other one.'

'Ah.' Eleanor set down a cup. She did not look up at once. When she did look up, her face was the careful face she had used in the corridor on the Wednesday. 'Are you replying.'

'I have replied. I have not posted.'

'I see.'

'I do not know whether the not-posting is wisdom or cowardice. I thought you might know.'

Eleanor looked at her for a moment. Then she took the brass cigarette case from the table and offered it. Ruth shook her head. Eleanor took one for herself, lit it, exhaled toward the window.

'I do not know either of us well enough yet to say which it is in your case,' Eleanor said quietly. 'I shall say what my mother told my brother about a letter to my father in 1944. She said: post it the same hour you write the version that does not feel clever. If you have a clever sentence in it, take it out. If, having taken it out, the letter still says the thing, post it. If it does not, write it again.'

'I see.'

'I have a stamp.'

'I have a stamp, Stratford-Reid.'

'Of course you have a stamp.' Eleanor turned the case in her hand and closed it and set it down. 'I am sorry, MacLeod. I do not know how to do this gracefully.'

'You have done it gracefully.'

'I have done it adequately. Which is what one can do, for these things, on a Sunday afternoon over the cups.' She smiled, briefly. 'Saturday. Wallis-Hewitt is reading law. She has the kind of mind that does not allow you to be alone in the wrong way.'

'Thank you.'

She went down the stairs to Room 412. The envelope to Lena was where she had left it, square to the desk edge. She read the letter through once more. It still said the thing. She took it down to the pillar box on the corner of Government Avenue at the foot of the residence path and posted it. The brass slot of the box took it without sound.

Walking back up the path she discovered that her hands were shaking, slightly, in the way that they had shaken the morning at Caledon Square when Hannah had not been there.


IV

Willi's letter came on the Monday with the first post.

It was in his hand, the careful round hand of a boy of twelve taught by Mr Holloway at the Merensky Farm School to give the descenders their proper length. It was on a half-sheet of the school's exercise paper. The envelope was addressed in his careful capitals: *Miss Ruth MacLeod, c/o General Botha Hall, University of Cape Town, Rondebosch, Cape Province*. He had drawn a small line under each line of the address and had crossed out one letter and written it again. She read the letter on the bench in the residence quad before her tutorial.

*Dear Ruth,*

*Lena says you have not written since November. I asked her on Sunday after Pa had gone back to school. I do not think she liked me asking. She did not say I should not have asked, but she did not answer the question, and at the supper she did not look at me when she passed the dish. So I am writing to you to ask the question Lena will not answer. Are you well.*

*I made a stool in the woodwork class at Merensky. It is my first piece. Mr van Wyk said it was good for a first piece. He said the legs were straight and the seat had not split and that the joins would hold. I have put it on the stoep beside Anna's wicker chair. Anna puts her tea cup on it and the cat sits on it when Anna is not putting her tea cup on it. I have carved my initials on the underside. I thought of carving yours too but I was not sure Ma would like it.*

*Anna had a bad seizure on the Wednesday after Easter. Doctor Caldwell came up from Pietersburg and stayed the night. Lena was awake all the night. I was awake too because I could hear them in the kitchen and I did not want to be the one who slept while Anna was bad. By the morning Anna was sitting on the stoep and Doctor Caldwell said the chest had not been as bad as he had thought it would be. I have been keeping the medicine bottles in a row on the dresser shelf in the order Doctor Caldwell wrote them down. I am the one who counts the doses now.*

*Pa says we are not to ask you when you will come up. I am not asking. I am writing because I would like you to know that the dresser shelf is the dresser shelf and the medicine is the medicine and Anna is Anna and the school is the school. I can tell you these things without asking you the other thing.*

*Your brother,*
*Willi*

She read the letter three times. The third time was the time at which she realised that she had not, since coming down from the train, written one sentence to Willi of any kind. She had written, instead, eight lines into the cover of a brown Tibullus and folded them at Tibullus 3.2, and had thought that was the same as having spoken.

There had been a second envelope in her pigeonhole at the morning post. Davies had handed it to her with Willi's letter and she had taken it from him together with Willi's and had not, on the bench in the quad, opened it because Willi's hand had taken her attention first. She took the second envelope out of her pocket now. The hand was Anna's, the careful upright hand of a girl of ten who was learning her letters slowly because the chest sometimes kept her from school. The envelope was addressed in pencil. The paper inside was a half-sheet of the lined writing paper Lena bought from the general dealer at Tzaneen, the same paper Lena's letter on the Friday had come on. Anna had taken the half-sheet from the stack on the dresser shelf and had written on it without telling her mother.

*Dear Ruth,*

*Willi told me he was going to write to you to ask if you are well. Lena said he could write but that she would not put a stamp on the letter for him. So I am putting a stamp on his letter and a stamp on mine. The stamps are from the small box Pa keeps on the desk in the front room. He will see when he goes to write the lower paddock account at the end of the month and he will know it was me.*

*I am eleven in October. I am in standard four at Merensky. I do not have to go to school every day because of the chest. Mr Holloway brings me the work on the Tuesday and the Friday and I do it on the stoep. I am very good at the spelling. I am not very good at the sums but Mr Holloway says I will get better.*

*The chest was bad in March. The chest is better now. I have a new bottle of the medicine that Doctor Caldwell brought up. It tastes of liquorice and I do not mind it. I had a seizure on the Wednesday but it was a small one and the chest came back at five minutes which is a good time. Doctor Caldwell was pleased.*

*I have your certificates from Pretoria in the brown box on my dresser. I look at them once a week. I keep the kruisement leaves with them so the silver does not turn black. The leaves smell of the kitchen.*

*Lena has your letter to her on the dresser in the kitchen. She has not opened it. I think she will. She is reading the post slowly this month.*

*I do not mind that you have not written. I am writing because I would like you to know that I am here.*

*Your sister,*
*Anna*

She read the letter twice. She read it a third time. She did not put it in her pocket. She held it on her lap on the bench under the oak in the quad with Willi's letter beside it on the slats of the bench and looked at the two letters for the length of time it took the wind to come up off the slope and move the leaves on the lower branch above her. She had not, in the eighteen months, asked Willi about Anna by name in any letter, and Willi had not, in any letter to her, written Anna's name except in passing. Lena's letter on the Friday had not contained Anna's name either. In all the correspondence of the term, Anna's name had been present only by absence, in the way Anna had now decided to write to Ruth herself, in her own hand at ten years old, on her own paper, with the stamp she had taken from Pa's box, on a bench somewhere on the stoep at Letaba in the early autumn.

She put both letters in the inside pocket of her coat and went into the tutorial. Dr Hofmeyr was on Catullus 68. She translated when called upon. She did not afterwards remember what she had said.


V

On the Tuesday morning at the breakfast post a small cream card was in her pigeonhole. Davies handed it to her without remark. The hand on the front was the upright Edwardian hand of a woman of her father's generation. The address was *Miss Ruth MacLeod, General Botha Hall, University of Cape Town*, written in the formal slanted manner she would later come to recognise as her aunt Catherine's. There was no return on the front. She turned the card over.

*22 April 1949*

*Dear Miss MacLeod,*

*I am Catherine MacLeod, of Kenilworth, your father's first cousin. We have not met. I write to introduce myself and to ask after your health, having heard from a mutual acquaintance that you are in your second year at the university. I have been remiss in not writing before. If you should have an afternoon free in May or June, I should be glad to receive you for tea at my house. The address is below. There is no obligation. I write only that you should know the offer stands.*

*Yours sincerely,*
*Mrs C. MacLeod*

*Edenholm, Lower Bishop's Avenue, Kenilworth.*

She read it once. She put it in the inside pocket of her coat with William's letter, Anna's letter, and Audrey's pound note. She did not, that morning, reply to it. She did not, in the next two weeks, reply to it either.

On the Tuesday afternoon at three Hannah was at the corner of Caledon Square nearest the bus rank, in the dark coat she wore for the District, with the leather bag over her shoulder. Ruth had walked down through the Gardens at the half-pace that did not look like running and had arrived at five to. Hannah saw her coming and did not move from the corner, and did not smile at first, and when Ruth was close enough to speak said, quietly, 'Walk with me to the upper end of Roeland Street. The tea room is shut on Tuesdays at present. We shall walk.'

'Yes.'

They walked up past Caledon Square and turned right into Buitenkant Street and up the long incline to the upper end of Roeland Street. Hannah set the pace. The pace was the pace of two women who had a private conversation to have and wished to be seen, by anyone who saw them, to be having an ordinary one.

'You missed last fortnight,' Hannah said.

'A tutorial overran. I am sorry.'

'I waited the full forty. I assumed an academic obligation.'

'It was Hofmeyr on Peleus.'

'Ah.' Hannah, briefly, smiled. 'Then I assumed correctly.'

They walked perhaps a hundred yards in silence. The afternoon was bright and dry and the wind had the new April edge that meant the Cape autumn had begun in earnest. At the corner of Buitenkant and Commercial Hannah said, 'I have something to tell you and I should rather tell it walking. Do not stop. Do not take notes. Listen and continue at this pace.'

'Yes.'

'A man came to my flat on the Thursday before Easter. He introduced himself as from the office of the registrar at the university. He said he was conducting a courtesy enquiry into the unofficial tutoring arrangements that some students are reported to have entered into in the city. He said he would be obliged to me if I would tell him for whom I conducted such tutoring and on what days and at what fees.' Hannah's voice was even. 'I told him that I had once given private tuition in conversational German to a postgraduate student of theology, that this had concluded in 1947, that I was not at present engaged in any such arrangement, and that I would be happy to take down any further enquiry he wished to leave. He did not leave any further enquiry. He stayed for ten minutes in my front room. He looked at the books on the shelves. He left.'

Ruth did not stop. She did not slow.

'He gave a name,' Hannah said. 'It is a name I do not know whether you know. He said his name was Pryce.'

'I know it.'

'I thought you would. A junior under-warden gave you his name in November in the lower hall.'

'Yes.'

'He did not give the names of the students about whom he was enquiring. He did not need to. It was enough that he had come.' Hannah, walking, looked straight ahead of her. 'He was not from the office of the registrar. There is no office of the registrar that conducts courtesy enquiries of that sort. He was, I assume, from another office.'

'Yes.'

'I have continued the Tuesday channel. I shall continue it. I think we should consider, in the next several weeks, whether the channel ought to move. Not yet. Not on the day after the visit. Not for some weeks after the visit, so that the move does not appear to be a response. After May, perhaps. We shall agree it then.'

'Yes.'

They had reached the upper end of Roeland Street. Hannah turned and they began to walk back down the long incline at the same pace.

'There is another matter,' Hannah said. 'Joseph Adams's son Daniel did not come back from the verification visit on the Friday after we were at the shop. He was held, on the original document irregularity, until the Monday. On the Monday he was released to the registered address in Buitenkant Street. On the Tuesday a second visit was paid by a different officer who said the address was inadequate. He was taken again. He has been in the Roeland Street prison since the Tuesday awaiting administrative review. Joseph Adams has a lawyer. The lawyer is competent. The boy is not yet charged.'

'I see.'

'I am not telling you in order that you should do anything. I am telling you because you were in the room when it began and I do not believe in keeping the people who were in the room from knowing how the room continued.'

'Thank you.'

'Sarah asked after you,' Hannah said, not turning her head. 'On the Friday at the shop. She did not say it as a message. She said it as a thing she had observed in herself.'

'Yes.'

They walked on down the incline. At the corner where they would separate, Hannah by the bus rank for District Six, Ruth back across Government Avenue to the residence, Hannah stopped and looked at her properly for the first time in the conversation. Hannah's face was the face Ruth had seen at the tea room the afternoon they had walked up to Tennant Street. It was the face that did not pretend that what was happening was not happening.

'Ruth.'

'Yes.'

'Whatever you are writing in the evenings in your room: do not post the letter you are not certain about. Post the letter you are certain about. The other one you keep, or you burn. Pryce has not taken any letter of yours. He may, in time. The letters that he takes will be the ones that he can use. The ones he cannot use he will not bother with. Make sure the letters that you post are the ones he cannot use.'

Ruth did not answer.

'I am not telling you not to write the other letters,' Hannah said. 'I am telling you not to post them.'

'Yes.'

'Friday at six, your aunt rings the porter's box. You may, if you wish to, mention to your aunt that I have had a visit. You may not mention me by name and you may not mention the District. You may say a friend who teaches privately in the city has had a visit from a man who introduced himself misleadingly. Your aunt will know what to do with the sentence.'

'Yes.'

'Good.'

Hannah turned and went toward the bus rank. She did not look back.


VI

On the Tuesday evening Ruth sat at the desk for a long time. She did not light the lamp.

She had on the desk in front of her: Willi's letter, Anna's letter, the silk square, the brown Tibullus with the eight lines folded inside, a clean half-sheet of the residence writing paper, a bottle of the standard residence ink, and the brass paperknife.

She wrote Willi a letter. She wrote it slowly. It took an hour. It was two paragraphs at the pitch of a letter to a twelve-year-old brother who had thought of carving her initials on the underside of his first stool and had not been sure their mother would like it, and it contained no mention of Hannah, no mention of Pryce, no mention of the German or Hebrew evenings, no mention of the silk square, no mention of the bench at Muizenberg, no mention of Lena's letter, and no mention of the eight lines folded into the brown Tibullus. It contained the news that she was well, the news that she was reading classics with Dr Hofmeyr who was a good man and slow, the congratulation on the woodwork stool at the precise pitch the news had earned, a question whether Mr van Wyk taught the upper standards as well, the sentence *I am sorry the silence has gone on as long as it has*, the sentence *I am glad Anna has the stool*, and the closing sentence *Tell Anna that I have her letter and that I shall write to her separately on the Wednesday*. The handwriting at the close was the slow careful hand she had used to copy out her own first Latin verbs at his age.

She read it through. She folded it. She addressed an envelope: *Master W. MacLeod, Letaba Estates, P.O. Mooketsi, Letaba District.* She put a three-penny stamp on the envelope from the strip in the desk drawer. She set the envelope on the right of the blotter, square to the edge.

That was the certain letter.

She took out the brown Tibullus.

She unfolded the letter inside. Eight lines, written over six weeks, the ink at the first lines violet-black and at the eighth a paler blue. She read it through. It said the things she had not been able to say in any letter since November. It said them in a Latin grammar more careful than her actual speech, and it said them to a person whose name was not on the head of the letter.

She did not, that evening, add a ninth line.

She refolded the letter at Tibullus 3.2. She closed the brown cover. She sat for some time with her hand on the cover.

Then she did a thing she had not in six weeks done. She took out a fresh half-sheet of the residence writing paper. She did not, on this sheet, write a letter to anyone whose name she had not put at the head. She put at the head, in her ordinary hand, *Judith,*. She wrote three sentences underneath. She wrote them in English. She did not look up between them.

*The Greek Anthology arrived. I have not opened it. I am writing to thank you for the book and to ask whether you would prefer me to return it unopened or read it.*

*Ruth.*

She read the three sentences. They were the certain sentences. They were the sentences she could write to Judith in such a way that, were they read by anyone, they would say only what they said, and would be intelligible only to Judith because Judith would know that Ruth was a person who would not write *unopened or read it* without knowing exactly what she was offering and exactly what she was asking.

She folded the sheet. She addressed the envelope: *Miss J. Cohen, c/o 14 Brownlow Road, Tamboerskloof.* She put a three-penny stamp on it.

She had now two stamped, addressed envelopes square to the right of the blotter. The Tibullus stayed in the brown cover, unopened, on the desk to the left.

She sat for some minutes. Then she put her coat on and took the two envelopes down through the residence quad to the pillar box at the corner of Government Avenue. She posted them in the order she had written them. William first. Judith second. The brass slot took each in turn.

She walked back up the path.

The wind had dropped. The pines were quiet for the first time that week. The mountain above the residence was dark against a sky that still carried, in its upper third, the daylight. She did not feel, as she walked, the shaking that she had felt on the Sunday after Lena's letter went into the box. She felt, instead, the closer and more particular emptiness that came from having posted the letters that were certain and from having left, in the brown Tibullus on her desk, the letter that was not.


VII

The Wednesday morning post came at half past nine. Ruth had a tutorial at ten. She went down through the porter's box at twenty to.

Davies handed her a letter.

She looked at the hand. It was Judith's hand. She had seen it once, on the small card with the Greek Anthology, and once before in November on the brown-paper note that had come with the silk square. She knew it. The envelope was the cream of Brownlow Road. The postmark was the previous afternoon, four o'clock, Cape Town.

She thanked Davies. She walked out of the residence and down the path and stopped under the first oak in Government Avenue and opened the letter.

*Ruth,*

*I have sat with this for some weeks before writing. I shall be sailing for England on Saturday on the Edinburgh Castle from Table Bay docks, the eight o'clock tide. Brownlow Road is being closed for a year. My mother, who is unwell, has asked me to come back to Hampshire and I have not the energy this winter to find the reason to refuse. I shall be away for at least a year and possibly two.*

*I am not asking you to come to the docks. I do not think it would do either of us a service. I should rather you did not come.*

*The book is yours. Read it, do not return it, do not write me about it. If you wish to write to me about anything else, the Hampshire address is at the foot of this letter; the Brownlow address will be looked after by Mrs Bellingham, the upstairs tenant, who is discreet.*

*You and I have not been a thing one writes a sentence about, and I shall not now. I have been, however, glad of you. I shall continue to be. I shall not be sorry to be at sea for ten days.*

*Look after yourself.*

*Judith*

*Edenfield, Lower Bockhampton, Hants.*

Ruth stood for a long time under the oak.

She thought of the brass slot of the pillar box at the corner of Government Avenue. She thought of the envelope she had posted on the Tuesday evening, the envelope to *Miss J. Cohen, c/o 14 Brownlow Road, Tamboerskloof*, which would arrive at Brownlow Road on the Thursday morning at the earliest. By the Saturday at eight o'clock Judith would be on the Edinburgh Castle. The envelope would be at Brownlow Road. The envelope would be opened, possibly, by Mrs Bellingham, the upstairs tenant, who was discreet. The envelope would say only, in her hand: *The Greek Anthology arrived. I have not opened it. I am writing to thank you for the book and to ask whether you would prefer me to return it unopened or read it. Ruth.*

It would say, to Mrs Bellingham, only that it said. Mrs Bellingham would put it aside for Judith and Judith would, in due course, in Hampshire, receive it and read it and understand what it had said and what it had not said.

It was the certain letter.

She had posted the certain letter to a person who was leaving on Saturday, and the uncertain letter, the one that was eight lines folded at Tibullus 3.2 in a brown cover on her desk, was still on her desk.

She folded Judith's letter and put it inside the inside pocket of her coat where William's letter had been and where the pound note from Audrey was. She walked to the tutorial. She translated when called upon. She did not afterwards remember what she had said.

In Room 412 that evening she did not light the lamp. She sat at the desk and watched the brass-coloured western light go down the wall to the level of the skirting board. The mountain above the residence roofline lost its definition. The pines moved in their slow dry agreement of the evening.

She took out the brown Tibullus. She unfolded the letter inside.

She added the ninth line.

She did not, that evening, add the address at the head.

She refolded the letter at Tibullus 3.2 and closed the brown cover. She put her hand flat on the cover. Beside the cover, on the desk, was the silk square, still open. Beside the silk square was the Greek Anthology, still unopened.

She did not put any of the three away.

She sat in the unlit room for the time it took the western light to go from the skirting board to the floor. When the light reached the floor she stood up and went to the window. The avenue below was dark except for the porter's lamp. The brass slot of the pillar box at the corner of Government Avenue was a small black rectangle she could just make out in the lamplight. She had posted into it on the Sunday and on the Tuesday evening. It had taken her letters without sound.

She did not, that evening, write the line at the head of the letter to Judith. She did not, that evening, address an envelope.

She left the desk as it was.

She lay down on the bed and listened to the pines.

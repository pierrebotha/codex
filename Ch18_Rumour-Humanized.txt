CHAPTER 18

Rumour


I

The Saturday walking party met at the lower gate of Kirstenbosch at half past nine.

Eleanor was there first, in the brown corduroy trousers and the green Norfolk jacket she wore on the mountain, with Connie a pace behind her in tweed and a long woollen scarf. Hugh Stratford-Reid was twenty-eight and a barrister and had Eleanor's high colour and the same close intelligent attention; he had borrowed Robin Hayes-Whittaker's mother's car and Robin had got down from it with a flask and the Bartholomew map of the peninsula. Miss Wallis-Hewitt had walked over from her digs in Newlands and arrived at the gate at twenty to ten with no apology and no excuse, in a brown jacket and grey flannel trousers, carrying a canvas pack on one shoulder.

Eleanor performed the introductions in the courteous abbreviated way she used on the mountain.

'Ruth MacLeod, classics, second year. Frances Wallis-Hewitt, law, fourth.'

'MacLeod.'

'Wallis-Hewitt.'

The hand was broad and dry. The handshake was the handshake of someone who had decided in advance not to make an opening remark. Frances Wallis-Hewitt was perhaps an inch taller than Ruth, with a long pale face and pale brown hair cut at the nape and grey eyes set rather close together. The grey eyes did the looking that the opening remark would have done, and then released it.

'Have you walked the Saddle before, MacLeod.'

'No.'

'It is gentle. The path is broken in two places under the Apostles but Hugh has been up this week and reports it dry. We shall be three hours up and an hour at the top and three down with the picnic at the col.'

'Yes.'

That was the conversation at the gate.

Hugh took the lead. Eleanor and Connie went second. Robin third. Frances and Ruth fell in behind. The path went up through the gardens past the cycad collection and the protea beds and the fern dell, and then out through the upper gate into the contour path that ran north under Castle Rock. The morning was high and dry and the wind had not yet got up. The mountain above them was the wall of grey rock Ruth had now climbed once, in the freezing summit week of the Easter party, and her body remembered the freezing more sharply than it had remembered, in the moment, the climb. She had said yes to the Saturday with the assumption that her body would not, on the Saddle path, be required to refuse.

Frances walked at her shoulder for the first hour without speaking. The silence was the silence of a person who had decided that two people who were going to spend a day on a mountain together did not need to speak in the first hour. The silence was not a question. Ruth, because she had not been asked, did not answer.

At the saddle below the Newlands ravine the party stopped at a flat-topped boulder. Hugh produced biscuits. Connie produced a bar of chocolate divided into squares. Frances took out a green enamelled flask of cold tea and offered it first to Ruth.

'Cold tea is better than warm tea on a mountain.'

'Yes.'

She drank. The tea was dark and clean and faintly bitter. She handed the flask back. Frances drank after her without wiping the rim.

Eleanor was sitting on the boulder with Connie, the cigarette case open between them, the two of them in low conversation about Goldsworthy's daughter's engagement. Hugh and Robin had walked a little ahead to look down into the ravine. Frances sat down on the flat of the boulder and patted the place beside her once, briefly, in the manner of a person inviting a colleague to sit and confer.

Ruth sat.

'Eleanor said yesterday at tea that you had had a difficult fortnight,' Frances said. Her voice was low and even. 'She did not say what kind of difficult. I am not asking. I want to say only that I have a quiet flat in Newlands with a kettle and an armchair and a wireless that gets the BBC on a clear evening, and if you should at any point in the next several weeks need a quiet hour somewhere that is not the residence and not the city, the flat is yours. I shall give you the address before we go down. You may use it without me there. I shall have a key cut for the porter.'

Ruth looked at her properly for the first time since the gate. Frances was looking at the ravine.

'That is a kind offer.'

'It is a practical offer. I made it because Eleanor said you might need it. I am not in love with you, MacLeod. We have known each other forty minutes. I am an officer of the women's law society and I have, on three occasions in the last eighteen months, had to find a quiet flat for a woman who was not yet ready to leave a residence and not yet able to stay in one. I have learned that the offer is best made before it is needed. It does not, having been made, require to be taken up.'

'I see.'

'Good.'

Frances handed her the flask again. Ruth drank again. They sat on the boulder in a silence that was now a different silence, and Connie's voice carried over from where she sat with Eleanor, the long vowels and the easy laughter, and Hugh and Robin came back from the edge of the ravine, and Hugh said, 'Up we go.'

The party went up.

At the col below the Apostles they ate the picnic. Eleanor opened the basket. There were ham sandwiches cut into rectangles, hard-boiled eggs in twists of waxed paper, an apple each, and a jar of pickled cucumber. Connie poured the cold tea. Frances ate her egg slowly and without comment. Robin gave a brief account of a junior's brief in the magistrate's court that had ended in a confusion of typewritten pages and dropped pencils. Hugh corrected a minor point of procedure. Eleanor laughed, briefly, and did not look at Ruth.

The walk down was easier than the walk up. They came out at the gate at four. The car drove up Hof Street to drop Frances at her digs and Ruth at the residence on the way. Frances handed Ruth a folded sheet of paper at the gate of her digs.

'The address. The porter at my building is Mr Anstey. He has his lodge in the front hall. I shall leave the key with him this evening. He is told to give it to a Miss MacLeod.'

'Thank you.'

'Yes.'

Frances got out and went up the path to the front door of a Victorian villa with a shallow stoep and a glassed porch. She did not look back. Hugh closed the door of the car and they drove on. In the back seat Connie glanced at Ruth with the brief courteous attention of a woman who had registered something and would not, that afternoon, mention it. Eleanor, in the front beside Hugh, said nothing.

In Room 412 Ruth put the folded paper in the third drawer of the desk under the small tin in which the pound note from Audrey now lived. She washed her face. She lay down on the bed. She did not sleep but she lay there, and the body that had carried her up and down the Saddle path was, for the first time in a fortnight, the heaviest thing in the room.


II

On the Tuesday at three Hannah was at the corner of Caledon Square nearest the bus rank.

'Walk,' Hannah said.

They walked. Up Buitenkant. Past Commercial. The day was overcast and there had been rain in the small hours and the gutters were running.

'Daniel Adams was charged on the Friday,' Hannah said. 'The charge is irregular residence under the Urban Areas Act. He was moved on the Saturday from Roeland Street to the remand wing at Pollsmoor. Joseph Adams went out to him on the Sunday. He is well. The lawyer is preparing the case for hearing in three weeks. The charge as drawn is, the lawyer says, defensible. He thinks the boy will be discharged with a caution and a removal order to the township at Athlone. He thinks this will be presented in court as leniency.'

'Yes.'

'I am telling you this in the same spirit as before. You were in the room. You should know how the room continued. I am not asking you to do anything.'

'Yes.'

They walked perhaps a hundred yards in silence.

'There is a second matter,' Hannah said. Her voice was the same even voice. 'I should like you to listen carefully. I was at the Roland Street public library on the Friday afternoon and a woman I do not know well, a Mrs Behr who teaches piano in the upper Gardens, stopped me at the issue desk and said she had heard from a friend at Kirstenbosch that Eleanor Stratford-Reid's circle had taken a young Frenchwoman onto the mountain on the Saturday. I corrected her gently. I said that the circle had taken a young South African woman called MacLeod onto the mountain. Mrs Behr said: ah, the scholarship girl from the residence. I said: yes. Mrs Behr said: I had heard differently. I did not pursue the sentence. She did not press it.'

Ruth did not slow. Ruth did not look at Hannah. She continued at the pace.

'I had heard differently is not a sentence,' Hannah said. 'It is a place where a sentence is being built. Mrs Behr has heard something she has not yet decided how to say. She has heard it from a friend at Kirstenbosch, which is to say from someone who saw the party at the gate or at the contour path or at the col. She has heard it before the Tuesday, which means the sentence has moved at the speed gossip moves when it is being assisted. I do not yet know what the sentence is. I am telling you that there is a sentence.'

'I see.'

'Is there a reason a sentence might be assembling.'

Ruth thought. The long wet incline of Buitenkant Street was empty in front of them. Frances at the boulder with the green flask. Frances saying *I am not in love with you, MacLeod.* Connie in the back of the car looking sideways once. Vivienne, who had not been on the walk, who had been at Sunday tea the week before and had said almost nothing, whose father was on the residence committee. The Brownlow Road envelope arriving at Brownlow Road on the Thursday into the hands, possibly, of Mrs Bellingham the upstairs tenant, who was discreet, but who lived in a city in which the sentence *unopened or read it* had a register.

'There may be,' Ruth said. 'Two reasons. Either separately or together.'

'Tell me, walking, the structural shape of each. Not the content. The shape.'

'One is a letter posted on the Tuesday before last to an address from which the recipient had on the Saturday departed for England. The letter says nothing on its face. It contains a sentence that, between the recipient and me, has a private register. It will have arrived at the address on the Thursday. It will have been opened, possibly, by an upstairs tenant who is discreet but who is a Cape Town tenant.'

'Yes.'

'The other is a walk on Saturday up the Saddle with Eleanor's circle and a Miss Wallis-Hewitt of the law faculty. Miss Wallis-Hewitt offered, on the boulder below the ravine, the use of a quiet flat in Newlands. The offer was made in the open and without metaphor. It was practical. Eleanor saw it. Connie saw it. The sentence, were it overheard, would not embarrass anyone over thirty. Were it overheard by someone under thirty, it would be a sentence that could be repeated as anything one wished it to be.'

'Yes.'

'I do not know which.'

'It does not yet matter which. The two sentences do the same work in the city. They become, in Mrs Behr's mouth, *I have heard differently*, and in someone else's mouth tomorrow they become a complete sentence.' Hannah, walking, looked straight ahead. 'You should be ready for it to be presented to you by a person of authority within the next ten days. It will not be presented as accusation. It will be presented as concern.'

'Yes.'

'When it is presented, you will not deny it and you will not confirm it. You will receive it. You will say: thank you for telling me, I shall consider what you have said. You will not, in that conversation, name any other person. You will not name Miss Wallis-Hewitt. You will not name the letter. You will not name your aunt. You will receive and you will go.'

'Yes.'

'After the conversation you will write, in your own hand, a brief account of the conversation: who, when, where, what was said, what you said. You will give the account to me at the next Tuesday and I will keep it. If a second conversation is held at a later date, the account becomes the record. You do not want to be in a situation in which the only record is the other person's.'

'Yes.'

'Is there a person in the residence to whom you would say, if pressed, that you had been on the walk, on the Saturday, with a circle of friends, and would name them.'

'Eleanor.'

'Eleanor will, if asked, confirm her own name and say nothing else. Connie?'

'Yes.'

'Hugh and Robin?'

'Yes.'

'Vivienne Pryce?'

Ruth stopped, briefly, at the corner of Roeland Street.

'I had not said her name to you.'

'You had not. Her father, however, sits on the residence committee. Her father is not the Pryce who came to my flat. He is a cousin. The cousin is in the Eastern Cape. The Vivienne Pryce in your residence is the daughter of the cousin's brother, who is on the residence committee and who is also a member of the Athenaeum Club in Adderley Street where my Pryce, my visiting Pryce, has a town address. They are not the same man. They are not in the same office. They have the same Christmas list. I learned this on the Friday from a librarian.'

'I see.'

'Vivienne was not on the walk. Vivienne was, on the Saturday, in the residence common room at Sunday tea on the previous Sunday at which Eleanor first said your name in connection with Wallis-Hewitt's. She heard the name. She has had a week to write a letter to her father.'

'Yes.'

'Walk on, Ruth.'

They walked. They turned at the upper end of Roeland Street and came back down the long incline.

'There is one further thing I shall say to you and not say again,' Hannah said. 'If you need the flat in Newlands, you may use it. It does not, in itself, signify. If you do not use it, you may, also, choose not to use it. Either is yours. The flat is not the question. The question is what record exists, and who has built it, and what it can be made to say.'

'Yes.'

At the corner where they would separate Hannah did not stop. She nodded once, briefly, in passing, and went toward the bus rank for District Six. Ruth crossed Government Avenue and went up the path to the residence.


III

Audrey rang at six on the Friday.

'Bellevue.'

'Aunt Audrey.'

'Ruth. I shall not keep you. I rang chiefly to say that a gentleman by the name of Mr Pryce telephoned the house yesterday afternoon and asked to speak with me about a matter of residence committee business. I declined the call and asked the housekeeper to take a message. The message, when written down, was that Mr Pryce wished to speak to me about the welfare of a young woman of his acquaintance who he understood to be a relation of mine. He did not give the young woman's name. He left a number at which I might reach him. I have not reached him. I shall not reach him.'

'I see.'

'I do not propose to play that conversation. If he wishes to address a question to my husband or me about our niece's welfare, he may put it in writing on the appropriate university letterhead and send it through the registrar. He may not ring Bellevue at three on a Thursday and leave numbers with my housekeeper. It is not, in my house, the form.' Audrey's voice was mild. The mildness was the same mildness with which she had said, three weeks before, *Thomas has not been home since Sunday afternoon.* 'Have you had any difficulty at the residence.'

'Not yet.'

'Define *yet*.'

'I have been told, by a person whose judgment I trust, that a person in authority will present a concern to me within ten days. I do not know who. I do not know precisely the concern. I have been instructed how to receive it.'

'Good. By whom instructed.'

'The friend who teaches privately, who had the visit from the man who introduced himself misleadingly.'

'Then I need not instruct you also. I shall, however, say this. If at any point you wish to leave the residence at short notice and come to Bellevue, you do not need to give me a reason, and you do not need to ring in advance, and you do not need to bring more than what fits in your case. Mrs Robertson will give you the back bedroom on the first floor and ask no questions. You will not be required to dine downstairs.'

'Yes.'

'Thomas is well. He is reading. He has shaved.'

'Yes.'

'And Ruth.'

'Yes.'

'It is not a small thing that you have not asked me what *I* have heard.'

'No.'

'I have not heard anything of substance. I have heard one general murmur from one person at one luncheon, and the murmur was of the variety that one notes and does not pursue. I shall not name the person. I shall say that the murmur, in its general shape, was that a niece of mine had been seen in company that the speaker's set considered indelicate. I said: my niece has been on a walking party with my husband's cousin's daughter and her circle. I named, at that table, Eleanor Stratford-Reid and Hugh and Connie. The speaker withdrew the murmur. The murmur, however, having been spoken once, will be available to be spoken again. I tell you this so that, if it reaches you in another form, you will know that one form of it has been answered and is, in that quarter, no longer fresh.'

'Thank you.'

'Friday again at six.'

'Yes.'

The line closed. Davies, who had been at his ledger throughout the call, stood up, came round the counter, and handed her the registered-post slip that had come for her at four and that he had been holding since.

'For your signature, Miss MacLeod. From Hampshire. The post officer brought it across from the central depot.'

She signed.

The envelope was small. The hand was Judith's, the same Hampshire hand on cream, the letter posted at Southampton on the day the *Edinburgh Castle* had docked. Davies handed her the letter and stepped tactfully back to his ledger.

She did not open it in the porter's box. She went up to Room 412.


IV

The lamp she did not light. The brass-coloured western light was on the wall above the skirting board. She sat at the desk and laid the envelope on the blotter beside Lena's letter from the previous Friday, and beside William's letter from the Monday before, and beside the stamped unaddressed Tibullus letter at nine lines, and the silk square open under her left hand, and the still-unopened Greek Anthology Book V to the right.

She opened the envelope.

*Ruth,*

*Mrs Bellingham forwarded your note to me at Madeira. I have read it. I shall not answer the question it asked because the question it asked has been overtaken on both sides.*

*Mrs Bellingham did not open the letter. Mrs Bellingham, however, recognised the hand on the envelope, mentioned to her sister at supper that a letter had arrived for me from a Miss MacLeod at General Botha Hall, and Mrs Bellingham's sister is a Mrs Behr who teaches the piano in the upper Gardens. I learned this from a steward on the ship who had it from a passenger who knows Mrs Behr. The letter itself has not been opened by anyone whose hand it has not passed through. Mrs Bellingham has now sent it on. The damage, such as it is, is the damage of the envelope having been seen on a hall table.*

*I am sorry. I should have foreseen it. I did not.*

*Read the book. Do not return it. Do not, for the next several months, write to me at the Brownlow Road address. The Hampshire address is safe.*

*Look after yourself.*

*Judith*

Ruth read it once.

She set the letter on the blotter to the right of the envelope and put both hands flat on the surface of the desk on either side of it. She did not move for some time.

Then she got up. She went to the window. The avenue below was quiet. The porter's lamp was on. The brass slot of the pillar box at the corner of Government Avenue was a black rectangle in the lamplight. She looked at it for some time without seeing it.

There was a knock at the door.

She went and opened it. It was Vivienne Pryce.

Vivienne was in the pale grey wool cardigan she had worn at Sunday tea, with her hair done and her hands clasped at the front in the way the housemistress at her old school had taught her to clasp them at confirmation. Her face was the face of a woman who had been sent on an errand and had memorised the sentence she had been given to deliver.

'MacLeod, may I come in for a moment.'

'Yes.'

Vivienne came in. She did not look at the desk. She looked at Ruth. She remained standing.

'My father has asked me to convey a word to you. He asked me to do it privately. He thought it would be kinder coming from me than from the warden. I am not going to say very much. I am going to say it once and then I shall go.'

'Yes.'

'My father and the warden have had a conversation about a concern that has reached them concerning the company you have been keeping. The concern is not specific. It does not, my father said, need to be specific. He asked me to say to you that the residence is a community in which the conduct of a few reflects upon the standing of the many, and that he hopes you will, in the next several weeks, consider carefully the company you keep both inside and outside the residence, with attention to the private arrangements of older students of the law faculty whose reputations, while not subject to formal complaint, are not matters of indifference to the committee.'

She had delivered the sentence as a recitation. She did not, having delivered it, look pleased.

'Thank you, Pryce,' Ruth said.

'I have nothing further to say from my father. I shall add, only on my own account, that I did not volunteer to bring the message and I did not enjoy bringing it. I should have rather it had come from the warden, who is paid to do these things.'

'Yes.'

'Goodnight, MacLeod.'

'Goodnight, Pryce.'

Vivienne turned. She paused at the door, briefly, with her hand on the handle. Without looking back she said, 'I do not believe what is being said about you. I think you should know that. I am going to bed.'

She went out and closed the door.

Ruth stood for a moment in the centre of the room.

She went to the desk. She sat. She took out a fresh half-sheet of the residence writing paper. She did not light the lamp; the western light on the skirting board was enough to write by for one short paragraph.

She wrote, in her ordinary hand:

*Friday 13 May 1949, 6.40 p.m. Room 412 General Botha Hall. Vivienne Pryce came to my room and conveyed to me, on her father's instructions and not her own, a message from her father and the warden concerning a concern, unspecified, that has reached them concerning the company I have been keeping, with particular reference to older students of the law faculty. The message was delivered as a recitation of one prepared sentence. After delivering it, Pryce added, on her own account, that she had not volunteered to bring it, that she had not enjoyed bringing it, and that she did not believe what was being said about me. Present in the room: Pryce and myself. No notes were taken at the time. This account is written from memory at 6.40 p.m. on the same evening and the language of the recited sentence is given as closely as I can recall it.*

*Ruth MacLeod.*

She read it through. She folded it in half. She put it in an envelope and addressed the envelope to herself care of Hannah, the Caledon Square corner, the next Tuesday, and put it in the third drawer of the desk on top of the folded address Frances Wallis-Hewitt had given her at the gate of her digs.

The brown Tibullus was still on the desk. The letter inside was at nine lines. The silk square was open. The Greek Anthology was unopened. The pound note from Audrey was in the tin. Lena's letter was in its envelope. William's letter was in its envelope. Judith's two letters were now in their envelopes.

She did not, that evening, put any of them away.

She did not, that evening, light the lamp.

She sat at the desk in the western light until the western light reached the floor, and then in the dark, until the porter's lamp came on outside and threw the quiet pale rectangle of its light across the lower half of the wall opposite the window, and then for some time after that.

The pines, at last, were quiet.

CHAPTER 19

The Glove


I

The telegram came up to the lodge on the Wednesday morning of the third week in May, in the hour Ruth was at Greek and Mr Steyn the porter was sweeping the stoep. Miss Pryce took the form from the messenger at the door, signed for it, and put it inside the side drawer of her desk where she kept letters that were not to be left at the corridor pigeonholes for collection.

Ruth came back from the lower campus at one. She had walked up the path under the laurels and the loose-stoned bend in the drive in the high autumn light that did not warm; the wind was on the slope, off the rock face, and her coat had been buttoned the whole way. She passed the lodge window without looking in and was at the foot of the staircase when Miss Pryce called.

'Miss MacLeod.'

Ruth turned.

Miss Pryce was at the lodge counter with the ledger open and the pen across the place she had stopped writing, as if she had heard Ruth's tread on the stone and lifted the pen in the same movement. The brass key on its chain at her left hand caught the light briefly when she shifted her weight against the counter. Her face did not change.

'A telegram came for you at half past nine. The messenger waited five minutes for me to find a girl from the corridor who could go down to the lecture room. There was no one to send. I did not have it forwarded. A telegram is a domestic matter.'

She opened the side drawer and took out a folded yellow envelope with the South African Post Office crest on the upper corner. She held it out across the counter without ceremony, the second finger of her right hand resting at the upper edge of the paper in a manner that did not relinquish the form until Ruth had taken it.

Ruth took it.

'Thank you, Miss Pryce.'

Miss Pryce did not answer. She picked up the pen and returned to the ledger as if the exchange had not happened, except that she did not, until Ruth had reached the first turn of the staircase, resume the line.


II

Ruth did not open the envelope on the staircase.

She walked the length of the second-floor corridor with the envelope held against her side, between the wool of her coat and the soft inner pocket where the silk square Judith had given her in March had ridden against her ribs since the previous Friday. She did not look down at the pocket, and she did not adjust the envelope, and the corridor lamp at the far end was not yet on for the afternoon, and the light in the corridor was the high cold light of the upper window above the stairwell at the south end, falling along the worn boards in a line that thinned as it went.

She reached her own door. She turned the handle. She went in. She closed the door.

Room 412 in the early afternoon was the room emptied of its night-self. The bed was made. The Greek grammar on the desk was shut at the place she had marked it that morning, a slip of paper with the next morning's translation held inside the page. The cup from her tea, half-finished, stood on the saucer beside the grammar, the spoon laid across the rim, the brown skin of the tea drawn over the surface of the leftover liquid.

She did not sit. She did not take her coat off.

She set the envelope on the corner of the desk where the cup of tea was not. She stood at the desk with both hands on the lower edge of it for the count of three breaths. Then she took the pen-knife from the drawer and slit the upper edge of the envelope with a single short sawing movement, and she did not, while she did this, take her coat off.

The form inside was the standard yellow telegraph form. The header was printed in faded violet. The date was the Tuesday previous. The point of origin was Pietersburg. The message had been struck in the upper-case lettering of the operator's machine and re-typed on the form for delivery.

ANNA TAKEN HOSPITAL TUESDAY EVENING STOP DOCTOR CALDWELL HOPEFUL STOP NO NEED COME UNLESS YOU CAN STOP I WILL WRITE STOP PA

She read it once.

She read it a second time, more slowly, and she stood with the form held flat against the desk under her two hands for a longer moment, and the mountain behind her on the south wall pressed against the room without making itself heard, and the wind off the slope moved against the upper sash of the window in the slight intermittent rattle the window had always had, and the residence on its Wednesday afternoon was for that minute entirely quiet.

She set the form on the desk.

Anna had grand mal. Ruth had been in the kitchen on the farmhouse floor with her five times in the year before she left, and she knew the rhythms of it: the cry before the convulsion that was not crying, the three-minute violence in the limbs, the slow ten-minute reach back into breath afterwards. Anna's chest, which had never been right since the second winter, made the breath the harder of the two phases; the seizure ran its course in the body and let go, and then the lungs began their own separate work, and the work was sometimes long. ANNA TAKEN HOSPITAL TUESDAY EVENING meant that on this occasion the violence had not stopped at three minutes, or that the breath had not come back at ten, or both. Her father would have carried Anna out to the lorry with her head in the crook of his arm. Her mother would have walked behind to the gate without her coat. The lorry would have gone the eighty miles down to Pietersburg in the cold dark of a May evening with Anna in the back wrapped in the brown blanket from the kitchen settle. Doctor Caldwell hopeful was the register Pa had chosen, written in the brief plain English Pa had used at every kitchen door of Ruth's life and meant to keep Ruth from acting. The prognosis itself was not in the telegram.

She took the writing case from the second drawer of the desk and laid it open on the place where she usually wrote her translations. She took out the cream notepaper and the envelope. She took out the small round bottle of black ink and her own pen. She unscrewed the ink and set the pen across the rim of the bottle, and she did not, in any of these motions, take her coat off, and she sat down at the desk in the coat with the buttons all done up and pulled the chair in.

She wrote:

Dear Pa,

Then she stopped.

She sat with the pen above the page and the line below the salutation empty. She heard, under the residence stillness, the dull single chime of the wood-cased clock in the lodge below as it struck the half-hour.

She did not know what to say to Pa about Anna because what she would say to Pa about Anna would be about her own absence and not about Anna. To say *I shall come* would be to say a thing that was not true; the term ran for another three weeks; the scholarship would not stand a leave of absence in May for a sister who was, on the Tuesday, still alive; and the train fare to Pietersburg in May was three pounds eleven, which she did not have, and which she could not ask Pa to send. To say *I cannot come* would be to say a thing she had not thought yet to say to anyone in the family in the eighteen months since she had left. To say *I shall write to Lena* was a thing that referred to a person who had not, in the year and four months since the platform, written to Ruth at all, and a letter to her mother on the matter of Anna's hospital admission would arrive in Pietersburg on the Tuesday and be put on the dresser unopened, and her mother would not, in the kitchen at sundown, lift it from where Pa had set it down.

She did not write any of this. She wrote:

Dear Pa,

I will come if I can. The term ends on the seventh of June. I shall write again with the train. Please tell Anna I think of her. Please tell Willi.

Ruth.

It was four lines including the salutation. She blotted the ink. She folded the page once. She took the cream envelope and addressed it to W. J. MacLeod, Letaba Estates, P.O. Mooketsi, Northern Transvaal, in the slanted formal hand she used for letters home that were not, in the year past, addressed to a particular person except in the matter of the salutation. She sealed it. She put the envelope in the right pocket of her coat.

She put the telegram form into her left glove. She folded it once down the middle and once across the middle and slid it inside the glove between the lining and the leather, where her hand, when she put the glove on, would press against it. She put both gloves on. She picked up her purse and counted out one shilling and eightpence into her right hand, then put the rest of the purse contents back in the desk drawer and put the one and eightpence in the outer pocket of her coat.

She went down by the back stairs. She did not pass the lodge.


III

The tram from the residence stop went down the long Newlands slope through the oak-canopied length of Main Road and into the lower city, and she sat at the front of the white car with the empty seat beside her and her gloved hands folded in her lap, and she did not put either hand to the pocket where the letter to Pa was, and she did not look down at the gloves where the form was folded against the back of her left hand. The wind had risen on the way down. The sea, when it became visible at the lower turn, was the flat dark grey of the autumn Atlantic at three o'clock, the breakers white and broken-edged at the foot of the granite. The tram set her down at the lower end of Adderley Street.

The wind on Adderley was the wind off the bay, finding the gap between her collar and her neck and the cuff of the coat at the wrist. There were men in overalls on the corner near the bank waiting for the lorry to take them back to the docks. There were two women in twin-set and pearls coming out of Stuttafords with parcels in brown string-tied paper. The light off the colonnade of the post office was beginning to soften on the upper storey, the arched windows beginning to take the slow pink shift of the late autumn afternoon.

She did not go into the post office.

She turned right and walked up Adderley as far as the cathedral and then turned into Wale Street and walked along the lower side of Wale until she came to the post box at the corner of Wale and Burg.

The post box was the standard cylindrical pillar box of the Post Office painted post-office red, with the slot at the upper third under the metal hood, and the brass plate with the hours of collection screwed below the slot. The collection had been at twelve. The next collection was at four-thirty. It was a quarter past three by the clock above the chemist on the opposite corner.

She stood at the post box.

She took the envelope out of her coat pocket. The envelope was thick cream paper of the kind the residence stocked for letters to fathers and brothers. The address she had written was upright, plainly black, the slanted scholarly forming of the place name as she had been taught at the convent. She held the envelope in her right hand and put the right hand to the slot of the post box.

She lifted the lid.

The lid was the small hinged metal plate that hung over the inside of the slot to keep the rain off the letters. She lifted it with her thumb. The interior of the box was dark and, even from above, smelt of metal and the dust of the day's posted letters. The envelope under her hand was steady. There was still time, before the collection, for the letter to go on the four-thirty train to Johannesburg and be in Pietersburg by the Friday morning. The lid was open. Her hand did not move.

She let the lid fall from her hand.

The lid fell with the small dull clack of a piece of cast metal returning to its rest.

She put the envelope back in her coat pocket.

She did not stand at the post box. She turned and walked back along Wale Street the way she had come.

For a moment, between the post box and the corner of Wale and Long, she thought of her mother. She did not finish the thought. The thought was of Lena standing at the kitchen window of the farmhouse in the early evening, the light failing in the lowveld behind the bottlebrush, and the dresser in the hall on which the day's post sat unopened until her father came in. The thought went only as far as the dresser. It did not go to the question of whether her mother would or would not lift the letter.

She walked on.


IV

At the corner of Wale and Long there was a bakery.

It was a single-fronted shop with the door propped open against the inside wall and a wooden sign above the door painted in faded gold lettering on a green ground, reading SOLOMON'S in the upright Roman of an older sign-painter, and beneath it in smaller letters, BREAD AND CAKES. The window was warm to the eye. There were brown loaves and white loaves and the long thin loaves the city made in the French style on the wooden trays at the front of the counter, and a tray of round buns dusted with caraway, and behind the counter the older man in a white apron was setting a fresh tray of buns into the warm cabinet at the back wall.

She went in.

The bell over the door was the bell that all the small shops on Wale Street had, a single brass cup on a curled spring above the door-frame. The man turned at the sound and came to the counter. He was perhaps fifty, dark-haired going to grey at the temples, the apron pinned with a single safety-pin at the bib, the dark trousers of an older suit beneath. He took her measure in the second of the turning and adjusted his greeting accordingly.

'Goeie middag, juffrou.'

'Good afternoon.'

'A loaf?'

'A brown loaf, please.'

He took a loaf from the back tray and laid it on the wooden board on the counter. He took a sheet of brown paper from the pile beside the board. He wrapped the loaf in the practised quick economy of a man who had wrapped a hundred loaves that day. He twisted the ends. He laid it on the counter and set a hand on it briefly to confirm the wrapping, and then he looked at her properly for the first time.

'You are not from the Cape.'

'No.'

'I do not ask. I would not know to ask if you were. There is something in the way you stand at the counter.'

She paid him with the shilling and two pennies and put the loaf under her left arm against her side, between her ribs and the inside of the coat. He held the door open with his foot, his hands still on the next tray.

'Thank you.'

'Tot siens.'

She went out into Wale Street and she did not, at the corner of Wale and Burg, look across at the post box.


V

The tram up to Newlands left the lower stop at twenty-five to four, full because of the hour, women with parcels going home before the day's last work, and the two clerks in suits, and a child of perhaps eight in a school blazer with a satchel held in his lap. She stood at the front rail with her right hand on the brass and her left arm at her side under the coat against the loaf, and the loaf was warm against her ribs through the brown paper and the wool, and the form inside the left glove was a small flat square against the back of her left hand, and the letter to Pa in the right pocket of the coat was a folded stiff weight against her hip when the tram took the long bend out of the lower city onto Main Road.

She did not look at the city as it went past. She did not look at the sea on the long left turn under De Waal Drive. She did not look at the upper tram windows where the orange of the afternoon light fell on the brass of the pole and the dark wood of the upper bench. She kept her gaze at the boards of the floor, three feet in front of her boots, and she did not move the gloved hand from the loaf and she did not move the gloved hand from the brass.

The tram set her down at the residence stop at five minutes past four.

She walked up the residence drive in the high autumn light that did not warm. The pines along the drive were in the long single shadows of the late hour. The wind was on the slope, off the rock face. She had the loaf against her ribs under the coat, and the form folded inside her left glove against the back of her hand, and the letter to Pa in the right pocket of the coat, and the silk square Judith had given her in March against the cream of her blouse on the inside of the inner pocket. She walked the length of the drive slowly, neither hurrying nor pausing, the soles of her boots on the gravel making the small individual sound the gravel made under each tread, and the bread above the gravel sound, and the breath above the bread.

She did not pass the lodge.

She went round to the side door at the corner of the building and up the back stairs and along the second-floor corridor to her own door. She closed the door behind her. She set the loaf, in its brown paper, on the corner of the desk where the cup of tea was not. She took her coat off and hung it on the hook behind the door. She took the right pocket of the coat in her right hand and felt for the letter to Pa, and she touched it, and she did not take it out.

She stood at the desk.

The corridor lamp came on at five.

Down the corridor a door opened and closed. A voice said a goodnight to another voice. The residence began the slow domestic accumulation of its evening, the kettles going on for the early tea and the typewriter in the far room ticking with the steady evening concentration of a girl writing something she did not want to put down.

She did not move.

She stood at the desk in the gloves with the loaf in front of her, and the form was inside the left glove against the back of her hand, and she did not, that afternoon, take either glove off.

# Chapter 1: The Mountain and the Sea

She had been on the train for four hours before the farm came back to her.

It came the way the farm always came: through the hands. The lye was a particular ritual her mother had, a caustic, pale liquid she handled without tenderness, without any motion she had not performed two hundred times before. Every Tuesday, the floors were subjected to that chemical erasure, the wood bleached until it held the particular pallor of a thing that has been scoured into submission. Ruth had watched her mother's hands, cracked, labour-marked, carrying the persistent scent of the agent even after washing, and had understood, in the way a child understands a weather system, that there was no room in them for softness. Everything on the farm was functional. Everything was a performance of survival, and the drought was trying hard to make even that impossible.

Her mother had stood by the wood-stove, her back to Ruth, her attention on the dough. She had not looked at her: she had focused on the kneading, the rhythmic thud of the flour-covered wood against the board. The kitchen had been a world of trapped subtropical heat and the smell of yeast, its air thick with the moisture that the Letaba valley held in everything, the flour sacks, the wooden shelves, the grain of the table itself. Outside, the mist had still been sitting in the kloof above the treeline, and the red laterite road had shone dark with the night's rain, and beyond the fence the banana grove had caught the early light through its broad, slack leaves.

Her mother worked through the kitchen in the slow even Afrikaans of the lower Soutpansberg farms she had been born to. She used Afrikaans for the kneading and the counting and the inventory of the morning's work as she had used it for everything she had ever done with her hands. She used English only when the world outside the kitchen required it, and then with the brief unflinching efficiency of a woman who knew the language was not hers.

'Die trein sal nie vir jou pryse wag nie, Ruth,' her mother said, her voice flat as the board she was working. She did not look up. 'Hou daai lêer droog. Reën kom sonder waarskuwing aan die Kaap se kant.'

The Afrikaans was the Afrikaans of every morning of Ruth's childhood, the kitchen's reflex tongue, the language her mother had spoken to her when she had been three days old and had been speaking to her every morning since.

Ruth had held the folder in her lap, a cardboard thing with frayed corners and the university's blue crest, and had not answered. She focused on the texture of the bread in her hand, the crust hard and unforgiving against her thumb.

Her father had sat at the head of the heavy oak table. He had not looked at her either: he had looked at the coffee in his mug, a dark, saturated pool that reflected the diffuse morning light that came through the kitchen window at that hour, when the mist was still thick on the escarpment and the valley had not yet decided what the day would do. His fingers traced the edge of the ceramic.

He had been a man of the rock and the red soil, his leather-bound ledger a damp-edged object that recorded the farm's slow collapse. He had moved with a stiffness that the wet seasons and the bank's arithmetic had pressed into him, his gaze fixed on the escarpment wall.

'Your scholarship will keep you,' he had finally said, his voice dropping as he reached for the bank warrant. He did not look up. 'Mind you let it.'

He had said it in English, the only language he had ever used at the table, the careful unforced English of the schoolmaster who had been thirty years at the Merensky Farm School and had never, in any of those years, taken his wife's Afrikaans into his own mouth. The household's bilingual pattern had been, since before Ruth could remember, that Lena spoke Afrikaans and Pa spoke English and the children answered each parent in the parent's own language without being told to. The two registers had crossed each other in mid-sentence at every meal of Ruth's childhood and had not, in any of them, met.

That had been years ago. That had been the first departure, the morning the wagon took her to the rail stop and then the train took her to Pietersburg and St Joseph's. The convent had taught her Latin and a sterner English than Pa's. The university was going to teach her Greek and a different English again. The Afrikaans she carried with her on this morning's second train was the language she would speak only in dreams from this morning forward, and would not, in any of the years of Cape Town that lay ahead, hear in the corridors of the residence even once. This morning had been different.

The morning at Pietersburg station had arrived before she was ready for it. She had come from the doctor's rooms on Church Street, where Anna had been sitting in a wooden chair near the window, her hands folded in her lap in the particular way she had when she was trying to appear settled. Ruth had not been able to look at her for long. The room had smelled of carbolic and something sweetish underneath it, and the doctor had spoken to William in a careful, low voice that Ruth had not tried to hear.

William had come from the farm two days before, she had not asked him to, and had stood on the platform with the studied practicality of a man who does not trust his face in the open. He had not looked at her: he had looked at the suitcase, the platform edge, the approaching shape of the train.

Esther had come. Ruth had not expected it. She had stood a few feet from William, her coat buttoned to the collar against the morning cold, her hands not quite still at her sides. They had said what they could. There had not been much left to say that was not already said in the looking.

Kit had come too, still carrying the convent's posture, that particular straightness in the spine that St Joseph's had pressed into all of them. She had held Ruth's hand for a moment. She had not said anything. Ruth had been grateful for that.

Lena had not come. Ruth had known she would not. The knowing had not made the platform feel any less empty in the direction of the farm.

The third-class carriage was a world of vibrating mahogany and the dry, scorched scent of its occupants: a collective of silent, sun-baked bodies whose smell was a mixture of sweat, stale bread, and the sharp, metallic tang of the tracks. Ruth sat by the window, her forehead pressed against the cool, vibrating glass. Her fingers were tight around the handle of her suitcase, a cardboard thing that required a specific, forceful twist of the wrist to stay closed.

The passengers in the carriage were a world unto themselves. There were miners returning from the reef, their faces carrying the particular absence of men who have spent too long underground. There were market women with woven baskets, their voices a quick, rhythmic frequency that Ruth found both alluring and unreachable. Each person occupied their allotted space with the self-sufficiency of those accustomed to long distances. Ruth kept her forehead against the glass.

She watched the way the light changed as the hours accumulated. The country had been flattening since Johannesburg, scrub growing lower and more silver-grey, the ridgelines dropping away. Someone in the compartment said 'Karoo' with the flatness of a person naming a country they had always known. Ruth looked at the pale, wide distance outside the glass. Nothing about it resembled home. The red soil was gone. The kloofs were gone. The mist that arrived before first light and sat in the gully until midmorning was gone. She pressed her forehead against the glass and the scholarship folder lay in her lap without weight.

A woman in a floral headscarf sat opposite her, her hands folded in her lap, her eyes fixed on a point several inches behind Ruth's head.

'You going to the city?' the woman asked, her voice a dry rattle through the low hum of the compartment.

'The university,' Ruth said. Her own voice sounded thin and metallic.

The woman gave a short, silent huff of a laugh, a ripple that briefly transformed her face. 'My sister's girl went up to the university,' she said. 'In forty-four.' She looked at Ruth's scholarship folder. Then she looked at the country outside the glass. Whatever had been in the sentence stayed there.

The train journey was a slow, mechanical unspooling of the interior. Ruth watched the towns pass, small whitewashed settlements with names that tasted of Dutch certainty and imperial distance, each one a brief, pale assertion against the vast indifferent rock. The air in the carriage grew heavier, losing the thin, brittle quality of the high country and gaining a weight, a dampness that smelled of coal smoke, diesel, and the peculiar, sweet rot of the coming rain.

The conductor called for tickets, his voice cutting through the hum of the compartment. He moved through the carriage with the practised indifference of someone whose attention had long ago migrated to the work itself, the dates, the codes, the columns of the route, rather than the people behind the documents.

She handed over her warrant. The paper was slightly damp from the condensation on the window, the ink a dark, bruised mark on the white of the page. She took it back and replaced it in the folder. The conductor had already moved on.

At De Aar the engine changed and the stop was long enough for the heat to press into the carriage. Through the window she watched the railway workers move along the sidings, long, practised movements in the still air, the sound of their tools carrying across the emptiness. She looked until she could not look any longer without it becoming something else. The folder stayed closed in her lap.

As the tracks began to curve toward the coast, the air altered, gaining a salt-tasting weight that tore at the hem of her skirt. The mountain did not appear all at once: it emerged from the fog, a sheer, dark face of sandstone that seemed to be pushing the clouds back toward the sea. It was a scale of existence that made the train, the tracks, and the small, hurried towns they had passed look like temporary, fragile constructions.

The train slowed beyond the salt pans, the engine's rhythm breaking into a series of jagged, metallic gasps that shuddered through the floorboards. As the wheels groaned against the platform, the noise hit her first, dispersed and purposeful, the sound of a city that had been going about its business long before her arrival. The station was a cavern of soot and shouting, its granite arches black with accumulated smoke.

Ruth stepped onto the platform, the weight of the suitcase pulling at her shoulder. She saw the signs then. *Europeans Only. Slegs Blankes.* Small, white rectangles of polished enamel, fixed to the pillars with the matter-of-fact permanence of something that had been there long before her arrival and would remain long after. She had known the words. She had understood their logic in the abstract language of her history lectures. The lived reality was different: the silence between the letters, the way no one else in the station seemed to be looking at them.

Ruth walked toward the exit, her boots clicking with a hollow, exposed sound on the tiles. She felt the attention of the station: the neutral, measuring regard of a space built to sort people before it welcomed them. She kept her eyes forward. She walked as if she knew where she was going.

Outside, the light was even sharper, a coastal glare that pressed against the granite facades of the banks and the iron railings of the shops until the world seemed to dissolve into milk.

The taxi driver leaned against a black fender, his cap pulled low. He did not offer to take the suitcase.

'University?' he asked, the word sounding like a challenge.

'Yes,' Ruth said. 'General Botha Hall.'

The drive was a blur of stone facades, iron railings, and the sudden, shocking blue of the sea appearing between the gaps in the houses. The city was built of stone and shadow, every surface reflecting the coastal light back at itself, every building a statement of settled intent. By the time they reached the lower slopes of the mountain, where the university buildings sat against the green, Ruth's palms were damp.

The ascent was steep, the taxi's engine a low mechanical growl that matched the pulse in her own throat. The air altered here, losing the trapped urban roar of the station and gaining a weight, a dampness that smelled of pine needles and cold earth. The mountain did not seem like land: it was a sheer, blue-grey wall of stone that rose directly out of the city's streets, its summit cut off by a flat, white tablecloth of cloud that poured over the edge like slow-motion water. The scale of it made the buildings at its feet look like discarded toys.

The taxi moved through streets carved out of silence. The houses here were large, their white walls reflecting the coastal glare, their gardens precise and imported. Ruth looked at the iron gates, the gravel drives, the high stone walls. This was a world that did not acknowledge the drought of the interior or the industrial soot of the station. She looked at it without looking directly at it.

Ruth stepped out onto the gravel drive of the residence. The wind hit her with a solid, salt-tasting weight that tore at the hem of her skirt and forced her to squint against the grit. It carried nothing of the farm's warm, moisture-heavy air, nothing of St Joseph's trapped, incense-heavy stillness. This was a wind established and indifferent, as if it had been blowing through this place since long before the buildings were raised. A group of girls in tailored blazers and polished leather shoes walked past, their laughter bright and effortless, the sound of people who had arrived somewhere they already knew how to be.

Ruth looked down at her suitcase. In the sharp coastal light, the cardboard looked even greyer, the handle even more precarious. She adjusted her grip, the corded muscle in her forearm tight, and walked toward the heavy oak doors. Behind her, the mountain remained where it was.

The hall ran long under polished parquet, the air trapped and smelling of floor wax, sharp with the synthetic sting of institutional order. It was a cleanliness that did not invite comfort. Ruth stood by the heavy reception desk, her suitcase on the floor beside her. The air here was trapped, insulated from the mountain wind, and it carried the low, constant hum of girls' voices, a sound that rose and fell in waves from the higher floors.

A woman with silver-framed spectacles and a woollen twinset looked up from a ledger. Her gaze swept over Ruth, pausing for a fraction of a second on the frayed suitcase and the salt-stained hem of her skirt. The warden's eyes were not unkind, but they were the eyes of a librarian who had found an overdue book from a library that was no longer in the records.

'MacLeod?' the woman asked. Her voice was clipped, the vowels flattened into a shape that suggested efficiency over warmth.

'Ruth MacLeod,' Ruth said. She kept her hands at her sides.

'Room four-twelve,' Miss Pryce said, sliding a heavy brass key across the desk. It landed with a dull clink. 'Dinner is at six. Standard dress is required. Late arrivals are not accommodated.'

Ruth took the key. It was cold, the metal biting into her palm. She climbed the stairs, the banister smooth under her hand, and felt the weight of the building pressing down on her. On the third floor, a door opened and two girls stepped out, their voices bright with the easy confidence of the unexamined. One was wearing a silk scarf knotted at her neck, the fabric a pale, expensive lemon that matched the sunlight on the peaks. They stopped when they saw Ruth, their conversation hanging in the air like a question.

Ruth did not look at them. She focused on the numbers on the doors, the brass plates gleaming under the electric light. Behind her, she heard the whisper of fabric and the low, muffled sound of a laugh. It was a distant sound, the sound of a world she did not yet know how to enter.

Room four-twelve was small and smelled of cedar and old paper, a space that felt as if it were waiting for someone to finish a story that had been paused for a century. A single window looked out toward the back of the mountain, where the rock was darker, tangled with scrub and the skeletal remains of winter trees. There was a bed with a thin mattress, a desk of heavy, dark oak, and a wardrobe that stood in the corner like a silent sentinel.

Ruth placed the suitcase on the bed. The latch gave way with a dry, metallic snap that echoed in the room's neutrality. She began to unpack, her movements slow and deliberate. She laid out the two dresses, their fabric thin and weary from the lowveld humidity and the convent's constant washings, and placed her books on the desk. They were the only things she owned that carried any real weight.

She took out a small, cracked photograph of her grandmother, the edges yellowed with age. She placed it on the desk next to her Greek grammar. Her matriculation certificate sat in its folder beside it, crisp and white, the only document in the room that said she had a right to be there. Without it she was a girl from the lowveld with two dresses and a suitcase that had already started to give at the seam, going north.

Ruth looked at the thin duvet and the single pillow. The room had the neutrality of a space occupied by many people over a long time and personal to none of them. She touched the cold stone of the windowsill, her fingers tracing the patterns of the sandstone.

She touched the spine of her Greek grammar, the leather cool and familiar, and for a moment, the room felt like it might hold her. The images of St Joseph's came back through the body first. It was in the particular set of her shoulders, in the way her eyes went immediately to the door when she entered a room.

The Virgil she had found in the lye-damp stacks had been small and leather-bound, smelling of cedar and the dry, intellectual dust of a hundred years of careful neglect. No one had known she was reading it. The Latin had opened in her hands like a door she had not known existed inside a wall she had spent two years pressing her weight against.

Arma virumque cano.

The mountain was still there, visible through the glass, and as the light began to fail, the stone turned a bruised, deep purple. She looked at it until the dinner bell rang.

The dinner bell was different from any she had known: a low, brassy clangour, nothing like the high urgent tolling of the farm's emergency bell or the measured, silvered chime of the convent's Angelus. A sound of administrative necessity, calibrated for buildings rather than people.

Ruth stood in the centre of the room.

The corridors down to the dining hall were thick with the scent of lavender, expensive soap, and the astringent bite of floor wax recently applied. Under the high, vaulted ceilings, the voices of the other girls rose in a rhythmic, melodic hum, an easy, unthinking confidence that her own body did not know how to answer.

They moved in clusters, their blazers crisp and smelling of cedar, their laughter a bright, carrying sound that her own body had no register for. Ruth was aware of her own coat, its fabric weary from the lowveld summers and the convent's institutional grey, and her shoes, worn at the heels, the leather polished down to a thin, translucent grey.

Beyond the staircase, the common room opened into mahogany and large windows. A girl sat at the grand piano, her fingers moving over the keys with an absent, even precision. Ruth stopped for a moment, her gaze fixed on the silver ornaments on the mantle, the light catching them. The flowers in the vases were imported roses, their scent already reaching into the corridor.

The dining hall was long polished mahogany under the soft, orange glow of the electric lights, the mountain's wind reduced to a low thrum against the thick glass of the windows. Ruth stood in the queue, her hands clasped over her folder, her gaze on the floor.

The oatmeal was cold and grey in the bowl. The stew tasted of woodsmoke and something more distant that she did not try to name. Ruth sat at the periphery of the light, her hands tracing the silver pattern on her fork, watching the shadows move against the heavy cream curtains.

A girl sat opposite her, wearing a blazer that looked too big for her, a smile that carried no judgment. Her eyes were sharp and unblinking.

'Scholarship?' the girl asked, her voice a dry rattle that cut through the low hum of the room.

'Ruth MacLeod,' Ruth said.

'I'm Miriam,' the girl said. 'Miriam Sachs. You're the one in four-twelve.' She looked at Ruth's hands on the table. 'Scholarship.' She said it not as a question. She picked up her fork and set it down again. 'We're the charity scholars, Ruth. The ones they keep in the corners to remind themselves of their own proportion.' She said it in the tone of someone reading a clause from a lease they have already signed.

Miriam spoke with the clipped, rhythmic cadence of the Bo-Kaap, the kind that tests a stranger's nerve before it opens. It was a language of survival, moving just below the university's official register.

Miriam watched a group of girls at the far end of the table. Her fork made slow circles on the plate. 'They have a name for what they do to us here,' she said quietly. 'Not the official name. The other one.' She waited, as if to see whether Ruth wanted to know it. Then the bell for clearing the first course rang from the far end of the hall. Miriam looked up and did not finish the sentence.

'The mountain,' Miriam said. She did not complete it. Ruth looked at her, but Miriam's attention was already on the high windows and the dark beyond the glass.

After dinner the corridor had emptied. Ruth walked up the stairs, the banister cold under her hand, and did not look at the numbers as she passed them; she had already counted them. Room four-twelve held the silvered glow of the moon on the mountain's face. The Virgil lay where she had placed it on the desk, spine up, a line of moonlight along the leather. From somewhere on the floor below a door opened and closed without footsteps. Ruth lay down without undressing.

She had woken in her clothes.

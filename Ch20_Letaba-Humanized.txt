CHAPTER 20

Letaba


I

The train left Cape Town on the Thursday evening at quarter past eight and reached De Aar at four in the morning for the engine change. Ruth had a third-class compartment to herself for the first hour, then was joined by a woman in a tweed coat and a small boy of perhaps five who slept across his mother's lap from Worcester. She looked out at the dark of the Karoo coming up along the line and she did not, on the journey, take her gloves off, and the right pocket of her coat carried the same letter to Pa she had not posted on the Wednesday in May.

She had written again, on the Tuesday of the week before, a second letter, asking only whether Anna was at home and saying she would come north for the term break, and she had posted the second letter at the post box at the corner of Main Road and Kenilworth station, and a telegram of three words had come back from Pa: ANNA HOME COME.

She had bought the third-class ticket on the Wednesday morning at the railway booking office on Adderley with the four pounds twelve she had taken out of the post office account that held the small accumulated remainder of the scholarship stipend for her books. The return was open. The journey to Pietersburg was fifty hours.

At De Aar she got down for the half-hour the engine took, stood on the platform in the wind off the high karoo, bought a cup of tea at the refreshment counter and a corned-beef sandwich in greaseproof paper, got back on, and the train pulled out at half past four into the dark of the second night.

The morning came up over the highveld at five-past-six, the grass grey-brown in the cold light, the cattle standing in the early sun with their heads down at the drinking troughs, and the women at the line-side stations selling boiled eggs in twists of newspaper and pieces of fried mealie. She bought an egg at Bloemfontein and ate it standing at the carriage door. The boy of perhaps five was awake now and was looking out at the cattle and not asking his mother any of the questions Ruth could see beginning to form behind his face.

At Johannesburg she changed for the evening train to Pietersburg. The wait was four hours. She did not go out into the city. She sat in the third-class waiting room on Park Station with her suitcase between her boots, and she ate the second corned-beef sandwich, and she drank a second cup of tea from the urn on the side counter where the urn-keeper poured for the European queue from one urn and for the Bantu queue from another. She did not, in the waiting room, take her gloves off.

The Pietersburg train left at six and reached the town at five past midnight. The night porter at the Pietersburg station knew her by sight from the year past and the year before that, and he carried her suitcase out to the front of the station and told her in Afrikaans that the lorry from Mooketsi had left an hour before but that there was a man going up to Tzaneen at four in the morning who would take her as far as the Politsi turn-off. She thanked him. She slept in the station waiting room with her head on the suitcase for three hours.

At four she went out and the man at the lorry was Mr van Rensburg, who farmed below her father and who had taken her in to the station the morning she had left in January eighteen months ago, and who said her name once in Afrikaans without further ceremony and lifted her suitcase up into the cab beside her. They drove out of Pietersburg in the dark with the low headlights of the lorry on the gravel of the road north, and the air through the open side of the cab was the cold dry air of the high lowveld at the end of June, the smell of the road dust and the morning mopane, and she sat with her hands in her lap in the gloves and she did not, in the cab, sleep.

He set her down at the Politsi turn-off at a quarter past six, where the road went east into the valley and the morning had begun to come up behind the escarpment. He waited until she had walked the first two hundred yards down the gravel, then turned the lorry back onto the Tzaneen road and went on. She did not, after the first turn, hear the lorry again.

She walked the eight miles down to the gate of Letaba Estates alone.

The morning came up around her on the walk. The escarpment was at her back, the valley opening below her in the bowl of mist that lay at this hour every June over the lower estates, the bottlebrushes along the verges showing the first red of the early winter that she had forgotten the colour of in eighteen months of Cape weather. She walked at the pace she had walked this road as a child. The road had not been re-graded. The kloof at the third mile was the same kloof. The bridge over the lower stream was the same bridge with the same rust on the railing.

She reached the gate of Letaba Estates at twenty to nine.

The gate was the same gate. The same green paint, the same rust at the lower hinge, the same chain looped over the post with the lock not closed. She lifted the chain off and pushed the gate and walked through and let the gate swing to behind her on the small stone in the dust that her father had set there in 1939 to keep the gate from swinging back on the gatepost in the wind. The stone was the same stone. The gate did not swing back.

She walked up the long drive between the avocado trees her father had planted in the year she had been born.


II

The house at the head of the drive sat on the slight rise above the lower paddock with its corrugated iron roof painted the same dark red and its veranda the same bare wood and the kitchen window at the side opening on to the same patch of bottlebrush with the small tin watering-can on the stoep step that her mother had used to water the geraniums in the first year of the war and had set down on the stoep step one June afternoon and had not since picked up. The watering-can was the same watering-can. The geraniums in the bed below the stoep were thinner than she remembered them but were the same geraniums.

She did not go up to the front door.

She walked round the side of the house to the kitchen door, the worn path through the bare patch where the chickens had scratched the grass off in 1944 still bare, and she came up to the kitchen door and she stood at it for the count of three breaths and she did not knock and she did not open it. The door was open six inches against the wedge her mother had put under it on the first morning of the year she had married Ruth's father.

Inside the kitchen she could hear the small clear sound of a wooden spoon against the side of an enamel basin.

She put her hand on the door. She pushed it open. She went in.

Lena was at the table. The table was the same table. Lena was at the side of the table that gave on to the window, with the enamel basin in front of her and the wooden spoon in her right hand and a heap of mealie meal beside the basin on a folded cloth. She was in the dark blue cotton dress she had worn on the day Ruth had left. She had her grey hair pinned at the back of her head with the same two pins she had used on the day Ruth had left. Her sleeves were rolled to the elbow. She was working the spoon through the mealie meal in the slow deliberate motion that was the motion of her hands at this work and at other work and had been the motion of her hands at this work for the twenty-five years of her marriage.

She did not look up.

'You walked.'

'From the Politsi turn-off. The lorry left an hour before I got in.'

'You should have telegraphed Mr van Rensburg.'

'I did not know to.'

'No.'

She went on with the spoon.

Ruth set her suitcase down by the door. She did not take her gloves off. She took her coat off and laid it across the back of the wooden chair that had been her chair as a child. Her mother had not turned her head. Her mother was working the spoon through the meal in the basin.

'Anna is on the stoep. She has had her tea.'

'Yes.'

'Your pa is up at the lower paddock with Willi. They will be down at one.'

'Yes.'

'There is bread on the dresser. There is butter in the cooler. There is the milk from this morning. The kettle is on. You should eat.'

'Thank you.'

That was the conversation in the kitchen.

Ruth went to the dresser. She cut a piece of the brown loaf with the bread-knife that lay across the board. She fetched the butter from the cooler in the corner. She made herself a cup of tea from the kettle on the range with the leaves in the brown teapot that had been the brown teapot in the kitchen for as long as she could remember. On the lower shelf of the dresser was the stack of green-covered exercise books her father had brought down from the Merensky Farm School in May for the long winter marking. The pencil was clipped to the top one in the way his pencils had always sat. The stack was a little lower than it had been in May. He worked at it on the stoep in the early evenings when the light was good and at the kitchen table at the supper hour in the small clean lamp he kept by his place. She sat at the chair that had been her chair and she ate the bread and butter slowly and she drank the tea, and her mother went on with the spoon through the basin without looking up, and the kitchen sat in the early-morning sun coming through the side window with the same yellow cloth on the dresser and the same blue jug on the shelf above the dresser and the same brass-rimmed clock on the wall above the door making the same dry tick-and-tick it had made since Ruth was three.

Her mother had not asked her how she was.

Her mother had not asked her about the train, or the city, or the university, or any of the things that, in a house that had not been this house, a mother would have asked a daughter who had been away for eighteen months and who had come up the long road from the Politsi turn-off on foot in the cold dawn of a June morning.

She had asked whether Ruth had walked. She had said where Anna was. She had said where Pa and Willi were. She had said where the bread was. She had not, in any of these sentences, used the word *Ruth*.

Ruth finished the tea. She washed the cup at the sink with the dishcloth that hung on the nail beside the sink. She set the cup on the rack. She went out through the kitchen door onto the stoep.


III

Anna was on the stoep on the wicker chair her father had bought her at the Pietersburg show in 1947, in a blanket over her knees and a cardigan over her shoulders, in the early sun on the side of the stoep that took the morning warmth before the sun came round. Her face was turned toward the bottlebrush at the lower edge of the stoep. The wooden stool Willi had made in his first woodwork class at Merensky stood beside the chair with her empty enamel tea cup on it. The cat sat on the stool beside the cup with its tail folded round its paws. She did not turn her face when Ruth came out.

She knew it was Ruth.

'You came.'

'I came.'

Anna held her hand out without turning her face.

Ruth took the hand. The hand was thinner than it had been in January eighteen months ago. The skin was paper-warm. Ruth knelt beside the wicker chair and held the hand in both of hers. Anna's face stayed turned to the bottlebrush.

'You are thinner.'

'I am ten.'

'You are not thinner because you are ten.'

'I am thinner because of June. June is always the worse one. The chest does not like the cold. Doctor Caldwell said so when he was last up.'

'When did they let you come home.'

'Saturday. They kept me eight days. Doctor Caldwell did not want to let me come, but Boet said he would drive twice a week into Pietersburg to fetch the medicine in the lorry. Boet has been very good. I did not have one in the night last night. I had one on Wednesday morning. The Wednesday is the day I usually have one, in a week when there has been one.'

She said all of this in the slow careful even register a child of ten might use to discuss the weather, because the rhythm of her own condition was the rhythm she had known for the half of her life she could remember.

'Was the one on Wednesday a bad one.'

'It was a long one. The one before it was the bad one. The one before it was the one that took me into the hospital.'

'How long was the long one on Wednesday.'

'Five minutes. Maybe a little more. The chest took an hour to come back, but it came back. Doctor Caldwell wrote it down in the book.'

'Anna.'

'I am all right. I am sitting on the stoep in the sun. The cat is on Willi's stool. You are here.'

She did not turn her face. Ruth held the hand in both of hers and did not let go and did not, at this moment, take her own gloves off, and the bottlebrush at the edge of the stoep moved in the small morning air, and the cat that had been her cat as a child got down from the stool and crossed the boards to a place where the sun had moved.

'I should have come sooner.'

'Yes,' Anna said. 'But you are here now. Pa said three weeks. I will have three weeks of you. Go and put your things in the room. Come back when you have washed. The sun is good here.'

She let go of Ruth's hand then. She turned her face toward Ruth for the first time and Ruth saw the marks of the eight days at the hospital on her sister's face: the thinness at the temples, the pallor, the small scar at the corner of the lower lip where Anna had bitten through during a seizure at some point in the spring; and behind all of these marks the same Anna, the same dark-grey eyes, the same steady level look that had been Anna's at four and at six and at eight and was Anna's now at ten.

'Go.'

Ruth went.


IV

Pa came in from the lower paddock at one with Willi behind him. Behind Willi at the kitchen door was Boet van Eden. Boet had been at the lower paddock with Pa and Willi since the morning and had been a friend of the family since before Willi was born and had had an eye on Ruth, quietly, for the four years since she had been fourteen and his eye had not in those four years been encouraged or refused. Pa was the same man. He was thinner in the face than he had been but he was the same man. He took Ruth's hand at the kitchen door and he held it for a longer second than he had held it on the platform at Pietersburg in January eighteen months ago and he said, 'Ruth,' and that was what he said. Willi behind him said *Ruth* in his careful boy's voice and held her by the elbow for a moment with the tight earnest grip of a brother of twelve who had not seen his sister in eighteen months. Boet stood at the kitchen door behind Pa and Willi and removed his hat and held it in his hand and said, 'Ruth,' once, in the slow even Afrikaans he had always used to her. He did not come in past the doorway. He inclined his head once to Lena across the kitchen and once to Pa and turned and went out to the lorry. They heard the engine start at the front of the house.

Pa, Willi and Ruth washed at the sink. Lena had set the table. The meal was the meal of a working day at Letaba: the cold mutton from the Sunday joint, the bread, the butter, a dish of stewed pumpkin, a jug of buttermilk. Anna came in from the stoep at her own pace and sat in her chair at the side of the table. Pa said grace. They ate.

The conversation at the table was the conversation of a working day. The lower paddock fence. The matter of the vlakte calf that had come down to the kraal with a swollen foreleg on the Tuesday. The price of the maize from the syndicate at Tzaneen. Boet's plan to take the lorry into Pietersburg on the Friday for the new alternator that had not yet come up from Cape Town on the goods train. Pa asked Ruth nothing about Cape Town. Willi asked her nothing about Cape Town; he was too taken up with the question of the alternator and with what part of it Boet would let him hold while Boet fitted it. Lena asked her nothing about Cape Town. Anna asked her, at one point, what the colour of the autumn light off the bay was at five in the afternoon, and Ruth said it was a flat dark grey with the breakers white at the foot of the granite, and Anna nodded, and that was all that was said about the city at the table.

Lena cleared at the end of the meal. Pa went back to the lower paddock. Willi went out to the front yard where Boet had taken the lorry round to the lower side of the house to look at the carburettor. Anna went back to the stoep.

Ruth did the washing-up at the sink with her mother. Her mother washed. Ruth dried. They did the washing-up together for the count of nineteen plates and saucers and cups and Ruth handed her mother each piece for the rinse and her mother handed each rinsed piece back to Ruth for the cloth. They did not speak. The brass-rimmed clock above the door made the same dry tick-and-tick.

When the washing-up was done her mother set the cloth on the rail and went to the back door without looking at Ruth and went out to the bottlebrush hedge to bring in the morning's washing from the line. Ruth folded the cloth on the rail. She did not, at the kitchen sink, take her gloves off.


V

She went to her room that evening at sundown.

The room was the same room. The iron bedstead with the white painted frame she had slept in since she was four. The wooden chair beside the bed with the chipped rail. The chest of drawers under the window with the lace runner across the top that her grandmother had made. The window opened onto the bottlebrush hedge. The bottlebrush in the late evening was beginning to take the deep red of full June. The light in the room was the long low light of the lowveld winter sundown, moving up the wall above the bed in the slow careful way the light had moved up the wall above her bed when she was nine and had lain looking at it.

She sat on the bed.

She had imagined the return as a wound reopening. She had imagined that the house would meet her with some new violence; that her mother's silence would have hardened, that her father would have receded, that Willi would have grown shy of her after eighteen months, that Anna would have weakened beyond the body Ruth could speak to. She had imagined the return as a meeting of the old house with the new self, and she had braced for what such a meeting would do.

It was stranger than that.

The wound had been there all along. The house had merely kept its shape around it. The mother who had not asked her name at the kitchen table this morning was the mother who had not come to the platform at Pietersburg in January of the previous year, and was the mother who had not, in the year before that or the year before that, sat down at the supper table with Ruth and asked her about her day at school. The father who had said *Ruth* once at the kitchen door and not the second time was the father who had stood on the Pietersburg platform and said the practical things and let the train go. The brother who had held her elbow for one second with the tight earnest grip of a child of twelve was the brother who had held her elbow for one second on every visit home from the convent at St Joseph's since he had been a boy of three and could reach up to the elbow. Anna who had not turned her face this morning, and had said *Pa said three weeks*, was the Anna who had sat on the stoep at four and at six and at eight and at ten now and had taken the rhythm of her own body at the rate the body gave it.

The eighteen months had not made the house. The eighteen months had made Ruth able to see the house.

She did not weep. She did not, in the room, take her gloves off. The light moved up the wall above the bed and reached the line of the picture rail and stopped at the line of the picture rail in the same way it had stopped at the line of the picture rail every June evening of her childhood.

The kitchen door closed at the back of the house. The voices of Pa and Willi came up from the lower stoep, with Boet's voice in low Afrikaans between them and the small bright laugh of a boy of twelve at something Boet had said about the carburettor. The sound of a kettle going on at the range came through the wall. The cat that had been her cat as a child made a small sound at the door of her room that the door, slightly ajar, let through.

She lay down on the bed.

She turned her face to the wall.

CHAPTER 21

The Gate


I

She had not packed on the Saturday evening.

She had sat with Anna on the stoep until the sun had gone behind the escarpment, and she had brought Anna in to the kitchen for the supper her mother had set out, and she had sat at the supper table in her chair and had eaten the cold meat and the bread without speaking unless spoken to, and Pa had said grace and then had said the practical things about the lower paddock and the price of the maize at the syndicate, and Boet who had stayed for the supper because of the early start in the morning had spoken about the alternator he had brought back from Pietersburg on the Friday, and Willi had asked Boet two careful questions about the alternator that Boet had answered as if Willi were the kind of person whose questions deserved being answered, and Lena had said where the bread was and where the butter was and had cleared her plates in the same sequence in which she had cleared them on every evening of the three weeks past. After the supper Anna had gone in to bed at her own pace and Ruth had sat with her on the edge of the bed for the count of fifteen minutes and Anna had said only *Goodnight* and the cat had come and gone. Ruth had gone to her own room and had sat at the iron bedstead and had not packed. She had not packed because there was almost nothing to pack. She had brought the suitcase and her two dresses and the Tibullus brown volume Clara had lent her in March and the writing case and a few books she was reading for the first weeks of the new term, and she had used the dresses and the writing case during the three weeks but had not opened the books and had not opened the Tibullus, and the things were where they had always been, and she had only to put them back into the suitcase, and she had not done it on the Saturday evening because she had wanted the room to be the room until the morning.

The Sunday came up over the bottlebrush at half past five in the same pale blue light as every other June morning in the Letaba valley. She woke without an alarm, in the same minute the kitchen door went on the latch downstairs, and she lay on the iron bedstead and looked at the line of the picture rail above her bed and listened to the small early sound of her mother's wooden spoon against the side of the enamel basin in the kitchen below.

She got up. She washed at the basin in her room. She dressed in the dark dress and the wool coat and put her gloves on. She packed the suitcase. The Tibullus, the writing case, the two dresses she would wear in Cape Town, the small toilet bag, the books she had not opened, the brown notebook with the scholarship code SC-BA-1955-0047 written on the front cover, the unposted letter to Pa which had ridden in the right pocket of her coat from the Wednesday in May at the Wale Street pillar box and which she had not, in the three weeks at the farm, given to him. The letter was a folded weight in the pocket of the coat. She put it in the suitcase under the writing case. She closed the suitcase. She set the suitcase by the door of the room.

The light came in at the window onto the lace runner on the chest of drawers. The bottlebrush moved in the small early air. She did not, in the room, take her gloves off.

She went down to the kitchen.


II

The breakfast was the breakfast it had always been. Mealie meal porridge. Bread. Butter. The brown teapot. The yellow cloth on the dresser. Lena was at the range. Pa was at the head of the table reading the *Pietersburg Northern Review* Boet had brought down on the Friday with the alternator. Willi was at the side of the table with his school satchel on the floor by his chair, sleepy in the way a boy of twelve is sleepy at six in the morning when he had not been the cause of the early hour. Anna was not yet down. Boet was outside warming the lorry.

Ruth sat in her chair. Lena set the porridge in front of her without looking at her. Pa said *Good morning*, which was what he said in English to anyone who came into the kitchen at the breakfast hour and had said in English for as long as Ruth had known him. Ruth said *Good morning*. Willi looked up from his porridge and said *Ruth* and went back to his porridge.

The conversation at the breakfast table was the conversation of a working day. Pa read out a paragraph from the paper about the rainfall figures for May at the Tzaneen station. Boet, coming in to the kitchen door with the cold of the morning still on his coat, said the rain on the lower paddock had been better than the figures suggested, but only because the wind had been off the escarpment. Pa said the wind would not hold past July. Pa spoke in the careful unforced English of an English-Scots farmer who had been thirty years in the Northern Transvaal and had never, in any of those years, taken the language of the place into his own mouth, the lowveld words for the rainfall and the kraal and the vlakte stitched into the sentence as borrowed nouns and the sentence shape always English. Boet answered him in the same English, his accent the slow even Afrikaans-shaped English of a young farmer of the lowveld who had been schooled at Merensky himself fifteen years before. Lena did not contribute. Lena worked at the range with the spoon in the basin.

Pa folded the paper. He drank the last of his coffee. He stood. He said, 'Half past eight, Boet,' through the kitchen door, and Boet's voice came back from the lorry yard, 'Ja,' and Pa went out through the kitchen door.

Willi finished his porridge. He took his cup to the sink and rinsed it. He stood at the kitchen door for a moment looking out at the yard where Boet was at the lorry, then went out to find Boet.

Boet came to the kitchen door at half past seven, his hat in his hand. 'I will take the lorry to the Politsi turn-off at ten,' he said to Ruth. 'You will catch the noon out of Pietersburg. The Cape train at five. Mr van Rensburg will telegraph the station from Tzaneen to hold the connection.'

'Yes.'

'Anna is up. She is dressed. She is on the stoep.'

'Yes.'

He looked at her for a longer second than the conversation required. He did not speak. He went out to the lorry.

Lena was still at the range. The kitchen was the kitchen with the brass-rimmed clock making the same dry tick-and-tick. Ruth sat at her chair with the cup of tea in her hand and the porridge bowl half-finished in front of her, and her mother stood at the range with her back to the table and the spoon in the basin, and the kitchen window above the dresser had the pale blue of the early morning still on it. The kitchen door was open six inches against the wedge.

Ruth set her cup down.

'Ma,' Ruth said.

It was the word she had used in the kitchen and at the supper table for as long as she had had the words. She had used it without thinking, eight times in the three weeks past, in the small practical exchanges that had formed the daily speech of the household. She used it now in the same register.

'I will write to Anna,' Ruth said. 'I will write to her every week from Cape Town. I will write a letter she can read aloud to Pa in the kitchen on the Sunday evening if she wants to. And I will send for her in the spring. The September. When the chest is better. I will take her down to the sea for a week. There is a doctor at Groote Schuur in Cape Town who is a friend of a girl I know, and he will see her. The chest is better at the coast in September. I have read it. Doctor Caldwell will know.'

Lena did not turn from the range. She set the spoon down across the rim of the basin. She wiped her hands on the apron. She turned.

She looked at Ruth for the first time in three weeks. Her eyes were the same grey eyes Ruth had known for nineteen years. They were not unkind. They were not kind. They were the eyes of a woman who had borne five children of whom three had lived and who had buried two and who had stood at the kitchen door of this house through a drought and a war and three winters of Anna's chest and who had not, in any of those events, asked the world for a softer arrangement than the one it had given her.

'Anna gaan nie see toe nie,' Lena said.

She said it in the slow even Afrikaans of the household, the language in which she had spoken to Ruth at the kitchen table for nineteen years and on the Pietersburg platform on the morning of the wagon to the rail stop and at every supper of Ruth's childhood.

'She would ...'

'Anna goes nowhere. Anna does not go to the sea. Anna does not go to Cape Town. Anna does not go to a doctor in Cape Town because a girl in Cape Town knows him. Anna stays here. The chest is the chest. The seizures are the seizures. We have known them since she was four. We have a doctor in Pietersburg who has known her since she was four. We do not need a Cape doctor.'

She did not raise her voice. She spoke as she had always spoken, in the slow even register of a woman who did not waste words.

'You write to Anna,' Lena said. 'You write to her if you want. The letter will sit on the dresser. I will not stop her from reading it. But you do not send for her. You do not take her anywhere. Anna is not yours.'

She paused.

She added, in the same even register, the second half of the sentence she had perhaps not meant to say, or had perhaps been waiting to say from the morning Ruth had walked up the drive between the avocado trees three weeks before.

'Dit is my huis. Nie joune nie.'

She said it in Afrikaans first. Then she said it in English, as if the English were the language in which Ruth would now have to hear it.

'This is my house. Not yours.'

She did not raise her voice for either sentence. She turned back to the range. She picked up the spoon. She put it back in the basin and worked it through the meal in the slow deliberate motion that was the motion of her hands at this work and at other work and had been the motion of her hands at this work for the twenty-five years of her marriage.

That was the conversation in the kitchen.

Ruth sat at the table with the cup of tea cold in her hand. The brass-rimmed clock made the same dry tick-and-tick. After a minute she stood. She did not say anything to her mother. She rinsed her cup at the sink. She set the cup on the rack. She went out through the kitchen door onto the stoep.


III

Anna was on the wicker chair in the early sun.

She had a cardigan over her shoulders and the brown blanket over her knees and the small enamel mug of milky tea on the boards beside the chair. The cat was at her feet. She did not turn her face when Ruth came out.

'You heard her,' Ruth said.

'I heard her.'

'I am sorry.'

'Do not be sorry. She is not wrong. She is not right either. She is what she is. You knew that before you came up.'

'I will write.'

'I know.'

'I will write every week.'

'I know.'

Ruth knelt at the side of the wicker chair. She took Anna's hand. The hand was the same hand. She held it in both of her gloved hands.

'Anna.'

'Yes.'

'I want you to listen to me. In September I will send the train fare. I will send it to Pa at the post office at Tzaneen. He will know what to do with it. If by then you want to come down to Cape Town for a week, Boet will bring you. Pa will let you come. I will write the letter to Pa before September so that he has had time to settle to the idea. Do you understand me.'

'Yes.'

'I will be in Cape Town. You will have an address for me. You will have it from me before September. I will write it on a card you can keep with the medicine bottles on the dresser shelf where you keep them.'

'Yes.'

'I am asking you to know that I am there. Do you understand me.'

Anna turned her face toward Ruth. She looked at her sister. The dark grey eyes were level.

'Ruth.'

'Yes.'

'I have always known you are there. Even when you were not in the house. That is not the question. The question is what you do with knowing I am here.'

Ruth did not answer. She held the hand. The cat at Anna's feet made a small adjustment of its tail on Willi's stool. The bottlebrush at the lower edge of the stoep moved in the small early air. Above the bottlebrush, beyond the kitchen garden, the lower paddock came up to the wire and beyond the wire the avocado trees stood in their dark even rows.

'Anna. If she does anything. If the chest goes badly. If the seizures change. If Doctor Caldwell says something you do not understand. You have Pa. You have Boet. You also have me. Pa or Boet can write. The post is three days to Cape Town. I will come.'

'Ruth.'

'Yes.'

'Stop.'

She said it kindly. She squeezed Ruth's gloved hand once and let it go.

'Go and do your scholarship. Read the books. Win the prizes. I will be here. The house will be the house. The chest will be the chest. The seizures will be the seizures. You go.'

Ruth stayed.

'Anna.'

'No, Ruth.'

'I am not arguing. I am saying I am not arguing.'

'I know what you are saying. You are saying you would like to make me different. I do not want to be different. The chest is the chest. The seizures are the seizures. They have been mine since I was four. I have learnt the rhythm of them in the years I have had them. The rhythm is a rhythm I know. It is not a rhythm Doctor Caldwell can change. It is not a rhythm a doctor in Cape Town would know better than Doctor Caldwell. I am ten and I will be eleven in October and I have learnt to live inside the rhythm. I do not want a sister to come and offer me a different one. I want a sister who will write to me at the dresser and let me write back. Now go.'

She did not turn her face.

Ruth stood. She bent and kissed Anna once on the temple, on the line of the dark hair where the eight days at the hospital had left the small shadow of pallor that had not yet gone. Anna did not turn her face.

Ruth went back into the house.


IV

She fetched her suitcase from her room. She brought her coat from the hook in the kitchen. She did not look at her mother at the range. Her mother did not look at her.

Pa was at the front of the lorry with Boet. He took Ruth's hand at the cab door. He held it for a longer second than he had held it on the day she had arrived. He said, 'Ruth.' He said that the new alternator was working well and that the road from the Politsi turn-off had been graded three weeks before. He said both things in the brief plain English he had always used for the practical matters of the farm. He did not say anything else. He stepped back. He raised his hand once to Boet. Boet put the lorry into gear.

Anna was on the stoep. She did not stand. She raised her hand once. Ruth raised her hand back through the open side of the cab. Willi was at the lower step of the stoep beside Anna's wicker chair, watching the lorry go. He raised his hand once too. Then the lorry took the bend in the drive and the stoep was no longer in the line of sight.

The lorry went down the long drive between the avocado trees and out the gate. The gate did not swing back. They turned west along the gravel road toward the Politsi turn-off.

For the first three miles they did not speak.

The road was the road she had walked down on the morning of her arrival. The kloof at the third mile was the same kloof. The bridge over the lower stream was the same bridge with the same rust on the railing. The morning was beginning to lift the mist off the valley behind them. The escarpment ahead was in full sun, the rock face very pale at this hour, the line of the upper road as a thin scratch above the lower farms.

Boet said, at the fourth mile, 'Your ma has been quieter than usual this year.'

'Yes.'

'I do not know what it is. Pa would know. Pa says nothing about it.'

'I know what it is.'

'I am not asking. I am only saying that the household has been quieter this year. Anna feels it. Anna does not say it but she feels it. The chest has been worse this year and I think it is part of that.'

'Yes.'

They drove for another mile.

'There was a letter for you,' Boet said. 'It came up from Pietersburg on the Friday when I went down for the alternator. From a Catherine MacLeod in Kenilworth. I put it on the dresser in the hall on the Friday evening. You did not see it.'

'No.'

'I have it here.'

He took one hand off the wheel and reached into the inside pocket of his coat and brought out a cream envelope and handed it across the cab. Ruth took it. The envelope was unopened. The hand on the front was the upright Edwardian hand of a woman of her father's generation, the address very precise, the words *Care of W. J. MacLeod* written above her own name.

'Catherine is Pa's first cousin,' Boet said. 'The English side. Lena does not have her in the house. Catherine has not been to the farm since before Willi was born. Pa has not been to her house in Kenilworth since before the war. They send the Christmas card. They do not see each other. She wrote to Pa two months ago to ask whether you were well. Pa did not answer. Pa does not write the English letters when he can help it. She has written to you herself this time.'

'You did not tell me.'

'No. I did not know what she would write to you. I thought I would let her say it herself.'

Ruth held the envelope. It was the same cream paper as the envelope she had addressed to Pa at the post box on Wale Street in May and had not posted and had brought north in her coat pocket and had now packed in her suitcase under the writing case. The paper was the paper of an English household. The hand was the hand of an English household. The address *Care of W. J. MacLeod* was the formal courtesy of a relative who did not know the daughter directly and was writing, for the first time, to ask.

She put the envelope in the inside pocket of her own coat. She did not open it.

'Thank you, Boet.'

'Yes.'


V

He set her down at the Politsi turn-off at twenty past ten.

The morning had risen fully. The escarpment behind them was in the high light. The road ahead went south through the lower farms and on to Pietersburg. There was no wind. The road dust sat where the lorry had left it.

She got out of the cab. Boet handed the suitcase down to her. He stood beside the cab in the heavy brown coat he had worn since he had come down from the lower paddock in the cold dawn. He was twenty-eight and the four years he had had an eye on Ruth had not lifted his hand from his side at any point in those years. He stood with his hands at his sides now. He did not put his hand on her shoulder.

She put her gloved hand in the right pocket of her coat. She drew out a folded envelope and held it for a moment in her hand.

'There was a letter I wrote to Pa in May,' she said. 'I did not post it. I brought it up in the coat. I have not opened it again. Will you give it to him.'

Boet took the letter. He looked at the address. He put it inside his coat without opening it.

'I will give it to him on the way back. I will not say anything to him about it. He will see it on the dresser tonight when he comes in from the lower paddock and he will know it was you.'

'Yes.'

'The Pretoria train will hold for the Tzaneen connection. Mr van Rensburg has telegraphed. You should be at Park Station by half past eight tomorrow night. The Cape train leaves at five tonight. You will be in Cape Town on the Tuesday morning.'

'Yes.'

'Take care.'

'I will.'

He looked at her for a longer second than the conversation required. The four years of his quiet eye were in the look and were also not in the look, because Boet had decided long ago that they would not be enacted unless Ruth one day asked them to be, and Ruth had not asked. He turned. He got into the lorry. He started the engine. He drove off east along the road back toward the farm.

She watched the lorry until it took the rise in the road and disappeared.

She stood at the Politsi turn-off with her suitcase at her feet and her coat collar turned up against the small wind that had begun on the road. The Catherine letter was in her inside coat pocket. The Cohen silk square was in the same pocket against the cream of her blouse. The Tibullus volume from Brownlow Road was in the suitcase. The letter she had written to Pa in May was now in Boet's coat going back to Letaba and would be set on the dresser in the front hall in the evening when Pa came in from the lower paddock and would be read by him in the kitchen of the house that was no longer hers.

She knew her mother had said the thing her mother had been waiting to say. She knew it had been said not kindly, not in a way that could save anyone. She knew her mother had said it once and would not say it again, and that the not-saying again was the same act as the saying once, and that the silence she had read for eighteen months as withholding had been, in fact, the only language her mother had had for what she had now found the one sentence to name.

She bent and picked up the suitcase.

She turned south.

She walked along the gravel of the Politsi road toward the rise where the lorry from Tzaneen would come for her.

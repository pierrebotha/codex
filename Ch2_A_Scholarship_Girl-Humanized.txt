# Chapter 2: A Scholarship Girl

The light came through the window at a different angle than she expected. The mountain blocked the east, so the morning arrived from the sea, a slow, salt-grey brightening that collected on the sandstone sill before it reached the room. Ruth lay still for a moment and listened. The pines outside moved with a sound unlike the wind she had known. This was coastal wind, purposeful and cold, with no interest in anything it found in its path.

She dressed without looking at herself in the mirror on the wardrobe door. The bathroom was at the end of the corridor, a white-tiled room that smelled of other women's soap and the particular chemical warmth of a boiler that had been running longer than any of its current occupants. Three girls stood at the basins, their voices carrying the easy cadence of people who have shared a morning routine before. They did not look at Ruth. She took the basin at the far end and kept her eyes on the water.

Breakfast was served in the dining hall from half past six. Ruth arrived at ten past and took a tray. The dining hall had a different quality in the morning. Less theatrical than the previous evening, more institutional. The smell was of porridge and industrial toast and the flat warmth of a room heated by bodies and not by design. She sat where she had sat the night before, at the periphery of the light, and ate without tasting. Across the room, two of the girls from the previous evening arrived together and moved to a table near the windows where the mountain was visible. They took the same seats they had taken at dinner.

She opened the scholarship folder and checked that every document was in its place: the offer of scholarship, the letter from the board, the matriculation certificate. She closed it again. It was a ritual she had performed since the envelope arrived in Pietersburg. She pressed her hand flat against the cover. The cardboard was slightly damp from the morning air.

She did not go back for her coat. She had left it on the chair with the intention of taking it, but at the top of the stairs she could see through the window that the sun had come off the ocean and the campus was in moving light. She adjusted the strap of the folder against her ribs and walked out.

The campus sat on the lower slopes of the mountain in the particular way that a thing belongs to a place it did not choose. The main buildings, the Jameson Hall with its Roman steps, the library's arched entrance, the old quad, ran along a contour line as if whoever had laid them out had been told to stay below a certain altitude. Above them the mountain went vertical almost immediately: scrub and sandstone and the pale flash of a kloof where the water ran in winter. Below them the city dropped in stages: the Observatory, the flats, the docks, and then the sea, a flat pale band at the end of every street that ran downhill. Ruth learnt this not from a map but from walking, triangulating position from the mountain behind and the ocean ahead, the way she had learnt to read the farm by the direction of the late shadow and the pitch of the drainage lines.

The path from the residence to the Registrar's office curved around the base of Jameson Hall and crossed the main quadrangle diagonally, the stone flags worn smooth at the centre where a generation of students had taken the shortest line. Ruth walked along the wall, which was slower, and counted the buildings she could name. Three. After three days, she had three buildings.

The Registrar's office was a forest of dark wood, green reading lamps, and the relentless, rhythmic clatter of typewriters. Ruth stood in a queue that snaked out of the door and onto the stone portico, her hands clasped over the folder that held her matriculation results and the letter from the scholarship board. The paper was slightly damp from the mountain mist that still clung to the ivy-covered walls of the quadrangle.

She had been standing for twenty minutes before the queue moved more than a body-length. The mist was still sitting in the ivy above the portico. A boy two places ahead was telling another about a summer spent on a fishing boat off Hermanus, his voice so satisfied with itself that the story seemed complete before it had finished. Ruth focused on the scholarship code printed in the top corner of the registration letter: SC-BA-1955-0047. The forty-seventh scholarship candidate in the Faculty of Arts for that year. She had not known there would be forty-six before her.

Around her, the air was bright with the easy, unthinking noise of transition. Students moved in clusters, their blazers crisp and smelling of cedar and expensive soap. Their laughter carried the unmistakable frequency of those who had never seen a drought or a debt, their belonging a habit as natural as breath. They spoke of summer holidays in Hermanus, of tennis matches, and of fathers who were judges or surgeons in the city.

Ruth felt the thinness of her own coat, its fabric weary from the farm and the convent. Her shoes were worn at the heels, the leather polished until it was a thin, translucent grey.

Behind her, a group of boys with polished leather shoes and voices that tasted of expensive tobacco discussed the coming rugby season. She did not look at them. She focused on the texture of the stone beneath her feet and the way the shadows of the ivy moved against the grey granite.

'Next,' a voice called from behind a frosted glass partition.

Ruth stepped forward into the registration hall. It was a cavern of trapped light and the smell of old ledgers, of paper that had absorbed a century of small authority. The clerks moved with a synchronised, bored efficiency, their fingers moving over the ledgers with a precision that had nothing to do with care.

The clerk was a small man with ink-stained cuffs and a face that seemed to have been carved out of the same grey stone as the university buildings. He took her papers without looking at her face, his gaze pausing for a fraction of a second on the lack of a private school crest on her blouse.

'MacLeod, R,' he murmured, his eyes scanning the scholarship code. 'First-year Bachelor of Arts. Latin I, Greek I, Ancient History, English I.' He paused, his pen hovering over the ledger. 'A full scholarship?'

'Yes,' Ruth said. Her throat felt tight, as if the word were too large to escape.

The clerk looked up then. A brief, speculative glance. She felt it move to the ink on her fingers, to the collar she had not straightened. He stamped the form with a heavy, final thud.

'Seminar block B, room twenty-two,' he said, sliding the papers back across the mahogany. 'Keep these on you at all times. The scholarship is contingent on attendance and performance. Do not lose them.'

Ruth took the folder. The stamp was still wet, a dark, purple bruise on the pristine white of the page. She walked out into the sunlight.

She found the lecture hall by following the stream of first-year students downhill from the quad and then up a separate staircase into the Humanities building, where the corridors smelled of old timber and the accumulated tobacco of a generation of men who had thought and argued in these rooms and left their scent behind. She was the first to sit down.

The first lecture was in a hall that smelled of dust, tobacco, and the faint, sweet rot of the sea. The tiered wooden benches were carved with the initials of a thousand boys who had gone to war and never come back to finish their degrees. A long, quiet record of the interrupted. Ruth sat in the front row, her notebook open, her pen poised. She did not look around. She did not want to see the way the other girls leaned into each other, whispering behind their hands, their eyes scanning the room for people they already knew.

Professor Arbuthnot entered with a swirl of black robes that carried the scent of old wool and pipe smoke. He was a man of sharp angles and a voice that had been shaped by years of speaking to rooms full of students who were not quite paying attention. He began with Agamemnon and the question of necessity, whether what the king did at Aulis was commanded or chosen, and he put it not as philosophy but as a problem of record, the kind that arises when the testimony conflicts and the judgement has already been carried out. He spoke without notes. He did not appear to expect questions.

His gaze swept across the rows of faces and stopped when it reached Ruth. She was the only one whose pen had not stopped moving, the only one whose spine was straight against the hard wood of the bench.

'Is that not so, Miss...?'

'MacLeod,' Ruth said. Her voice sounded louder than she intended in the vaulted hall.

'Miss MacLeod.' His eyes narrowed slightly behind his silver-framed spectacles. 'A name with the north in it. Perhaps you understand the concept of endurance better than most in this room.'

A ripple of low-level amusement moved through the benches behind her, a sound like dry leaves on a pavement. Ruth did not turn around. She felt the heat rising in her neck. She wrote faster. She did not look up. The north in her name was Pa's Scots, the surname he had carried into the lowveld and never let go of. The north in her was Lena's Afrikaans, the language Pa had not just heard. Arbuthnot had read only the surname.

Arbuthnot moved with a quick, performative energy, his hands tracing the lines of the text as if the very ink were a source of physical strength. He turned to the question of hubris, not as a character flaw, he said, but as a misreading of proportion, a failure to correctly measure the distance between a man and the gods. Then, very briefly, he appeared to glance toward the windows before correcting himself and returning to the page.

By the time the lecture ended, her fingers were cramped. She looked down at the last line she had written and found she could not parse it.

She walked out into the quadrangle, the wind hitting her with a solid, salt-tasting weight. A girl was waiting by the stone arches of the library, dark hair already loosening from its pins in the wind, watching Ruth with the attention of someone who had already decided something.

'The Lion of the Classics has bitten you, I see,' she said. Her voice was a bright, irreverent sound.

'He's thorough,' Ruth said.

'He's a warden of the past.' She scanned the quadrangle, her eyes moving over the signs without appearing to read them. 'I'm Judith. Judith Cohen. I was in the back row.' It was not an explanation. 'Come on. I'll show you where the good books, the ones not approved by the Registrar, are kept.'

The library entrance was set into the face of the building at an angle that required you to turn sideways under the arch to read the Latin inscription above the door. Judith did not look at it. Ruth read the inscription in full, parsing the genitive case without pause, before following her inside.

The interior was cool and smelled of old paper and the particular humid dust of a building that held more things than it let anyone see. The main reading room ran the length of the ground floor, its tall windows letting in a coastal light that fell flat and grey on the long tables. The students at the tables had the concentrated, absorbed quality of people who have learned to disappear into their work. Ruth felt her own breathing alter, slower, lower, as if the room demanded it.

'Down here,' Judith said.

She led Ruth past the reference cases and the issue desk and through a door marked PERIODICALS into a narrower section where the shelving ran closer together and the ceiling dropped by two feet. Here the smell changed: more acid, more vegetable, the particular odour of paper that had been damp and dried and damp again over a great deal of time.

'The approved reading list gets you as far as the main corridor,' Judith said. She ran one finger along a shelf. 'Latin primers. Greek primers. Approved editions.' She said the last two words in the tone of someone reading from a list they had not written. 'Arbuthnot recommends all of these. He has read all of them.' She paused. 'Do you know what a careful man fears?'

'The thing he doesn't include,' Ruth said.

Judith looked at her.

She moved to the far end of the shelving and crouched down. The bottom shelf held a row of journals, their spines facing out. She pulled one free and passed it up. It was a slim volume with a buff cover, printed at the University of the Witwatersrand: African Studies, Volume Eight. Ruth turned it over. The paper was cheap, the margins narrow. It had the look of a thing produced without institutional blessing.

'The Registrar does not approve of scholarship conducted north of a certain intellectual border,' Judith said. 'The Greeks are permitted because they are sufficiently dead. Contemporary research on the continent we actually inhabit is another matter.'

Ruth held the journal. The first article was titled: 'Migration Patterns and Cultural Continuity in the Northern Transvaal, 1890-1940'. She read the opening paragraph twice. The world it described, the escarpment, the seasonal movements, the pressure of the colonial land register on the patterns of ordinary life, was closer to the one she had looked at from the kitchen window every morning of her childhood than to anything she had been taught at school.

She handed the journal back carefully.

'You can borrow these,' Judith said. 'The librarian who manages this section takes her lunch on Tuesdays and Thursdays. Forty minutes precisely. The checkout system does not extend here.' She replaced the journal and straightened. 'I am not telling you to take anything. I am telling you how the room works.'

'How did you find this?' Ruth said.

'I followed someone who already knew,' Judith said. 'That is how most useful knowledge travels.' She dusted her palms on her skirt. 'Come on. I want to show you the eastward view from the top steps before the cloud comes back.'

She had two afternoon lectures. The first was Ancient History with a man named Brink who spoke with his back to the class, his chalk moving across the board in a fast small script that required Ruth to observe and transcribe simultaneously. He covered the Persian Wars in forty minutes without referring to notes. He did not pause for questions. When the bell rang he was still writing and did not turn round.

The second was Greek I, and the tutorial room was on the upper floor of the Humanities building in a corridor whose windows looked directly at the mountain's face. Ruth arrived three minutes early. Two other students were already present: a boy with a loose tie who was reading ahead in the text, and a girl with the careful posture Ruth recognised from St Joseph's, the convent's particular way of sitting, back straight, hands folded, the body performing an attention the face did not entirely confirm. The girl met Ruth's eyes and looked away, adjusting the position of her notebook with the small deliberate motion of someone who has already sorted the room into categories.

The tutorial lasted fifty minutes. They parsed four lines of Homer slowly, root by root, the tutor, a young lecturer named Henning who wore his academic gown over a brown jersey, correcting each attempt with the patient precision of a man who knows the right answer and is merely waiting for it to arrive. Ruth gave three correct responses without being asked and was not called on again. She wrote oikos in the margin of her notebook and drew a line around it. The Greek word for home contained, inside itself, the words for management and for law. She had not known that before today.

After the tutorial she walked back through the quadrangle alone. The afternoon wind had come fully in from the sea and the campus smelled of pine resin and the distant, salt-chemical tang of the ocean. The mountain above the buildings had gone into shadow, only the uppermost edge still catching the light, the rock pale against a sky beginning to go the colour of old iron. She did not know a single face in the moving stream of students past her. She counted the buildings she could identify: Jameson Hall, the library, the Beattie block. She counted the paths she knew by destination. Three. She had three days and three buildings and a fragment of Homer in the margin of her notebook.

When the afternoon lectures ended, the campus emptied itself in uneven streams. Students spilled down the stone stairs in pairs and clusters, their voices bright with weekend intention. Ruth crossed the road slowly, the scholarship folder held against her ribs, and stopped for a moment opposite Mostert's Mill. The windmill stood a little apart from the university's confidence, its old body pale against the slope, its presence so stubborn and ordinary that nobody seemed to look at it any more. Boys used it as a point of direction. Girls arranged to meet there and were late without apology. It had already entered the campus language as if it had always belonged to everyone else.

'Wait for me by the mill,' Judith had said after lunch. The omission caught Ruth more sharply than the instruction itself. On the farm, things were named in full. In the convent, everything had a title before it had a feeling. Here the local people she had known for less than a month were already speaking in abbreviations of belonging. The mountain. The mill. The hill.

She stood with the road at her back and looked up. The mountain was half in cloud, the white cloth stretched over the edge and lifting again in the wind. That was how the girls in residence spoke of it when they came in from supper or came back from town with their collars full of grit. Not Table Mountain. The mountain. As if there were no other one in the world worth specifying.

From higher up the slope, somewhere near Rhodes Memorial and the old zoo ground, a low sound travelled through the afternoon air. It came too slowly to be a tram and too deep to be traffic. She turned before she understood it. Another came after a pause, flattened by distance and wind, but unmistakable once it had entered the body. Lion. For a second she was back in the north, in the long dusk after heat, when the land seemed to listen to itself. The mountain, the city, the university, the stone road at her feet: all of it remained where it was. But the roar had opened something under it.

Judith arrived at a run, hatless.

'You heard them,' she said, following Ruth's face rather than her gaze.

'The lions?'

'On certain afternoons. Or at night when the wind swings. You get used to it.'

Ruth did not answer at once. Judith had already linked her arm through hers and turned them down toward the road.

'Come on,' Judith said. 'If you look at the mountain too long, it begins to think you're asking it for permission.'

Ruth went with her, carrying the unfinished sentence.

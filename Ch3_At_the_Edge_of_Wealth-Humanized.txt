# Chapter 3: At the Edge of Wealth

The invitation arrived in the form of a folded note, the paper thick and slightly cream-coloured, placed on the edge of the library table by Judith. It smelled of lavender, beeswax, and something sharper, more industrial. The scent of a city always under construction. The ink was a dark, saturated blue against the sepia dust of the Jagger Library.

'Friday night,' Judith had whispered, adjusting the emerald scarf around her neck. 'My mother says there's always enough for a scholarship girl who looks like she's starving for more than just Latin. Sea Point, seven o'clock. Don't be late or the soup will be a memory of itself.'

Ruth had sat for a long time after Judith left, her fingers tracing the edge of the note. The library's vaulted stone carried the muffled, reverent sound of turning pages and the faint acid of paper held too long in the dark. But the invitation in her hand felt unlike the brittle, institutional sheets of her scholarship reports. It had a texture and weight that belonged to a world where not everything was being counted. There was also, tucked at the bottom in a different hand, a smaller, older script that smelled of something faintly medical, a line from Judith's mother: 'Esther Goldman wrote ahead about you. We are glad you have come to Cape Town.'

Ruth read the line twice. Esther Goldman was the name Anna had said in the doctor's room at Pietersburg, the name that had moved between Lena and the doctor in a low careful Afrikaans Ruth had not been meant to follow. Esther had been at the platform also, a few feet behind William, her coat buttoned to the chin. Esther was the cousin whose letters Lena had read at the kitchen table without commenting on them and had then put in the second drawer of the dresser unanswered. Esther had, somehow, sent ahead to a household in Sea Point that Ruth had not yet known the name of, and the household had received the sending and had now sent back through a daughter at the Jagger Library a folded note on lavender paper. The geometry of the sending was a geometry Ruth had not yet learned to read.

She folded the note and placed it in the scholarship folder between her registration papers, where she would not lose it.

The preparation began in the communal bathroom of the residence, under high, yellowed ceilings and the relentless gurgle of plumbing. The air was thick with the scent of carbolic soap and damp stone. Ruth stood before one of the chipped ceramic basins, the water coming from the tap in a series of cold, metallic gasps. She washed her face slowly, trying to scrub the library's cedar-scented dust from her skin. The soap was hard and unforgiving, smelling of bleach, a sharp contrast to the lavender note tucked into her pocket. Around her, the other girls moved with a quick, unthinking energy, their voices echoing in the tiled cavern. They spoke of tennis matches at the Newlands courts and summer holidays in the whale-watching towns of the south, their ease as settled as the furniture.

Ruth stood at the edge of the mirrors and kept her eyes on the water. She remembered the thick, red dust of the Letaba valley, the sharp scent of the lye they used to scrub the convent shelves. She scrubbed her fingers until the skin turned a raw pink.

She looked at her reflection in the spotted mirror. Her eyes were too large for her face, her mouth pulled into a tight line. She adjusted her collar. The fabric of her blouse was stiff and unyielding from the laundry's heavy starching. Her hair refused to stay under her headband, escaping in dark, heavy curls that she tried, twice, to pin flat against her scalp.

The other girls had finished their preparations and had drifted out of the bathroom in their twos and threes, leaving the room to the sound of the cisterns refilling at the wall. The voice of one of the lemon-scarf girls from the third-floor corridor carried through the door from the upstairs landing, an unhurried account of a tennis afternoon at Newlands. Ruth heard the words without listening to them. She had not, in the seven weeks of the residence so far, learned the shape of the social weekend the residence assumed every girl had. The Friday-night dinner at Sea Point did not belong to that shape. It would have to be its own thing.

She lifted the soap and replaced it in its dish. She wrung the cloth at the edge of the basin and hung it on the rail above the radiator pipe to dry. She looked at her hands once more, the ink-stained second finger of her right hand and the small permanent rough patch at the side of the palm where the cardboard handle of her suitcase had been pressed against it for two days in January. The hands were a working girl's hands. The Sea Point dining room would, presumably, find this out.

'Going somewhere?'

The voice belonged to Miriam Sachs, the other scholarship girl from Room 310. She was leaning against the doorframe, wearing a blazer that looked too big for her. She offered a smile that carried no judgement, only a sharp, observant recognition.

'To a friend's house,' Ruth said. Her own voice sounded thin and metallic in the tiled room.

Miriam gave a short huff of a laugh, the Kaaps lilt catching in her voice. She wrung a damp flannel between her hands, watching the water drop into the basin. 'Sea Point? Or the Gardens? The socials got their own maps, hey. They live in silver and mahogany. Just make sure you know which door takes you home.'

Ruth didn't answer. She focused on the texture of the towel in her hand, the fabric rough and grey against her fingers.

She walked back to her room, the linoleum carrying each footstep further than she wanted. In the cramped confines of Room 412, the light was a soft, coastal silver pressing against the glass. She unpacked the emerald scarf Judith had lent her at the library that afternoon, the silk folded into a small precise rectangle inside its tissue paper, the kind of folding the Sea Point shop would have done at the counter when the scarf was first bought, perhaps a decade earlier and perhaps before. The silk was a deep emerald with a thread of darker green at the edge. It smelled of an expensive shop in the city, of lavender and cedar and something more particular underneath, the smell of a wardrobe in a household that did not have to think about how to keep its clothes. She draped it over her shoulders. The silk was cold against her skin and warmed slowly with the warmth of her neck.

She had not had word from William. She had written to him in November when she had reached the residence, a short careful letter that had said nothing of substance and had not asked anything she expected him to answer, and the reply had not come. She did not know whether the letter had reached him at the farm or whether it had stopped at the Mooketsi post office. She had not, in the seven weeks since, written again.

She checked her scholarship folder one last time. The university's blue crest looked back at her with its cold, institutional rigidity. She tucked it into her bag, felt the sharp cardboard corners against her palm, and stepped out into the mountain wind.

The carriage arrived with a rhythmic, industrial clatter that felt established and indifferent. Ruth sat by the window, her forehead pressed against the cool, vibrating glass. Her fingers were tight around the handle of her bag, the cardboard corners digging into her palm. She watched the way the light shifted from the high silver of the mountain to a more persistent, urban glare. The university hills, with their pines and pillars, began to give way to the packed terraces of the lower city.

She saw the signs then. *Europeans Only. Slegs Blankes.* They were small, white rectangles of polished enamel, fixed to the pillars. She had seen them at the station. She understood their logic. But on the tram, pressed close against the window with the Sea Point road already beginning to curve into view, they felt different from what they had been on the first day. Less startling. More settled.

A group of workers in blue overalls stood on the platform at the edge of the city. Their movements were efficient and self-contained. They didn't look at the passengers in the Whites Only car; they looked at the couplings and the oil caps. The conductor came through and punched her ticket and went on. A woman in the seat opposite Ruth was knitting a long pale piece of work whose pattern Ruth could not read at the speed of the woman's needles. The carriage smelt of the dry brass of the brake handle and the closed warm wool of late-afternoon coats.

As they rounded the docks and reached the coast at Sea Point, the air altered, losing the thin, brittle quality of the interior and gaining a weight, a dampness that smelled of salt, fish, and the peculiar, sweet rot of the tide. The Atlantic was a hammered silver mirror, the waves breaking against the grey granite of the promenade. The mountain was a stony witness at her back, its sheer face dark against the rising moon. The line of the Sea Point road came into view in stages: first the iron railings against the sea, then the row of white-fronted apartment buildings at the upper side of the road, then the smaller commercial premises at the lower end with their late-afternoon shopfronts shuttered against the salt.

She stepped off the tram. Everything was edges.

The wind reached her before the sea did. It came off the water with the force of something that had crossed a distance, and it carried with it the smell of open salt and the cold mineral dark of the Atlantic before any coast. The waves broke against the promenade railing with a deep, periodic percussion she could feel in the soles of her shoes. She stood for a moment at the tram stop, the hem of her coat pressing against the backs of her calves. The sea was not what she had expected. It was not contained or decorative. It simply ran out from the granite edge into the dark, and the dark ran on, and there was no visible end to either.

She walked toward the Cohen apartment building, the pavement sharp under her heels in the cold. The salt air was cold against her face. She passed a butcher's shop already shut for the Sabbath with the brown paper still on the inside of the door, and a small newsagent's at the corner with the *Cape Times* placard still in its wire frame on the pavement, and a dry-cleaner's with the lamp out at the front. The Friday afternoon had given way to the Friday evening at some point on the tram, in the way the late-autumn coast has of letting the day go without ceremony.

The apartment building was a structure of white, sun-bleached stone, four storeys high, with a small portico at the front and a brass plate beside the door listing the eight apartments by surname. *COHEN* was at the third floor, on the third line, in the upright lettering of a brass-plate engraver who had been at his work for forty years. Ruth ran her gloved finger across the lettering once before she opened the lobby door. The lobby smelled of polished brass and floor wax, carrying the same faint administrative sting as the university library, but warmer, lived-in, the wax laid over the traces of decades of private life rather than institutional order. A small Persian runner ran from the door to the lift. A side table at the wall held a copper bowl with three keys in it that the lobby's residents had set down for the porter to recover, and a small folded note in a hand Ruth did not recognise. The porter was not at his desk.

The lift was a mechanical, vibrating cage that rose with a series of rhythmic, metallic groans. The grille gate sat across the front of the cage and opened on a hinged arm; Ruth pulled the gate closed behind her and pressed the brass button marked III. The cage moved upward at the unhurried speed of a mechanism installed in 1923 and serviced once a year since. She adjusted the emerald scarf, her fingers trembling slightly against the silk. Through the grille she watched the floors slide past: the first floor with a pair of black umbrellas leaned against a closed door, the second floor with a small wreath of dried flowers on a wall hook, and then the third floor coming up into view, the door of the Cohen apartment already slightly open, the warm yellow light of the corridor visible through the gap.

Judith met her at the door. Her face was a map of quiet, professional composure, but her eyes were bright and unblinking. She wore a dress of dark silk that caught the light.

'You came,' Judith said, her voice dropping. 'I was beginning to think the mountain had decided to keep you. The wind is higher than usual today. It's trying to shake the trees out of their roots.'

She pulled Ruth into the hallway. The warmth was immediate and complete, the smell of it arriving before anything else: candles, food, beeswax over old wood, a hundred ordinary Fridays laid down in the air. Ruth stood with her hand still on the door frame for one second before she let herself look. The apartment was a world of heavy curtains and mahogany furniture. A grandfather clock measured the corridor with a relentless, rhythmic ticking. In the library the light had been distant and academic. Here it was golden, anchored by the warm glow of the candles.

The dining room was visible from the corridor. The table was laid for six, the white cloth pressed without a crease, the candlesticks burning at the centre. Mr Cohen sat at the head with his hands loosely folded, listening to something David was saying at his shoulder. Mrs Cohen stood at the sideboard with her back to the door, lifting a ladle with the economy of a woman who had done the Friday evening a thousand times. None of them had looked up. Ruth had perhaps three seconds in which the family existed at the candlelit table without knowing she was there. Then Judith said her name and the picture reorganised itself.

The air was still, carrying the scent of fried onions, roasted brisket, and the sharp, sweet smell of Sabbath wine. The floors were covered in Turkish carpets that muffled her footsteps. The room held the presence of things: paintings of European landscapes, silver bowls of fruit, books everywhere. Not the brittle, institutional volumes of the university library, but books with soft leather bindings and cracked spines that looked as if they were handled every day.

She saw a collection of poetry on a side table, its pages open to a verse she didn't recognise.

'Ruth, this is my mother, Mrs Cohen,' Judith said, as they reached the dining room. 'And my father, Mr Cohen. And my brother, David. He thinks the piano is a weapon.'

David was a man of quick, nervous energy, his hands always in motion. He looked at Ruth with a gaze that held no judgement in it, only curiosity. 'Judith says you're the one who can recite the Aeneid while the world is burning.'

Mrs Cohen was a woman of sharp angles. She greeted her with a brief, dry press of a hand. Her scent was a mixture of lavender and something dry and bookish. 'You're the one Judith mentions,' Mrs Cohen said. 'My husband's cousin Esther wrote about you too. She said to expect someone who reads everything in the room before sitting down.' She held Ruth's eye for just a moment. 'Come. The soup is getting cold, and we like our scholars well-fed before they argue with the Fates.'

Ruth followed her into the dining room. The heat hit her first, a dense wave of roasted meat and burning wax. Beneath the heat, behind the half-open service door at the far end of the room, the small persistent sounds of the kitchen carried. A pan setting down on a hot range. A tap running. The dry careful tread of someone moving between sink and stove. The smell was of the meat and the wax, but underneath was the thinner smell of carbolic and unrinsed dishwater, the smell of the room that the dining room was being held against.

She had been in the room for perhaps thirty seconds and she had not yet sat down and she had already understood the architecture of it. The dining room and the kitchen were not the same world. The gap in the service door was the only place where the two admitted their adjacency. Ruth understood, without being told, which side of the door she had been placed on.

Behind her, the door swung closed.

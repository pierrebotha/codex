# Chapter 3: At the Edge of Wealth

The invitation arrived in the form of a folded note, the paper thick and slightly cream-coloured, placed on the edge of the library table by Judith. It smelled of lavender, beeswax, and something sharper, more industrial — the scent of a city always under construction. The ink was a dark, saturated blue, the letters formed with a quick, purposeful grace that stood out against the sepia dust of the Jagger Library.

'Friday night,' Judith had whispered, adjusting the emerald scarf around her neck. 'My mother says there's always enough for a scholarship girl who looks like she's starving for more than just Latin. Sea Point, seven o'clock. Don't be late or the soup will be a memory of itself.'

Ruth had sat for a long time after Judith left, her fingers tracing the edge of the note. The library's vaulted stone carried the muffled, reverent sound of turning pages and the faint acid of paper held too long in the dark. But the invitation in her hand felt unlike the brittle, institutional sheets of her scholarship reports. It had a texture and weight that belonged to a world where not everything was being counted. There was also, tucked at the bottom in a different hand — a smaller, older script that smelled of something faintly medical — a line from Judith's mother: 'Esther Goldman wrote ahead about you. We are glad you have come to Cape Town.'

Ruth read the line twice. Then she folded the note and placed it in the scholarship folder between her registration papers, where she would not lose it.

The preparation began in the communal bathroom of the residence, under high, yellowed ceilings and the relentless gurgle of plumbing. The air was thick with the scent of carbolic soap and damp stone. Ruth stood before one of the chipped ceramic basins, the water coming from the tap in a series of cold, metallic gasps. She washed her face slowly, trying to scrub the library's cedar-scented dust from her skin. The soap was hard and unforgiving, smelling of bleach, a sharp contrast to the lavender note tucked into her pocket. Around her, the other girls moved with a quick, unthinking energy, their voices echoing in the tiled cavern. They spoke of tennis matches at the Newlands courts and summer holidays in the whale-watching towns of the south, their ease as settled as the furniture.

Ruth stood at the edge of the mirrors and kept her eyes on the water. She remembered the thick, red dust of the Letaba valley, the sharp scent of the lye they used to scrub the convent shelves. She scrubbed her fingers until the skin turned a raw pink.

She looked at her reflection in the spotted mirror. Her eyes were too large for her face, her mouth pulled into a tight line. She adjusted her collar. The fabric of her blouse was stiff and unyielding from the laundry's heavy starching. Her hair refused to stay under her headband, escaping in dark, heavy curls that she tried, twice, to pin flat against her scalp.

'Going somewhere?'

The voice belonged to Miriam Sachs, the other scholarship girl from Room 310. She was leaning against the doorframe, wearing a blazer that looked too big for her. She offered a smile that carried no judgement, only a sharp, observant recognition.

'To a friend's house,' Ruth said. Her own voice sounded thin and metallic in the tiled room.

Miriam gave a short huff of a laugh, the Kaaps lilt catching in her voice. She wrung a damp flannel between her hands, watching the water drop into the basin. 'Sea Point? Or the Gardens? The socials got their own maps, hey. They live in silver and mahogany. Just make sure you know which door takes you home.'

Ruth didn't answer. She focused on the texture of the towel in her hand, the fabric rough and grey against her fingers.

She walked back to her room, the linoleum carrying each footstep further than she wanted. In the cramped confines of Room 412, the light was a soft, coastal silver pressing against the glass. She unpacked the emerald scarf Judith had lent her. It smelled of an expensive shop in the city. She draped it over her shoulders. The silk was cold against her skin.

She had not had word from William.

She checked her scholarship folder one last time. The university's blue crest looked back at her with its cold, institutional rigidity. She tucked it into her bag, felt the sharp cardboard corners against her palm, and stepped out into the mountain wind.

The carriage arrived with a rhythmic, industrial clatter that felt established and indifferent. Ruth sat by the window, her forehead pressed against the cool, vibrating glass. Her fingers were tight around the handle of her bag, the cardboard corners digging into her palm. She watched the way the light shifted from the high silver of the mountain to a more persistent, urban glare. The university hills, with their pines and pillars, began to give way to the packed terraces of the lower city.

She saw the signs then. *Europeans Only. Slegs Blankes.* They were small, white rectangles of polished enamel, fixed to the pillars. She had seen them at the station. She understood their logic. But on the tram, pressed close against the window with the Sea Point road already beginning to curve into view, they felt different from what they had been on the first day — less startling, more settled, and that settling was itself a new kind of discomfort.

A group of workers in blue overalls stood on the platform at the edge of the city. Their movements were efficient and self-contained. They didn't look at the passengers in the Whites Only car; they looked at the couplings and the oil caps.

As they rounded the docks and reached the coast at Sea Point, the air altered, losing the thin, brittle quality of the interior and gaining a weight, a dampness that smelled of salt, fish, and the peculiar, sweet rot of the tide. The Atlantic was a hammered silver mirror, the waves breaking against the grey granite of the promenade. The mountain was a stony witness at her back, its sheer face dark against the rising moon.

She stepped off the tram. Everything was edges. She walked toward the Cohen apartment building, the pavement sharp under her heels in the cold. The salt air was cold against her face.

The apartment building was a structure of white, sun-bleached stone. The lobby smelled of polished brass and floor wax, carrying the same faint administrative sting as the university library, but warmer, lived-in, the wax laid over the traces of decades of private life rather than institutional order.

The lift was a mechanical, vibrating cage that rose with a series of rhythmic, metallic groans. She adjusted the emerald scarf, her fingers trembling slightly against the silk.

Judith met her at the door. Her face was a map of quiet, professional composure, but her eyes were bright and unblinking. She wore a dress of dark silk that caught the light.

'You came,' Judith said, her voice dropping. 'I was beginning to think the mountain had decided to keep you. The wind is higher than usual today. It's trying to shake the trees out of their roots.'

She pulled Ruth into the hallway. The warmth was immediate and complete, the smell of it arriving before anything else: candles, food, beeswax over old wood, a hundred ordinary Fridays laid down in the air. Ruth stood with her hand still on the door frame for one second before she let herself look. The apartment was a world of heavy curtains and mahogany furniture. A grandfather clock measured the corridor with a relentless, rhythmic ticking. In the library the light had been distant and academic. Here it was golden, anchored by the warm glow of the candles.

The air was still, carrying the scent of fried onions, roasted brisket, and the sharp, sweet smell of Sabbath wine. The floors were covered in Turkish carpets that muffled her footsteps. The room held the presence of things: paintings of European landscapes, silver bowls of fruit, books everywhere — not the brittle, institutional volumes of the university library, but books with soft leather bindings and cracked spines that looked as if they were handled every day.

She saw a collection of poetry on a side table, its pages open to a verse she didn't recognise.

'Ruth, this is my mother, Mrs Cohen,' Judith said, as they reached the dining room. 'And my father, Mr Cohen. And my brother, David. He thinks the piano is a weapon.'

David was a man of quick, nervous energy, his hands always in motion. He looked at Ruth with a gaze that was not a judgement, but a curiosity. 'Judith says you're the one who can recite the Aeneid while the world is burning.'

Mrs Cohen was a woman of sharp angles. She greeted her with a brief, dry press of a hand. Her scent was a mixture of lavender and something dry and bookish. 'You're the one Judith mentions,' Mrs Cohen said. 'My husband's cousin Esther wrote about you too — she said to expect someone who reads everything in the room before sitting down.' She held Ruth's eye for just a moment. 'Come. The soup is getting cold, and we like our scholars well-fed before they argue with the Fates.'

Ruth followed her into the dining room. The heat hit her first, a dense wave of roasted meat and burning wax. Behind her, the door swung closed.

# Chapter 4: The Girl in the Dining Room

The Cohen dining table was a vast, dark lake of polished mahogany that trapped the orange glow of the Sabbath candles in its surface. The heat from the candles was thick and cloying, carrying the heavy scent of melting beeswax that mixed with the smell of floor polish extending into the corridor.

Ruth sat at the far periphery of the light. She felt the coldness of the silver fork against her palm, its elaborate scrolling pressing into her skin. She had counted the utensils before her when she first sat down. Three forks on the left. Two knives and a heavy, rounded spoon on the right. Crystal glasses of varying heights clustered above the knives, their cut edges catching the light and throwing sharp, tiny prisms across the starched white linen.

It was a heat that did not invite her to soften. She adjusted the collar of her blouse. The fabric had been washed in the residence laundry sinks earlier that morning, treated with carbolic soap that left the cotton stiff and unyielding against her neck. It scratched against her throat every time she swallowed. Across the table, Judith leaned forward in a dress of dark, liquid silk, her movements entirely free of friction. Judith reached for her wine glass, her fingers curving around the crystal stem with a casual, unthinking entitlement that Ruth watched without looking away.

Ruth looked past Judith to the artwork hung in ornate gold frames along the far wall. The paintings depicted damp, grey European forests and pale rivers entirely foreign to the red laterite of the Letaba valley. The silence in the room was not empty; it was measured and expensive, broken only by the methodical ticking of a grandfather clock standing somewhere in the darkened hallway. The clock beat with a rhythmic consequence in the settled silence of the room.

Mr Cohen sat at the head of the table. He wore a heavy woollen waistcoat, though he had loosened his tie in a gesture of Friday night ease. His hands rested flat on the white linen tablecloth. They were large hands, the skin scrubbed raw at the knuckles. He stared at the flame of the nearest candle without speaking, his eyes narrowed slightly against the glare.

Next to him sat David, Judith's brother. He possessed a frantic, vibrating energy, his eyes moving constantly from the clock to the window. His fingers were in perpetual motion, tapping the rim of a water glass, shifting the alignment of his unused fish knife, smoothing the edge of his linen napkin against his knee.

Mrs Cohen sat opposite her husband, framed by the dark wood of the sideboard behind her. Her voice was dry, not unkind, a voice that had said many necessary things precisely. The scent of lavender came with her. Her grey hair was pinned immaculately to her scalp, glinting silver in the candlelight. 'We are eating,' she announced. It was not an observation but a command, cutting through the low hum of the radiogram playing a scratchy violin concerto in the corridor.

From beyond the swinging door at the far end of the dining room came the small persistent sounds of a kitchen at the working hour: the soft clatter of a ladle against the rim of a heavy stockpot, the hiss of fat hitting a hot iron pan, the brief cold note of water from the tap. The dining room held itself against these sounds the way a drawing-room holds itself against the weather outside, by polite refusal of acknowledgement. The sounds did not stop. They were the floor on which the candlelight stood.

The swinging door from the kitchen pushed open, exhaling a wave of heat into the dining room.

The first course arrived with a rush of steam. It was a golden yellow broth, clear and shimmering with fat, carrying the scent of dill and boiled bone. Floating within the wide porcelain bowls were pale, dense matzah balls, sitting like islands of resistance against the liquid. Ruth lifted her soup spoon, the heavy, rounded one on the far right. She tried to emulate the angle at which Judith was holding hers. The silver handle was cold, drawing the heat instantly from her fingers.

She took a slow, deliberate sip. The liquid seared the roof of her mouth, a sudden fierce heat followed by a deep, resonant sting of salt. The warmth spread heavily down her chest, relaxing the tight, defensive knot she had carried in her stomach since stepping off the tram in Sea Point. She ate with absolute precision. She ensured the bottom of the spoon did not scrape the porcelain bowl. She measured the angle of her wrist. Her elbows stayed two inches from the table edge, mimicking the geometry of Mrs Cohen's posture.

David broke a piece of bread with a sharp, fracturing snap that echoed unnaturally loud against the velvet curtains, making Ruth flinch. He pushed his soup bowl away, leaving the golden broth half-finished.

'Arbuthnot is reading Sophocles again next week,' David said. He picked up his knife, turning it over to watch the candlelight catch the silver edge. 'He spent forty minutes yesterday dissecting the metre of the chorus. Not a single word about what happens when the state actually demands a body. We spent an entire tutorial translating grief into hexameters while the military police lined up down the street.'

Mr Cohen continued eating. He broke his bread slowly, soaking it directly in the thick fat of the soup. He did not look up at his son.

'Arbuthnot is a scholar, David. His job is the preservation of the text. His job is not to govern the streets.'

'The streets are governing us,' David said. His voice dropped in register, taking on a tight, strained frequency that vibrated against the heavy silence of the room. 'They put up new signs at the central station this morning. White enamel with black lettering. Nailed right into the limestone pillars by the entrance. You have to walk around them to get to the platforms.'

He set his knife down. 'The parliament passed the Prohibition of Mixed Marriages Act yesterday. They are drafting the Group Areas zoning.' He stopped there. He reached for his wine glass instead of finishing.

She swallowed. The collar bit at the base of her throat. She remembered the white enamel boards she had seen on her first day. *Slegs Blankes. Europeans Only.* The glare of the afternoon sun hitting the polished metal, the physical gap in the air around the signs where people adjusted their pace to avoid contact. She focused on the rim of her soup bowl, watching a small bead of yellow fat slowly slide down the porcelain curve. She forced herself to swallow the soup.

'The Greeks understood that the law is a blunt instrument,' Mr Cohen murmured. 'Agamemnon thought the blood he spilled was a necessary toll. He thought the wind required it to sail to Troy. But the Fates do not care about the paperwork. They only care about the blood. These men in parliament, they think they can manage the wind with geography and categories. They think white stone and enamel signs will stop the sea.'

He looked back down at his hands, his fingers rubbing the raw skin of his knuckles in a repetitive, soothing motion. He did not raise his voice. 'You watch the signs, David. I watch the registry.' He stopped there.

Ruth sat utterly still, the stiff carbolic collar of her blouse cutting into her neck. She listened to the heavy ticking of the clock in the hall.

'You're quiet, Miss MacLeod,' Mrs Cohen said suddenly.

The older woman leaned forward, the lavender scent sweeping past the burning wax of the candles. Her eyes were sharp, pale, pinning Ruth against the hard wooden back of the dining chair. 'Judith tells me you are from the north. The Letaba mist-belt. They must view the city's changes very differently up there in the heat among the fruit farms.'

Ruth placed her spoon down on the silver underplate. The metal touched with a quiet, ringing sound. She felt the eyes of the table on her. She pulled her shoulders back, feeling the seams of the cheap residence blouse pull tight against her skin.

'The valley is very isolated, Mrs Cohen,' Ruth said. Her voice came out thinner than she intended. 'People there do not read the signs on the station platforms. They simply look at the colour of the soil.'

David smiled, a brief, sharp tightening of the mouth without warmth in it. He reached for his wine glass again, swirling the dark crimson liquid. 'A diplomatic answer. A scholarship answer. You learn quickly how to survive in the library, Ruth.'

The swinging door from the corridor pushed open again with a muffled breath of air.

A woman stepped into the light of the dining room. She wore a black cotton dress beneath a stark white apron, the starch so heavy that the fabric moved with the rigidity of cardboard, rustling against her legs. Her face was dark, lined deeply around the mouth, carrying the unmistakable topography of the Cape. She moved with a silent, practised efficiency, gathering the empty porcelain bowls and stacking them against her forearm with a balancing grace that suggested she had performed the ritual ten thousand times.

Ruth watched her hands. The skin across the woman's knuckles was rough, powdered with a fine grey ash.

'Sarah,' Mrs Cohen said, not turning her head from her conversation with David. 'We will need the water pitcher refilled. And bring the brisket.'

'Yes, Madam,' Sarah said.

Her voice was low, carrying a thick, rhythmic Kaaps lilt that immediately stripped the polite, clipped English fiction from the room.

Sarah moved to the sideboard and retrieved the heavy glass carafe. The glass was beaded with thick condensation, completely frosted with the cold. As she stepped behind Ruth's chair, the scent of the room shifted sharply. Beneath the roasted meat and the sweet wine, Ruth caught the metallic tang of an iron stove, the smell of cheap yellow soap, raw onions, and damp wool.

Sarah leaned over to pour the water. The heavy ice cubes clattered against the thin crystal glass. For a fraction of a second, Sarah's dark eyes met Ruth's. There was no subservience in the glance. There was only a rapid, assessing intelligence that calculated the cost of Ruth's stiff collar and her rigid spine.

Sarah leaned in closer, the icy carafe heavy in her hand. The cold radiating from the glass pressed against Ruth's bare arm.

'You the one who looks like the mountain is going to eat her,' Sarah whispered.

The words were almost inaudible beneath the sudden, loud scrape of David pushing his chair backward across the floorboards to adjust his seating.

Ruth flinched, the ice water splashing slightly over the rim of the crystal glass onto the pristine white linen. A tiny, dark circle of moisture opened on the tablecloth.

'I'm trying to hold it up,' Ruth whispered back, the words tearing out of her stiff throat before she could stop them.

Sarah paused in the middle of pouring. She gave a short, silent huff of a laugh, an exhalation that barely moved her shoulders. It was a ripple of unpolished amusement that transformed her lined face, softening the harsh set of her jaw and turning the deep lines around her mouth upward.

'Keep your back straight then,' Sarah murmured, stepping backward into the shadows near the sideboard, her white apron catching the dim light of the hall. 'Or the rock gets heavy for everyone.'

The main course arrived on a massive silver platter that required both of Sarah's hands to carry. It was a slow-cooked cut of brisket, the meat dark and glistening, falling away from the bone under its own weight. The gravy was thick with roasted onions and caramelised carrots, smelling intensely of woodsmoke and a sweet, dark earthiness that overpowered the lavender in the room completely.

Mr Cohen rose slightly from his chair. He carved the meat with a long, sharp knife, the blade sliding horizontally through the brisket with surgical ease. He laid the thick slabs onto the porcelain plates with precise, measured movements.

Ruth ate. She chewed the aggressively rich meat, feeling the sting of the horseradish paste hit the back of her sinuses. She kept her back straight against the wooden chair, remembering Sarah's words at the table. The conversation around her swelled again, moving away from the new apartheid laws and settling into a debate about the fluctuating price of wool imports and the cost of maintaining tennis courts in the coastal salt air.

The tall Sabbath candles burned steadily down into shallow pools of liquid orange wax, casting long, distorted shadows across the floral wallpaper.

By the time the fruit compote arrived, the cloves and bruised stewed apple filling Ruth's nostrils, her back ached from the effort of sitting straight. She set her spoon down more carefully than she needed to.

When the meal finally ended, the Cohens retired toward the sitting room, Judith leading the way with a rustle of heavy silk. The tall grandfather clock in the hall struck ten.

Ruth stood up. She excused herself, murmuring an apology about needing to collect her belongings from the entryway. She walked out into the narrow hallway, the sound of Judith playing a classical piece on the piano filtering through the heavy timber door of the sitting room. Ruth moved past the brass coat rack, seeing her own thin, worn woollen coat beside Judith's thick, fur-lined velvet cloaks.

She turned away from the coats. She walked to the swinging door at the end of the hall. She placed her palm flat against the wood, felt the heat radiating through the timber, and pushed it open.

The air in the kitchen stripped her immediately. It was unventilated and close, thick with the steam of boiling wash-water bubbling in a copper pot on the stove. The stench of brisket bones piled in a tin bucket by the back door mixed with the harsh sting of raw bleach.

Sarah sat on a low, unpainted wooden stool beside the cast-iron stove. Her white apron was unpinned and draped over an empty wooden chair. She wore a heavy, ragged jersey over her black dress, a garment that had been washed until the wool was pilled and grey. She was rolling a thin cigarette, her dark, lined fingers moving with unthinking precision, separating the tobacco flakes without looking at them.

In the harsh yellow light of the single bulb hanging from the peeling ceiling, Sarah looked smaller, physically hollowed out by the accumulated weight of the evening.

'You lost on your way to the piano?' Sarah asked. She did not look up from the tobacco leaf in her lap.

'I needed my coat,' Ruth said, letting the farm back into her voice.

Sarah looked up from the tobacco leaf. She gave Ruth one unhurried look, then looked back down. 'Your coat is back in the passage,' she said. 'Same rack it was on when you came in.' She picked up the tobacco flake. 'Come in and shut the door.'

Sarah struck a wooden match against the stone wall. The sulphur flared, sharp and stinging in the confined space, cutting through the smell of the boiling fat. She lit the cigarette, inhaling deeply, the thin white paper crackling in the quiet room. She exhaled a long stream of blue smoke toward the ceiling.

'The piano music is nice,' Sarah said. 'They play it when they want to forget that the wind is blowing outside. They think the heavy wood on the doors can keep the noise of the city out.'

Sarah gestured with the glowing tip of the cigarette toward an old, dented tin pot sitting on the back iron plates of the stove. 'There's tea in the pot, if you want it. It's not the silver service. Just old bags and milk.'

Ruth walked to the chipped tile counter. She found a heavy, discoloured ceramic mug and poured the dark, opaque liquid from the tin pot. It smelled strongly of condensed milk and cheap leaf that had been boiling for hours. She wrapped both her hands around the hot ceramic, letting the heat burn into her cold palms. She took a sip. It was aggressively sweet, thick and strong, tasting of labour and condensed milk.

'Mr Cohen was talking about the new signs at the central station,' Ruth said softly, staring into the dark surface of the tea.

Sarah laughed. It was a harsh sound, scraping deep in her throat, utterly devoid of the quiet amusement she had shown in the dining room. She tapped a cylinder of ash onto the stone floor beside her boot.

'Mr Cohen talks about the signs like they are a bad poem he was forced to read in school,' Sarah said. She stood up abruptly, moving to the deep concrete sink to scrape a thick layer of congealed fat off a massive iron roasting pan. The wire brush scraped against the metal, sending small flecks of grey fat flying against the white tiles of the splashback.

'They want to clear us out, you know,' Sarah said, her voice dropping into a tight, furious cadence that matched the rhythm of the scrubbing. 'They want to take Hanover Street. They want to move the fishermen and the tailors and the women out to the Cape Flats. Have you seen the Flats, girl? It is just sand and bush. It is nothing. They want to moer the whole district flat and put us out there where the wind never stops blowing, just so they can build more white stone houses on the slopes of the mountain like this one, looking at the sea.'

She stopped scrubbing. She dropped the wire brush into the murky wash-water. She turned her head, the harsh yellow light carving shadows beneath her eyes and along the sharp ridge of her cheekbone. The set of her shoulders dropped by a fraction.

'The mountain trams are freezing this time of night,' Sarah said.

She turned, wiped her wet hands on a grey rag hanging from the oven door, and reached out to the counter. She grabbed a small bundle wrapped in thick, translucent wax paper. She pushed it firmly into Ruth's hand. The package was heavy, and it smelled of salt and the roasted brisket.

'Take the meat,' Sarah said. 'The food they serve in those university residences is made for people without stomachs. You need real salt in your blood if you are going to survive the winter up on that hill. They teach you to think, but they don't teach you how to keep warm.'

'Thank you,' Ruth said. The wax paper was cold against her skin, the grease already beginning to seep through the folds.

'Wrap your collar,' Sarah said, turning back to the sink and plunging her hands into the water. 'The Atlantic wind is different from the wind up north. It doesn't get tired.'

Ruth stepped out of the Cohen apartment building and walked into the Sea Point night. The cold hit her cleanly after the kitchen's trapped heat, the salt smell immediate and sharp. She stood on the pavement for a moment, the wax paper package in both hands. The building behind her was still lit in the upper floors. She did not look back at it.

The return tram pulled away from the coast and turned back toward the pines. Through the glass, the lower city lay below her: the terraced streets and the flat-roofed houses packed close together, lights in individual windows going out as the hour ran late. She watched them until the tram turned and took them out of view. Then the high stone walls began, and the oak trees, and the silence of the upper suburbs.

She held the package in her lap the whole way up. The smell of the kitchen came through the wax paper. Bone and salt and the cheap soap Sarah had used on the pan.

She did not know Sarah's surname. She had not thought to ask it.

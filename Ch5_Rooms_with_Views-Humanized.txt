# Chapter 5: Rooms with Views

The day began in the claustrophobic silence of Room 412, a space that felt less like a bedroom and more like a high-altitude cell. The walls were an unpainted white that caught the first, bruised light of the morning and gave back nothing. Ruth lay still for a long time. Her breath came in a white plume that drifted toward the ceiling before vanishing into the freezing air. The room held no heater. The radiator, a series of rusted iron pipes below the window, was cold to the touch.

She watched the sash window. It was thick with the condensation of the mountain's breath, the glass holding a vertical slice of the morning mist that pressed against the granite foundations of General Botha Hall. Behind the mist, the wind was already at work, a low, persistent thrumming that felt like a vibration in the floorboards themselves. It was the Cape Doctor preparing its rounds, a wind that carried the salt-tasting weight of the Atlantic and the sharp, chemical bite of the hidden ocean.

Ruth shifted her weight. The narrow iron bedstead creaked with a sharp groan that echoed unnaturally loud in the silence. She reached for her woollen stockings, the fabric stiff and unyielding against her skin. She had washed them in the residence laundry sinks earlier that week, using the harsh yellow soap that left the wool pilled and stiff, the fibres matted against each other. She pulled them on, feeling the coldness of the fabric sink into her shins.

She dressed in the dark, her fingers moving with a bored efficiency that she had learned in the convent. She pulled on her salt-stained skirt. The fabric was heavy and smelled of woodsmoke and the Atlantic mist she had carried home from Sea Point the night before. This dress was one of two she had brought from the north, a garment that had been washed until the seams were white with stress. It was a piece of the Letaba valley that she wore like a second skin.

Beneath the bed, tucked behind her only pair of leather shoes, lay the cardboard suitcase. It was a cheap, battered object that required a specific, forceful twist of the wrists to stay closed. She reached for it, her fingers tracing the cold metal of the latches. Inside, beneath her Greek grammar and her scholarship folder, the wax-paper package of Sarah's brisket was tucked away.

She didn't open it. The scent came anyway: rendered bone and a deep saltiness that permeated the small room before she could decide what to do about it. It was the Cohens' kitchen pressing through the academic stillness of the hill. She pushed the suitcase further under the bed with her foot.

She stood up, her legs stiff from the cold. She picked up the scholarship folder from the desk.

She walked out of her room and into the long, uncarpeted corridor of the residence. The silence here was administered, a stillness that smelled of floor polish and the long sediment of other people's years. The walls were lined with closed doors, each one a boundary to a world of privilege that Ruth did not know how to imitate.

Ruth moved past the common room, where the morning light was struggling to penetrate the leaded glass windows. The air was thick with the scent of roasted coffee and the first chemical hint of rain. She saw the Warden, Miss Pryce, standing by the heavy oak sideboard. Miss Pryce was a woman somewhere between fifty and sixty, her iron-grey hair pinned into a configuration of absolute administrative certainty. She held a silver tea strainer in her hand as if it were a weapon, her gaze moving over the room with a clinical, neutral indifference.

Miss Pryce's eyes found Ruth. They were close-set and pale, carrying the measuring quality of someone who had already placed her in a category and was waiting to see if she confirmed it.

'The mountain is holding its breath today, Miss MacLeod,' the Warden said, her voice carrying the clipped, administrative warmth of a person who delivered threats as pastoral care.

Ruth nodded. Something tightened at the back of her neck. She felt the eyes of the other students turn toward her.

'It is, Miss Pryce,' Ruth said. The words arrived smaller than she had sent them.

Across the common room, near the window, a tall girl in a dark skirt and correct white blouse had been watching the exchange with the unhurried attention of someone for whom nothing in this room is surprising. She was fair, composed, her hair controlled without effort. When Ruth's eyes reached her she did not look away. She simply tilted her head a fraction, the smallest possible acknowledgment, and returned to her conversation. The girl beside her, brighter in manner, brown hair, quick eyes, caught Ruth's gaze for a beat longer before a smile replaced what she had actually been thinking. Neither of them had moved from their position at the window. They did not need to.

She moved to the sideboard and poured herself a mug of the residence tea. It was dark and opaque, smelling of condensed milk and a cheap, bitter leaf that had been boiling in a copper pot since five in the morning. She wrapped both her hands around the hot ceramic, letting the heat burn into her cold palms.

As she stood there, leaning her weight against the heavy wood of the sideboard, she watched the other girls. They moved with an easy, unthinking confidence that her own body, raised in the farm-bred silence of the north, did not know how to imitate. They sat in clusters, their blazers carrying the faint cedar of good wardrobes and the warmth of bodies that had never been cold for want of a coat. Their laughter was a bright, irreverent sound.

She set the mug down on the sideboard, the ceramic clicking bluntly against the wood. She walked out of the hall and into the sudden, shocking dampness of the mountain morning.

The path down to the tram stop was a country of shifting greys and the sudden, sharp scent of damp pines. The wind was whipping ferociously off the ridge, carrying a fine, salt-heavy mist that instantly damped the front of her coat.

The tram stop was an exposed concrete platform, a space of waiting that didn't acknowledge the mountain's cold. Ruth stood with her scholarship folder held against her ribs, the cardboard edges biting through the thin fabric. Around her, other students shifted their weight, their breaths coming in short, white plumes that vanished into the grey-white air.

The tram arrived out of the dark with a high, vibrating whine. It was an electric trackless tram, its motor a high-pitched drone that cut through the silence of the southern suburbs. Ruth climbed into the third-class car, the rubber-treaded steps shuddering violently beneath her boots as the heavy vehicle idled. She found an empty wooden bench near the back and sat shivering by the window.

The carriage smelled of old dust, ozone from the overhead lines, and the damp wool of her own coat. As the tram began its steep, grinding ascent toward the university, Ruth looked out through the glass. The harbour was visible below, the dark, hulking shapes of the cargo ships and the distant sweeping beam of the lighthouse cutting through the marine mist. There were men working under the arc lights by the water, figures hunched against the cold, loading crates before the day came. She watched them until the tram turned and the harbour disappeared behind the pines.

She watched the dark streets of the lower suburbs.

The transition into the Jagger Library was a movement from the visceral reality of the wind into a cathedral of high, vaulted ceilings and the muffled, reverent sound of turning pages. Each time the massive oak doors swung open, they exhaled a heavy, collective breath of beeswax and turpentine, the scent of a century of maintenance that felt like a physical layer against the skin.

Ruth crossed the main hall, her boots clicking with a sound that did not belong to the marble. She felt the eyes of the portraits lining the walls following her progress with the settled attention of men. She clicked past them.

The cold inside the library was different from the cold outside. It rose from the stone floors and settled into the marrow of her bones, a temperature that the building's prestige did not acknowledge. It was a sanctified cold, a stillness so deep that the scratch of a nib on paper or the distant, rhythmic thud of a closing volume sounded like a violation of the building's administrative peace.

Ruth found her carrel in the northern wing, tucked away near the stacks of medieval history. Carrel Number 42 was a small, wooden box of a space, the cedar sides scarred with the initials of students who had passed through decades before her. She slid into the hard wooden chair, feeling the familiar ridge of the desk-edge against her ribs.

She took out her Greek grammar. The leather spine was worn smooth by her own hands, its weight a comfort that her residence room did not provide. She placed it precisely in the centre of the blotter, ensuring the edges aligned with the faint graphite lines left by a previous occupant. To her left, the high arched window held a vertical slice of the mountain, the granite face of the Devil's Peak sheer and dark against the pale blue of the morning sky. The cloud-cloth was already beginning to spill over the ridge, a slow-motion avalanche of white mist that looked like the breath of the stone itself.

Behind her, in the light-filled central nave of the library, the socials occupied the long mahogany tables. They didn't arrive; they manifested, their presence announced by the scents of expensive, imported soap: lavender and sandalwood, with a sharp, coastal citrus note. Ruth could hear the occasional whisper, a silver pen clicking, the unthinking ease of their movements. They leaned into each other over their textbooks, their laughter carrying easily across the nave.

Miriam Sachs arrived with a sudden, silent rustle. She slid into the chair beside Ruth's carrel, her blazer too big for her and smelling of peppermint and old clothes. She set her volume of Roman Law on the desk with the careful deliberateness of someone who has learned to treat every surface as borrowed.

'The socials are at the table by the entrance again,' Miriam whispered, her voice dropping to the quick, flat rhythm of the lower suburbs. 'They've got a silver tea service today. Must be nice to have your history served with a side of cream.'

Ruth didn't answer. She watched a small bead of blue-black ink gather at the tip of her nib.

Miriam opened her book. Her hands were ink-stained too, Ruth noticed: the same two fingers, the same smear along the outer edge of the palm. The same person who held a pen for too long and pressed too hard.

'Room three-ten,' Miriam said, without looking up. 'If you need it.'

Ruth didn't ask what she meant. She understood it was an address, offered the way a person offers an exit: without pressure, without expectation. She pressed the nib to the page and began.

She spent the next hour deep in the stacks of the classical section, searching for a specific commentary on the Sophoclean choruses. The air here was thicker, carrying the dry, sweet rot of leather and the sharp, chemical sting of the glue used in the newer bindings. The library was a maze of these secret geographies, pockets of shadow where the maintenance staff didn't reach.

She found a leather-bound volume of Pindar, its spine cracked and revealing the grey, fibrous threads of the binding. As she pulled it from the shelf, a small piece of paper fluttered to the floor. It was a library slip, dated 1934, the ink faded to a soft brown. Knowledge is a warrant, a hand had scrawled across the back. Mind you do not let it become a cage.

Ruth held the slip between her thumb and forefinger. The paper was brittle, feeling like a dried leaf about to crumble. She looked at the handwriting: quick and urgent, stripped of the polite flourishes she had been taught in the convent. The English was English, but the rhythm of the second sentence carried the slow careful Afrikaans imperative an Afrikaans-speaking child would have brought into English without thinking. Someone in 1934 had been raised in a house like Ruth's, with a mother who spoke Afrikaans and a father who taught English at a school like Merensky, and had left the message at the back of a Pindar. It was a voice from the dead, a message sent from a time before the enamel signs and the registry boards had drawn their lines through the city.

She tucked the slip into her pocket. She felt the sharp, cold edge of it against her thigh.

Judith Cohen arrived at a run, hatless, her fair hair loosening in the wind. Ruth reached into her coat pocket without thinking and held out the silk scarf. Judith took it without breaking stride, wound it around her neck. The lavender-and-cedar scent was fainter than Ruth remembered it. Neither of them mentioned it.

'You're making a monument of yourself again, Ruth,' Judith whispered.

Ruth stood up, her legs stiff. She packed her books with a clinical, administrative efficiency. They walked out of the library and into the sudden, shocking glare of the mountain sun. The wind was coming off the ridge in high, vibrating gasps. The Cape Doctor was at work, a ferocious south-easter that carried a fine, salt-heavy mist.

They walked along the stone portico, the granite arches creating both theatre and exclusion simultaneously. Ruth noticed how the ivy moved against the grey stone.

To their left, the Rhodes Memorial stood on the slope, its dark stone pillars rising above the scrub. At its base, the eight granite lions crouched in permanent forward motion, their open mouths fixed on the lower city.

'The Lawrences think you're one of them, Ruth,' Judith whispered as they passed a group of boys with polished leather shoes. 'A bit provincial, perhaps, but of the right stock. You've got the handshake down. The way you hold that folder... it's a performance of proportion.'

Ruth looked at her own hands, stained with the persistent, dark ink of her notes. She saw the signs then. Europeans Only. Slegs Blankes. Small white rectangles of polished enamel, fixed to the pillars with clinical precision. She stopped walking. Judith, a step ahead, did not notice.

The signs were at eye level, which she had not expected. She had imagined them higher, official and architectural, at a distance that could be read and moved past. These were at the height of a face. She stood still for a moment and read them again. Europeans Only. The enamel was very clean. It caught the afternoon light the way a mirror does, giving back nothing of its own.

She had seen the same signs on the station platform at Pietersburg, on the doors of the post office, on the benches along the Main Road. She had filed them the way a person files a sound they have heard so many times it no longer startles. To see them between the ivy, on the stone of the university, was different from knowing. The university had a different grammar from the post office.

It was not.

As they reached the Arts block, the air altered, losing the sharp, coastal glare and gaining a soft, silvered weight. Its high, granite arches and the vast, soot-stained roof created a cathedral of transit.

The seminar room was a space of sharp angles and the dry, academic weight of Professor Arbuthnot's robes. He was already at the table, his pen moving down the margin at speed, his body angled toward the text as if the room were interrupting him. He looked up as they entered, his silver-framed spectacles catching the light in a way that made him look like a blind prophet from one of his own plays.

'Miss MacLeod,' he said, his voice a deep, resonant rumble that cut through the low hum of the students. 'The name with the north in it. Perhaps today you can help us understand the tragedy of the unburied.'

Ruth sat at the end of the long wooden table, her hands folded in her lap. She felt the eyes of the other students turning toward her with a neutral, measuring curiosity. Arbuthnot opened his book, the heavy paper crackling in the silence.

'We are discussing the stasimon,' he announced. 'The ode to man's mastery over the elements. Antigone's commitment to the divine law of her family clashes with the state's rigid, administrative order. Is she a hero of the spirit, or a subversion of the state's stable geography?'

He paused, his eyes moving across the room. He stopped when he reached Ruth. 'What do you think, Miss MacLeod? Does the state have the right to define the boundary between the remembered and the erased?'

Ruth felt the heat rising in her neck. She thought of the enamel signs on the station platforms. She thought of Sarah, scrubbing the roasting pan in the unventilated heat of the Cohens' kitchen.

'I think the state's logic is a language of the dead,' Ruth said.

Her own voice sounded thin, metallic, stripped entirely of its usual resonance. The room fell into a sudden, deep silence. Even Judith stopped her nervous fidgeting with her scarf.

'It uses the grammar of order to justify a violence the gods do not recognise,' Ruth continued, her voice gaining a hard, rhythmic intensity. 'Antigone isn't subverting the state; she's revealing its lack of proportion. Her hunger for the truth of her brother's life is more permanent than Creon's edicts.'

Something shifted in the room. Arbuthnot's eyes narrowed in a fierce, sudden clarity.

'A dangerous interpretation,' he murmured. 'You speak of the proportion as if it were a thing that could be felt. But in the university, we are trained to parse the logic, Miss MacLeod. Not the hunger.'

'And if the logic is a machinery of control?' Ruth asked.

Arbuthnot closed his book with a heavy, final thud. The sound echoed violently against the high walls of the seminar room. 'Then you must be very thorough with your translations. The state does not forgive a lack of attention. It also does not forgive those who try to name the dark grammar of its own history.'

The seminar continued for another hour, the students debating the metre of the chorus and the legalities of Creon's edicts. Ruth felt the physical discomfort of the wooden chair, the ridge of the table edge pressing into her chest. One of the other students, a boy with leather patches on his blazer elbows, argued that Antigone was a purely private actor, her actions as irrelevant to the state as a library book that had never been checked out.

Ruth listened, her teeth together. The high windows had gone dark while she was not looking, the glass holding the dim interior of the classroom back at itself.

When the session finally ended, the students spilled out into the quadrangle. The air carried roasted coffee and, beneath it, the ozone edge of rain building off the ridge. Judith walked beside her, her steps quick and purposeful.

'You're going to get yourself noticed,' Judith said. 'And not in the way the scholarship board intended. They want a girl who can parse the Latin, Ruth. Not a girl who can see the partition.'

'I can't help what I see,' Ruth said.

'Good,' Judith said, her eyes sharp and unblinking. 'Because if you stop seeing it, you start belonging to it.'

The afternoon light was failing, the high silver glare of the morning replaced by a bruised purple the mountain absorbed without effort. Judith stopped at the edge of the stone portico, the wind whipping her emerald scarf around her neck.

'Come on,' Judith said. 'I'm going to the Union. They're talking about the new housing laws. It's a different kind of grammar, but it's one you need to learn if you're going to survive this mountain.'

Ruth stood still for a moment, watching Judith move away toward the student union building. The wind came off the ridge in a long, sustained note.

She thought of what was in her bag. Sarah's meat, wrapped in wax-paper, going cold now, going cold since she had pressed it against her ribs all morning on the tram up the hill. She thought of Sarah's kitchen: the trapped smoke and depleted air. The grey rag on the oven hook. The ash on the stone floor beside Sarah's boot.

She could not say this to Judith. The words had not arrived. But her body already knew where it was going. She looked up at the mountain, the granite face dark and unyielding against the failing light, and turned in the opposite direction, toward the university gates, leaning her weight fully forward into the freezing wind.

The tram was nearly empty when it came. Ruth climbed into the third-class car and found a seat near the back. The carriage smelled of wet wool and old tobacco and the particular exhaustion of a vehicle at the end of its day.

She opened her bag and reached for the package of brisket. The wax-paper was cold against her palm. She peeled back the folds. The meat was dark, glistening with fat, carrying the scent of the Cohens' kitchen: woodsmoke and rendered fat, a saltiness that made her mouth water.

She took a small bite. The fat melted on her tongue, a sudden heat followed by the deep sting of salt. She chewed slowly, the texture of the meat rough and satisfying. She thought of Sarah's hands pressing the parcel into hers. She did not think of anything else.

Outside the tram windows, the lower suburbs moved past in the dark: stone walls, wrought-iron gates, the black canopies of ancient oaks closing over the road. The packed terraces vanished, replaced by the silence of the affluent Southern Suburbs.

By the time the tram reached the residence stop, the package of meat was gone. She folded the grease-stained wax-paper carefully and tucked it into her coat pocket. She stepped off the tram and into the freezing mountain air.

The pines moved overhead in the dark. The path was uneven where the residence light did not reach. The building stood on the slope, its granite foundations dark and unyielding against the black sky. Ruth walked toward the heavy oak doors, her boots clicking against the stone.

Inside Room 412, she did not undress immediately. She stood by the sash window, her breath a white plume against the dark glass. The wind was still working on the other side. The mountain was still there. In her coat pocket, the grease-stained wax-paper and the library slip lay together. On the desk a letter had been left against the Greek grammar. Willi's handwriting, the careful round hand of a boy of eleven who had been taught at Merensky to give the descenders their proper length. She had not opened it. She pressed her fingers against the cold glass.

# Chapter 6: The Jewish Table

She left the university in the long hour before the light failed. The mountain's shadow was already across the upper streets, pressing the warmth out of the stone, when she came through the residence gates with her scholarship folder under her arm and the south-easter following her off the ridge. At the tram stop, two of the Humanities girls waited with the particular patience of people who had somewhere comfortable to go. Ruth stood apart from them. The tram came whining up from the lower streets, its overhead lines hissing in the damp air, and she climbed into the third-class section and found a window seat and watched the university's stone buildings disappear behind the pines.

The descent was a reliable loosening. Her shoulders dropped by a degree somewhere between the Observatory and the docks. Her jaw unclenched. She stopped performing the constant small calculation she performed in every public space on the hill. Which register. Which posture. Which version of a sentence before she committed it.

The lower city made its familiar sequence past the window. The terraced streets first, with their iron balconies and wash-lines and the specific, dense quality of lives arranged to use every inch of available space. Then the docks, the dark hulls of freight ships under the arc lights, the figures on the quayside moving in the long, practised patterns of men who had been doing this in every weather. Then the coast road, and the sea, wide and dark and carrying the last of the afternoon light on its surface, hammered flat and pewter-coloured by the wind that had been working on it all day.

A woman in the seat ahead managed a child on one knee and a basket on the other. The basket smelled of turmeric and something dried and sharp. She adjusted the child's coat with one hand without looking at it. Ruth watched her wrist until the tram rounded the headland and the figures on the pavement changed.

She got off at the stop below the Cohens' street and walked the two blocks, her heels sharp on the pavement. The sea was three streets west and audible: a low, rhythmic dispute with the granite of the promenade. She had not needed to check the address since the third Friday.

The Cohens' building was white-walled and fronted with polished brass. The lobby smelled of beeswax and old stone and the layered history of private life laid down in successive coats over the floor. She had learned to distinguish this beeswax from the university library's beeswax. The library's was the smell of preservation: things kept exactly as they had been. This was different. This was the smell of a place where the floors were polished because people lived on them.

The lift groaned upward in its brass cage. She adjusted her collar in the reflection of the door, looked at her own face for one second, and then the floor moved under her and the image tilted away.

Judith met her in the doorway. Sleeves rolled, the dishcloth over one shoulder, her hair already losing its pins.

'You're early tonight,' she said.

'The tram was fast.'

'Trams are never fast. You were early leaving.' She took the silk scarf Ruth held out and wound it round her own neck. Ruth had been carrying it since the previous Friday, meaning to return it, and had remembered it each morning and left anyway without it, until today when she had finally taken it from the desk hook and put it in her coat pocket before she could forget again. 'Come in. David is at the piano and my mother has set the table herself because Sarah is managing the brisket and apparently these two things cannot both be supervised at the same time.'

Ruth followed her into the hallway. The apartment gathered itself around her in the way she recognised now: the Turkish carpet underfoot, the candles lit in the corridor despite the last light still showing through the drawing room window, the grandfather clock counting at the far end with its quiet, serious authority. The brisket was already in the air from the end of the hall, the slow dark smell of the fat and the woodsmoke and the onions that had been cooking since the afternoon. Books were everywhere, as they always were. Leather spines cracked from use. A collection of poetry on the side table with a dried leaf pressed between pages a third of the way through, as if someone had been reading it that afternoon and fully intended to return.

Through the drawing room wall, the piano.

She stopped in the hallway. She knew Chopin from the gramophone records in the residence common room, knew the shape of this étude well enough to hear what was being done to it. The piece had a built-in flow, a controlled interior-weather quality that the composition carried. What she was hearing had the shape of it and the feeling of something else. The phrases came out uneven, the right hand maintaining precision while the left drove at the keys with a force they had not been asked for, so the whole thing sounded less like a piece of music and more like two separate arguments happening in the same room at the same time.

She went through.

David was at the piano in his jacket, tie pulled loose, shoulders narrow and taut. Ink-stained fingers on the keys. He did not register her arrival, or registered it and continued anyway, driving through the phrase to its end and then sitting back abruptly and letting his hands fall to his knees.

'You play like you're cross with it,' Ruth said.

He turned. The tiredness she had expected; it was a November evening in the final weeks of term. The redness at the rims of his eyes she had not.

'Mine generally refuse,' she added, and took the chair by the window.

He looked at the keyboard. His fingers lay on his thighs very still. 'They put up new signs at the university entrance this morning. Not at the station. Not at the post office. De Waal Drive. The entrance to the university grounds.' He picked up the fountain pen from the piano lid and set it down again. 'Four of them. New posts already in the ground. They must have done it overnight.'

Ruth looked at the sea through the glass. The last light was going off it, the surface turning from hammered silver to slate.

'Arbuthnot spent the afternoon on the metre of the Antigone chorus,' David said. 'The ode to man's mastery over the elements. I was in the second row and I could see through the window that two men were measuring the pavement outside Beattie block with stakes and a tape. For the next set of posts.' He paused. 'He did not look up once. He got through the entire ode. Man's mastery. Every line of it.'

The pen turned over on the lid of the piano. Once, twice.

'A student I know is photographing every placement,' he said. 'He has forty-seven photographs. He has nowhere to send them that will make any difference before Monday when they put up the next ones.' He moved his hand from the pen. 'The petition is at three hundred and forty signatures. Someone in the Registrar's office told Marcus it would need five thousand to be considered. Five thousand.' He looked at the keys. 'Do the mathematics on that.'

Ruth said nothing. The clock in the hall ticked. The wind pressed at the glass in a long, flat note.

'So the piano is the argument that doesn't go into the minutes,' she said.

He gave a short sound with no humour in it. 'Something like that.' He ran one chord, a minor chord, very quiet, and let it fade in the room. 'Come on. My mother will have sent my father to find us already.'

The dining table held the candles and the silver and the white linen and the four place settings Ruth now knew as well as the page layout of her Greek grammar: Mr Cohen at the head, Mrs Cohen at the foot, David beside his father, Ruth and Judith opposite them. The mahogany was deep and dark and returned the candle flames multiplied, small orange lights scattered across the polished surface, trembling when someone reached across the table. Ruth sat down and picked up her knife. Solid, cold, exact in her hand.

Dinner at the Cohens' had its structure. The blessing came first, then the soup, then the main course, then the debate. Mrs Cohen moved through it with an authority that was not the authority of the Warden presiding over the residence dining hall, who held order for its own sake. Mrs Cohen held order because she had decided the table was worth the effort of a world, and she was protecting the effort.

Sarah brought the soup.

She came through the kitchen door with the bowls balanced along her forearm, the steam rising from the golden broth in thin, wavering columns. Ruth watched her hands as she set each bowl down: the skin rough at the knuckles, a small cut on the ring finger that had not been there two Fridays ago. She set the bowls without spilling, retrieved the serving tray, and went back through the door without a word, her face a level evenness that moved through the room without settling on anything in it.

The soup was salt-heavy and deep, the matzah balls dense and warm at their edges and cool at their centres. Ruth ate.

'Samuel Weiss tells me they have begun the ancestry verification at the central registry,' Mr Cohen said. He was looking at his bread. 'Not just births and deaths. Three generations back on both sides.'

Mrs Cohen's spoon paused above her bowl.

'Weiss is a lawyer,' David said. 'He'll be busy.'

'He will,' Mr Cohen agreed. He soaked his bread in the fat of the broth, the bread softening and darkening in the liquid. 'The question is what a lawyer does when the law is the problem.'

'He still charges by the hour,' David said.

Mr Cohen looked at his son for a moment. Then back at his soup. 'He still charges by the hour.'

The clock in the hall dropped a quarter-hour. A wind gust hit the windows with the flat, soft sound of a hand on cloth and then was gone.

'The university has not made a statement,' David said. The particular flatness in his voice. 'Arbuthnot will quote the necessity of Aulis. Brink will cover the Persian Wars. The whole institution will remain very thorough about things that stopped mattering two thousand years ago and say nothing about the signs they installed in its own grounds this morning.'

'The university was built to turn the past into credentials,' Mr Cohen said carefully. 'The bureaucracy is the point. The paper is the point. By the time the legislation arrives with its full clarity, it has already been operational for three years in practice. The signs at the entrance are the announcement that the mechanism is already complete.' He rubbed the back of one hand with his thumb, the skin pale where the pressure was. 'You watch the signs. I watch the registry.'

The silence that followed had the texture of a subject that had been handled many times before and set down carefully.

Mrs Cohen looked across the table at Ruth. 'Judith tells me you spoke in Arbuthnot's seminar this week.'

'She told the room the state's logic was a language of the dead,' Judith said. 'With his silver spectacles on her and Stratford-Reid in the front row with her fountain pen.'

'And what did he say?' Mrs Cohen asked. She asked it of Ruth directly.

'He told me to be thorough with my translations.'

Mrs Cohen turned her wine glass slowly by the stem. The candlelight moved in the crystal, a small, continuous shifting. 'Arbuthnot is a careful man,' she said. 'There is a difference between careful and cowardly. He knows which one he is. Whether you do will depend on which one he chooses when choosing is required.' She held Ruth's eye for a moment, then set the glass down. 'Eat. You are too thin for a girl who argues with professors.'

Judith made a sound of combined affection and dispute. David looked back at his bowl.

Ruth ate. The weight of the spoon in her hand. The salt of the broth on the roof of her mouth, the warmth spreading down through her chest as she swallowed. Outside, the wind pressed at the glass in a series of irregular gusts.

Sarah brought the brisket on the silver platter, both arms required for its weight, the steam rolling off the dark gravy in slow, dense clouds that reached the table before she did. The smell was immediate and total: the rendered fat, the woodsmoke, the onions that had been cooking in the meat's own liquid since morning, a sweetness from the caramelised carrots underneath. Mr Cohen rose from his chair and took the long carving knife. The blade went through the brisket with a long, steady sound, the meat falling away from the cut in the way of something that has been cooking for exactly the right amount of time.

David was talking. The photographs. The student union petition. Something about a man called Marcus who was keeping a record and needed more people willing to be named on it. Judith listened with her elbows on the table, which Mrs Cohen normally corrected and this time did not. Ruth watched Mr Cohen's hands on the knife.

Sarah stood at the sideboard for a moment with the empty platter, and caught the end of what David had said.

'You people always sound as if a law only becomes dangerous after it's been discussed over silver,' she said.

She said it in the same level voice she used for everything in this room. The room went still anyway. Not dramatically. The candles still burned, the clock still ticked. But the quality of the air changed. Ruth looked at the pattern of the white linen.

Four beats of the clock in the hall.

'And when does it become dangerous, Sarah?' Mr Cohen asked. He asked it without irony, and without the kind of gentleness that would have been worse than irony.

Sarah picked up the tray. 'When the wrong person reads it and smiles.' She pushed the kitchen door with her shoulder and was gone.

No one reached to soften what she had said. Ruth picked up her fork. The brisket was on her plate, its edges dark and glistening where the fat had caramelised against the heat, and she ate it slowly, and listened to the conversation find its way back, as it always eventually did, toward the wool market and the salt air and the cost of maintaining a tennis court in a coastal suburb. She watched the candles burn another inch and the shadows on the wallpaper shift with the draught from the windows.

The tall Sabbath candles had burned to their collars by the time the fruit compote arrived. Ruth set her spoon down and listened to the clock in the hall.

When the Cohens moved toward the sitting room, Judith's silk rustling against the floor, Ruth gathered three soup bowls and carried them through the swinging door.

The kitchen stripped the dining room away at once. The close, unventilated heat. The copper wash-pot on the stove giving off a slow, steady breath of steam that had begun to thicken the air above the burner. The brisket bones piled in the tin bucket by the back door, their smell rawer without the dining room's beeswax and candles to soften it. The single bulb hanging from the ceiling cord, its yellow light flattening everything it touched and removing every diplomatic layer the dining room had laid over things.

Sarah was at the deep concrete sink, her sleeves pushed to the elbows. The white apron was draped over the wooden chair. She wore the thick, dark jersey she always wore once the dining room work was done, the wool worn through to bare threads at the elbows from a great deal of washing over a long time. She was working the wire brush across the inside of the roasting pan without looking up.

'You don't have to do that,' she said.

'I know,' Ruth said. She set the bowls beside the sink. The kitchen smelled the same as it had in October. She had eaten the brisket from the wax-paper parcel in her room two weeks after, alone, the paper spread on the desk in the lamp's light. She had not said so.

Sarah looked at her sideways. The same rapid, assessing look: not suspicious, not warm, simply accurate.

Ruth found the grey cloth on the hook beside the oven and began drying the rinsed bowls, working from the base, the way her mother had taught.

She dried three bowls in silence.

'You know how to do that,' Sarah said. Not a question.

'I grew up with very little else to know,' Ruth said.

Sarah made a short sound. 'They give you the scholarship,' she said. 'But not the drying cloth.' She lifted the soup pot out of the soak and set it on the draining board. Ruth took it and started on the outside.

'They're good people,' Ruth said, and heard how it came out.

'They are,' Sarah said. She picked up the wire brush and worked it across the inside of a serving bowl in long, regular strokes. 'That is not the argument.'

'What is?'

'Good people make the condition possible.' She kept working. 'The silver is bright because I polished it three hours ago. This floor was on my knees at quarter past five this morning. Mr Cohen can draw the line between what is happening now in the parliament and what happened to his people in Europe, and it is the correct line and he is right to draw it. He has named the thing clearly at that table tonight and he will go to bed having named it properly. And I will come back on Monday and the floor needs doing again.' She set the brush in the water. 'The condition is what it is. Good people are part of the condition.'

Ruth put the soup pot on the shelf. In the overhead light, the metal had the colour of old bone. She turned back to the sink.

'Is your family from District Six?' she said.

Sarah looked at her. A short pause. 'My sister is in Hanover Street, District Six. I live in the Bo-Kaap.' She passed a rinsed bowl to Ruth. 'Different community, different streets. The Bo-Kaap is Malay, Muslim. The streets there run steep and the houses are painted colours they repainted after a previous government told them they couldn't, so they painted them before the next law could arrive. District Six is something else. Mixed, noisy -- musicians above you and fishermen below, and every language the Cape has ever spoken in the space of one street market. It is not one thing. That is precisely why they want it.' She paused, water running over her wrists. 'You cannot file plurality in the registry. So they will clear it, and build houses that stay in one category, and the people who buy those houses will call it improvement.'

Ruth dried the bowl. She kept her eyes on it.

'My sister Fatima,' Sarah said, before Ruth could ask. 'Her husband's family are fishermen out of Kalk Bay and the Waterfront, three generations. Born in the same two streets in District Six. Their children at the same schools, the same mosques.' She handed Ruth another bowl. 'Her youngest is six years old. His name is Isaak. He was born in the same room his grandfather died in. A man in Pretoria will decide whether that room still belongs to a category of person that is permitted to occupy it.' She pulled the plug from the sink. The water drained in a low, grinding rush. 'So. You asked about good people. The Cohens will write to their member of parliament. Their son will add his photographs to a record. They will be on the right side of the history that is being written. I do not say this to dismiss the letter or the photographs. I say it so you understand what it costs and who pays it.'

Ruth set the last bowl on the shelf.

She stood with the cloth in her hands.

'What is your surname?' she said. 'I don't know it. I should have asked in October.'

Sarah looked at her. The look lasted a beat longer than the assessing ones. Then: 'Davids,' she said. 'Sarah Davids.' A pause. 'And I have your name from Judith. So now we are at the same place.' She looked at the remaining pots in the sink. 'You should go back to the piano music, Miss MacLeod. Before Judith comes looking.'

'They won't come looking,' Ruth said.

'I know they won't,' Sarah said.

Ruth folded the cloth and hung it back on the hook. She picked her folder up from the counter where she had set it down without noticing.

The Schubert came back as she pushed through the door: precise and measured, moving through its phrase in the sitting room with the clarity of something that had always known where it was going.

She said goodbye in the hallway. Mrs Cohen pressed her hand and said something about Aeschylus and fresh eyes if the Sophocles grew too heavy. Mr Cohen shook her hand with both of his, a warmth she had still not learned not to be surprised by. Judith walked her to the door with her hands in her pockets.

'Tuesday evening,' Judith said. 'Union meeting. Marcus is presenting the photographs. Forty-seven as of tonight. He says he'll have forty-eight by Tuesday because they'll put up the post by the science block over the weekend.' A pause. 'He needs people willing to be named alongside him when he submits the record. Known witnesses. It makes the record harder to set aside.' She looked at Ruth directly. 'I thought you would want to know.'

Ruth put on her coat. Judith pulled the scarf from her own neck and tucked it into Ruth's coat pocket.

She went down in the cage lift.

Outside, the Sea Point street received her with the cold of the open Atlantic three blocks away, the salt smell immediate after the heat of the apartment. She stood on the pavement for a moment. Above the building, the mountain was a dark mass against a sky that was not quite black, the cloud-cloth thick over the summit, the granite face below the cloud visible only as an absence of stars. She did not look back at the lit windows.

The tram came along the coast road with its high electric whine. She climbed into the third-class car, found a seat near the back, and sat with her coat around her as the tram turned inland.

The lower city moved below. The streets of the Malay Quarter were close and lit at this hour: stone facades with wash-lines brought in for the night, a spice cart being steered into an alley, the silhouette of a man closing a shutter three floors above the pavement. From somewhere she could not locate -- an upper window, or behind a wall, or carried on the wind that found its way through the tram's ventilation slats -- a sound that was not quite music and not quite speech. Rhythmic and syncopated, pointed at the city in a particular way. It did not belong to the gramophone in the residence common room or the piano in the Cohens' drawing room. It was present for five or six seconds and then the tram moved and it was gone.

She thought about Hanover Street. Not the phrase of it but the action of it: the pen, the column, the category assigned to a name, the name assigned to a place, and then the place altered until the name was what remained and the person was a thing the record either permitted or did not. She had stood in the Registrar's office on her first morning in Cape Town and received the stamp on her scholarship form, the dark purple bruise of it still wet when the clerk slid the paper back. She had filed it then as machinery, as the mechanism of enrolment. The stamp was not metaphor. The stamp was always the point.

She took the silk scarf from her coat pocket and held it in her lap. Its lavender was almost gone, the coal smoke from Sarah's kitchen worked into the weave, and underneath both, the salt from her own hands through an evening of handling. She rolled it between her fingers in the dark.

Fatima. Hanover Street. Three generations in the same two streets.

She put the scarf back.

The high stone walls of the Southern Suburbs rose around the road. The oaks closed overhead. The tram motor ground against the grade with a long, complaining effort, and then the ridge levelled and the pines appeared and the university's buildings were dark shapes against the dark sky, and the residence was ahead, its granite foundations solid and unyielding and lit in the upper floors by the electric light of girls who had exams on Monday.

She did not go straight to her room.

She stood in the corridor outside Room 412 with her key in her hand and listened. Behind Eleanor Stratford-Reid's door, a gramophone was replaying the same two bars of something: the needle lifted, reset, dropped back into the same groove again. On the floor above, a door opened and closed.

She went in.

The room smelled of cold and lamp oil and the cedar of the wardrobe. She hung her coat on the hook. She took the silk scarf from the pocket and draped it over the corner of the desk, where it lay in the lamp's small circle of light in a thin, pale line. She sat down and opened her notebook.

Then she turned it over and wrote on the back page, below the margin, in the same ink she had been using on the Sophoclean chorus all afternoon:

*Sarah Davids. Hanover Street. Fatima.*

She looked at the three words for a moment. She had written them without deciding to, the way she had written the scholarship code on her first morning: SC-BA-1955-0047.

She closed the notebook and placed it inside the grammar.

She had not asked Sarah what happened when the law moved past the registry. When the petition and the letter and the forty-seven photographs were not enough. When Fatima's name was written out of Hanover Street and the street became officially what it had been in the process of becoming for years. That question was still in the kitchen. In the water draining cold from the sink. In the grey cloth folded on the hook. In the ash on the stone floor beside Sarah's boot.

She did not open the grammar. She went to bed with the lamp still burning and the scarf at the edge of her vision, its pale line thinning as the flame moved in the draught under the door.

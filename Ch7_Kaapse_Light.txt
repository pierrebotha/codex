# Chapter 7: Kaapse Light


The note was in Sarah's handwriting, four words on a corner torn from a paper bag: *Chiappini Street, forty-one.* The letters were small and pressed hard, the downstrokes going through to the surface underneath, the kind of handwriting that does not waste space.

The tram had let Ruth off at the bottom of Wale Street. She had the address in her coat pocket and a general bearing toward Signal Hill, which was more than enough for most of the city but not quite enough for this. The tram line went along the flat. Everything above the flat was on foot.

She began to climb.

The road went up with the immediacy of a city built by people for whom the mountain was a fixed fact and the approach was simply the approach. No apology in the gradient. The paving changed within a hundred yards: the municipal stone giving way to smaller cobbles, worn to an uneven gloss, each one set individually and not quite flush with its neighbour so that walking required a distributed attention across the sole of the foot. Her boot went down at an angle on a stone that tilted and she shifted her weight before the slip could complete itself, her palm finding the wall beside her, the lime render cold and slightly powdery, leaving a white dust on her fingers that she looked at for a moment before walking on.

The cold here was different from the Atlantic cold of the lower city. The Atlantic cold was lateral, directional, it came from the west with the weather in it, it smelled of open water and long distance. This cold came from above. It had altitude in it, something mineral and specific, and it arrived at the level of her collar with the character of stone and height rather than of sea. The mountain pressed its weight down through the cloudbase without showing its face, and its cold found the narrow width of each lane and moved through it without meeting any obstacle.

She stopped where the lane opened onto Chiappini Street.

The houses ran in a terrace up the slope, their frontages pressed to the narrow pavement. The colours of them stopped her more thoroughly than she had expected. She had been told about the colours. Someone at the residence had mentioned them in the easy tone of a traveller describing a curiosity, a sightseeing note delivered between two other topics. The experience was not that. The blue of the house to her immediate left was a blue that had been decided on, committed to, repainted at every wearing-out until it was no longer a choice of pigment but a position. The yellow past it: raw turmeric, thick enough at the window edges that layer had built on layer into a slight crustiness, each coat a separate season's assertion. The pale green to its right, almost white, with the translucence of a colour that had been diluted deliberately in a kitchen bowl by someone who wanted something specific and not what the tin offered.

The stoeps beneath them had been swept. The stone was damp from the week's rain, pale grey in the winter light, and on two of the stoeps there were tin cans with red geraniums still going in the cold, the flowers the most unequivocal colour on the street.

She looked for numbers on the doors.

Forty-one was mid-terrace, the front wall terracotta, paler at the top where the eaves had kept off the wet, deeper at the base where the rain had concentrated the pigment season by season. The door was dark green. The brass knocker was shaped like a hand, the fingers extended, the thumb raised. The brass was polished. She lifted it and let it fall twice.

Footsteps within. A pause. The door opened.

The woman who opened it was perhaps sixty, small and broad-shouldered with the deliberate posture of someone who has been running a household for decades and has the back for it. A long dark skirt, a heavy cotton blouse, a headscarf of pale printed cotton pinned at the front with two bone-coloured pins. Her face was deeply lined at the corners of the eyes and the mouth, the lines of outdoor cold and kitchen steam and, in the deep past of the skin's weathering, the Wale Street wind in all its seasons. She looked at Ruth with the steady, unhurried attention of someone who has come to this door a thousand times and has long since learned to make the full assessment in the first two seconds.

'Miss MacLeod?' she said.

'Yes. Ruth.'

'Sarah is already here.' She stepped back from the door, an opening that contained no softness and no unfriendliness, simply the practical movement of a person making space. 'Come in.'

The hallway was narrow, the ceiling low, the linoleum worn to a near-transparency at the centre where feet had been going over it for a long time. On the shelf above the coat stand, a small framed photograph whose subjects were too far from the light to read, and beside it a pottery bowl holding three eggs and a sprig of dried protea, the kind of arrangement found in houses where objects have been in their positions long enough to belong there.

'Kitchen is at the back,' the woman said, and went ahead without waiting to be followed.

---

The kitchen took the full width of the house. A range on the east wall, a fire in it, the iron plates warm to the air at two feet. A long table in the centre, its surface pale with decades of scrubbing, with a zinc bowl of cut dough in the cool centre air beside a ceramic jar of ground cinnamon, a tin of mixed spice, a glass dish of syrup cooling at the far end. On the range: two pans, one with oil beginning to move at the surface under the heat, one with the syrup pot still warming, the smell of hot sugar and cardamom filling the kitchen to its ceiling and concentrating near the range in a warmth that was as much smell as heat.

Sarah was at the table's far end with a cup of tea and a folded newspaper. She looked up.

'You found it.'

'Eventually. The numbers run the wrong direction.'

'They all do, up here.' She turned a page of the newspaper. 'Fatima, this is Ruth MacLeod.'

Fatima had gone directly to the dough bowl without looking up. 'I know who she is,' she said. 'Sit somewhere.'

Ruth sat on the bench nearest the range. The warmth of the iron reached through her coat and through the wool and into the cold that had gone into her back during the climb, finding the vertebrae first and spreading outward. She unbuttoned the coat.

Fatima's hands moved through the dough in a plaiting motion: taking a length, dividing it with the pads of her fingers, plaiting the three sections together with a twist that kept each length dense and uniform. She did not watch her hands. Her eyes were on the oil pan and then on the syrup pot and then on the grey winter light coming through the window above the range. The plaiting continued without her attention, producing identical lengths with the consistency of someone whose hands had been taught this task so early and so thoroughly that oversight was no longer part of it.

'You know koeksisters?' she said.

'I've had them. From a shop. In Johannesburg, I think.'

Fatima set down a plaited length with the deliberateness of a person placing a conclusion. 'Shop koeksisters are a biscuit that has heard of koeksisters.' She held her palm above the oil pan without touching it, reading the temperature from the air a few inches above the surface. 'Dough cold. Syrup cold. Both at the right moment, not just one. Most people get one right and call it done. The syrup needs the dough's heat to go in. If the dough is already cold from sitting too long, the syrup stays on the outside. You need both, not one.'

She went back to the plaiting.

Through the kitchen window a slice of the back lane was visible, and across it the yellow rear wall of the facing house, bright even under the winter cloud, and on the second-storey sill of that house a hen sitting in a wooden box with the settled conviction of a creature that has found its position and considers the matter settled.

The muezzin call came from the mosque above the street, from somewhere up and to the left, the call clear and unhurried in the cold air, moving down between the rooflines and through the kitchen window and into the room with the ease of something that has been arriving at this exact time every day for longer than anyone in the house can account for. Fatima set the dough on the cloth, dried her hands at her waist, went to the small shelf beside the dresser where a covered ewer and a cup stood, poured a measure of water, washed her hands over the enamel basin with a deliberate, unhurried precision, dried them again, and went through the inner door. It closed behind her.

Ruth sat with the range on her left and the smell of cooling syrup in the air above the table and the sound of the south-easter moving in the gap between the two rooflines outside.

Sarah had put the newspaper flat on the table.

'She'll be ten minutes or so,' she said. 'You can look at the dough. Don't work it.'

Ruth pressed one finger into the nearest plait. Cold and dense, resistant in the way that clay is resistant before heat reaches it, with something considered in the resistance, as if the dough had been made with an opinion about what would happen next. She pulled her hand back.

'She makes them every Friday for the mosque,' Sarah said. 'And for the neighbours at the end of the terrace when they have a gathering. And for Radia next door, who stopped baking in January when her husband left for Johannesburg.' She aligned the edge of the newspaper with the table's edge. 'And whenever the council sends another letter, which lately is often.'

Ruth looked at the cooling syrup in its dish. The surface had gone smooth as it dropped in temperature, the same surface tension as still water in a glass.

Sarah went back to the newspaper and stopped speaking.

Ten minutes passed.

Ruth sat with the warmth of the range on her left side and let the kitchen settle into itself around her. The sounds from the street came through the back window in the particular way that street sounds come through kitchen windows: filtered by glass and distance into something more general and less urgent than they were at the source, children's voices reduced to their rhythm rather than their content, a cart going over the cobbles, the creak of the lane gate. The muezzin call had fallen away. Behind the inner door where Fatima had gone, no sound at all.

The kitchen was a room that knew exactly what it was. The shelves of the dresser held their objects in an order that had been arrived at over years and not recently revised: the blue and white enamel bread tin with a faded motif of a mosque on its side, a row of sealed preserving jars, a ceramic bowl with a lid, a glass jar of dried something that might have been rosebuds. High on the wall above the dresser, a framed passage in Arabic script, the letters flowing right to left in a careful hand, the frame painted white and gone slightly yellow with kitchen steam. Beside the range, a rack of iron implements, each one on its own hook, the hooks evenly spaced, the implements hanging plumb. Not alignment for its own sake. Alignment because these things were used and returning each one to its exact position was part of the use.

On the table, the dough bowl and the plaited lengths on the cloth, each one identical to the last within a small tolerance that was not mechanical but human, the slight variation that accumulates when hands have been doing the same task from girlhood and have been accurate enough for long enough that perfection and near-perfection have become indistinguishable from the inside. Ruth looked at the plaited lengths for a while. She had come from a farm kitchen and a convent kitchen and the university residence kitchen, and in each of those places the kitchen had told her what kind of life was being sustained within the walls around it. This one told her something she did not yet have the precise language for, only the impression, held in the palm of the hand that had touched the dough and found it cold and dense and considered.

---

Two raps at the back door. The rhythm of it specific and habitual, not a stranger's knock. Sarah got up, went to the door, opened it.

The woman outside was perhaps forty, in a work coat over her dress, her hair in the braid she had slept in and not yet attended to. She spoke in Afrikaans at a speed Ruth could not follow, not the school Afrikaans of the classroom but something faster, with the vowels moved from the positions she had been taught and the syntax compressed to the minimum a shared history required. Sarah answered in the same register. A brief exchange, and then the woman looked past Sarah and saw Ruth at the table.

She said something. Sarah answered without looking back.

The woman's gaze settled on Ruth for four seconds, unhurried, making its assessment openly and without apology. Then she said something else to Sarah.

'She wants to know if you speak Afrikaans,' Sarah said.

'Some. Not well.'

Sarah translated this. A brief pause.

'She says: any university girl who tries anyway is already ahead of the ones who don't bother.'

'Tell her I'll work on it.'

Another exchange. The woman's expression shifted, a recalibration without warmth or coldness, the conditional movement of someone conceding a point they intend to revisit. She nodded once and went back out the door. Sarah closed it.

'Who is that?'

'Radia. She lives in thirty-nine.' Sarah sat down again. 'She's been trying to get a pipe under her floor fixed for six weeks. The landlord says it is the council's pipe. The council says it is the landlord's pipe. Mariam's floor has been damp since October. She has three children and a damp floor.'

'What will happen?'

'She writes letters. Very good ones, I'm told. Clear, with dates and specific measurements of the damage and the relevant municipal reference numbers.' Sarah opened the newspaper at the previous page. 'The letters go into a drawer in an office on Adderley Street. A different drawer each time, by the look of it.'

Ruth looked at the dough bowl, the plaited lengths cooling on their cloth. 'What does she do when the letters come back?' she asked.

'They don't come back,' Sarah said. She turned to the back pages. 'That is the point. But Radia says she is building quite a contribution to the public record. She says when they finally write the history of these pipes, her letters will be the primary sources.' She smoothed the newspaper flat. 'She is very thorough about it.'

---

Fatima returned to the kitchen and went directly to the range without looking at either of them. The oil had reached temperature. She tested it with a rapid touch of one fingertip that registered the heat and withdrew in the same motion before the heat could register back, and took up two plaited lengths of dough. She set them in the oil with the care of someone placing an argument they intend to stand behind.

The sound was immediate: a sustained sizzle, the hot fat taking the dough without hesitation, the colour beginning its movement from pale through gold to the specific amber that Fatima was watching for through some measurement that did not use her eyes, because she turned away from the pan to adjust the heat beneath the syrup pot and turned back at the precise moment the colour was right.

She lifted them with the slotted spoon, let them drain for a duration she measured somewhere in her wrists rather than in any count she was keeping, and moved them into the cold syrup. The sound changed: the syrup taking the heat of the dough, the differential driving the cold sugar in rather than leaving it pooled at the surface, and the process finished in the time it took to transfer them to the cloth. She set them down. They glistened.

She brought two to Ruth on a small plate.

The outside was hot from the oil. The inside was cold from the syrup all the way through, not merely at the surface but distributed throughout the dough as if it had been made that way. The spice was in the dough itself, restrained and integrated, not announcing itself first but present when the other registers had been accounted for.

Ruth finished both and looked at the empty plate.

'My mother's mother's recipe,' Fatima said, back at the dough. 'Her mother's before that. The women it has been through, by the time it gets to my hands I wouldn't know how to separate out which part belongs to which woman. It has become one recipe made from several.' She pressed the next pair of lengths into the oil and listened to the sound they made. 'That is probably how it should be.'

She went on working. The kitchen filled with the smell of the browning dough and the cooling cloth and the small, precise sound of each new pair going into the fat.

---

In the middle of the afternoon Fatima took a dish from the dresser shelf and said they were going up the hill to return it.

They went out through the green door and walked up Chiappini Street toward the mosque. The afternoon light had altered the street's register from the morning: the grey had resolved to a cold, very clean winter brightness, the kind that occurs when cloud burns off without warmth, and in that light the colours of the houses were sharper and more decided than they had been earlier. The terracotta of Fatima's own front wall, seen from outside now, was the colour of the Far Northern Transvaal soil after rain, the particular red-brown of laterite when wet.

Fatima stopped two doors up the hill. The woman who came to the door was perhaps fifty, in a Saturday dress and headscarf, and the conversation that began was in Kaaps, rapid and internally referential, with the shorthand of two people who have been accounting for the same set of shared facts for years and need very few words for the scaffolding. Ruth stood on the step below and listened to the shape of it without the content: a son, a decision, a date that had been moved. Laughter that had exasperation in it alongside the laughter. Fatima gestured toward the mountain above the roofline. The woman looked up, shook her head, said something with the deflating intonation of a person who has heard a reasonable point before and is not fully persuaded and knows it.

Then she looked at Ruth.

'You're the one from the university,' she said. In English. Flat, a statement rather than a question.

'Yes.'

'Sarah says you're all right.' A long, deliberate look, the kind that takes its time because it has decided not to be rushed. 'Sarah doesn't say that about too many people up here. She's careful with it.'

Fatima took back the empty dish, said something brief in Kaaps, and came down the step. They walked on.

At the next corner four girls of about ten or eleven were at a skipping game with a homemade rope, the rhythm of the turning and the chant interlocking with the particular synchronisation of children who have been doing this specific thing together long enough to have absorbed each other's timing into their own bodies. They did not stop when Fatima and Ruth passed, but two of them assessed Ruth on the move, somewhere between one skip and the next, the assessment complete and filed before the rope came back around.

Fatima stopped further along where a woman was coming down her stoep steps with a basket of washing and a child on one hip, both at the same time and neither gracefully. Fatima took the basket without asking. The woman shifted the child to her other hip and came the rest of the way down the steps with the freed hand on the railing. A brief exchange followed, warm and quick, the woman laughing at something and shaking her head. She looked at Ruth with a curiosity that was open and unguarded, the direct interest of someone who has no particular reason to be careful.

'Where do you come from?' she said.

'The Far Northern Transvaal. Near Tzaneen.'

She repeated the name to herself, quietly. 'Is it cold there?'

'In winter. A different cold from here. Drier. The Drakensberg escarpment catches the mist.'

She nodded as if she were filing the information in a place that had been waiting for it. Fatima handed back the basket and they walked on up the hill.

At the turn of the lane a boy was sitting on the kerb with a wire car, working at the front axle with a small pair of pliers, tightening and testing and tightening again with the absorbed focus of someone for whom this problem is the only problem currently in existence.

'Yusuf,' Fatima said.

He looked up. 'Auntie Fatima.' Then he looked at Ruth with the same directness as the woman with the child, no adjustment of register for an audience. 'Do you know how to fix wire cars?'

'No.'

He received this without disappointment. 'Nobody at the university knows how to fix wire cars,' he said, with the resigned authority of someone who has done fieldwork on this question and arrived at a settled finding. He went back to the pliers.

Fatima made a sound through her nose, dry and quiet, and continued up the lane.

On the return she said, without preamble: 'Yusuf's father was at the docks in November. The day of the trouble there. He came home with a broken rib and a cut above the eye. The police said it was not from them.' She took the turn of the lane without redirecting her gaze. 'Yusuf turned twelve in December. He made his father a wire car for his birthday because his father had told him once that he'd had one as a boy. He's been improving the design since.' She paused. 'He'll work the axle out. He always works it out. He just needs someone to acknowledge that the problem is real.'

Ruth walked beside her without speaking. The cobbles were uneven in the shadow of the near wall, one cracked stone lifting at an edge that required the foot to register and compensate. A complaint requiring attention. Nobody had submitted it in writing, measured the damage, cited the relevant municipal reference numbers. The street held the cracked stone and the people who used the street held it too, in the soles of their feet and their accumulated muscle memory, the knowledge of where to put the foot and where not to.

Fatima stepped over the cracked stone without looking down. She had known where it was for years.

They walked back down the hill in the cooling air. The street was quieter than it had been an hour before, the midday sociability settling into the later domestic register of Saturday afternoon, the conversations already moved indoors with the dropping temperature. A cat was in a doorway with its eyes partially closed, the posture of an animal that has decided the afternoon requires nothing further of it. From behind one of the painted frontages came the sound of a man's voice reading aloud, steady and measured, the same sentence twice, and then a third time at a slightly different tempo. A child learning. Or a man reminding himself of something. The sound did not need an explanation.

At the bottom of the hill the tram stop was visible, its iron post and the distance of the sea beyond the lower rooftops. Ruth stopped and looked back up the slope for a moment, the colour of the houses against the winter-white cloud above the mountain, bright and decided and completely at odds with the grey municipal geometry of the city below them.

---

Sarah was gone when they returned to the kitchen. The newspaper was folded squarely at the table's far edge, the teacup beside it rinsed and placed inverted on the drying cloth. Ruth had not heard her leave. The kitchen had the particular quality that a room acquires when the last person in it has gone about their own business and taken their particular weight of presence with them.

Fatima filled the kettle and put it on without looking at the space Sarah had occupied.

---

They sat at each end of the table with their tea. The last of the koeksisters were cooling on their cloth in a row, done now, the pan soaking in cold water in the basin, the fire in the range let down to a maintenance heat so that the kitchen was quieter, the afternoon wind making its steady low sound outside between the rooflines.

'You want to ask me something,' Fatima said.

Ruth held her cup and said nothing.

'Most visitors do. They come up here with their questions between the pages of their good intentions, pressed flat and ready.' She drank. 'They want to ask why we stay. Or what it is like to live here. Or they want to tell me what they think about what is happening.' She set the cup down. 'All three of those things are the same question wearing different clothes.'

The afternoon wind in the eaves. The low sound of it moving without result.

'My father's father built this house,' Fatima said. 'Out of money saved from forty years of working at a wage that assumed he had no needs beyond subsistence. He chose this street because of the light from the mountain in the mornings. He said that the morning light from the mountain on these walls was the best light in Cape Town and he was not going to live anywhere else.' She turned her cup handle parallel with the table's edge. 'His father was brought here from Batavia in seventeen and ninety-something. I do not know the exact year because the records were not kept in a way that made it easy to find us later.'

She drank.

'The British,' she said, 'said we could only paint the houses white. Clean and uniform. Part of some idea about order and what a street should present to the people who were permitted to have opinions about what streets looked like. When the British arrangement changed, my mother's sister painted her house blue. Not a careful blue. Blue as she intended to do it.' A pause. 'The following week the family next door did theirs red. And so on down the street for about two years until it was as you see it.'

She looked at the window and the yellow of the wall across the lane, going deeper in the failing afternoon light.

'Now there is another arrangement,' she said.

The word for the arrangement was already present in the room without being named. Ruth could see it in the angle of Fatima's cup and the particular stillness she held herself in when she said *arrangement.*

'The man from the council came in November,' Fatima said. 'A young man with a printed form. Very correct, very polite. Our address was already on the form, typed in the right box. He showed me the proposed boundary on a map with one finger moving along a line that had been drawn with a ruler.' She straightened the handle of her cup again, a small alignment. 'He said he was sorry. He said it three times. He was genuinely sorry, I think, which is its own kind of difficulty.'

Ruth looked at her hands around the cup.

The kitchen held its sounds around the silence. The draft in the range, the small settling of the cooling pan in the basin, Fatima's cup on the table and Fatima's gaze not on it.

There had been something else. The form the council man carried had a column along one side with a code in it. Three letters and two numerals, printed smaller than the rest, and he had not explained it. Fatima had not asked, because asking would have required him to say it aloud in her kitchen, and the map had already said what it said: not a move to another part of the city, not a redistribution to keep people on the slope with only the names on the title deeds changed. The boundary the young man's finger had traced was not a boundary between one group's area and another's. It was a line between the slope as it currently existed and a use of the slope that did not include these houses or the people in them.

Fatima turned her cup handle parallel with the table edge. It was already parallel. She turned it anyway.

The form had a proper header. The seal in the corner, the reference number, the rubber stamp from the correct office on Adderley Street. Nothing about it suggested that it had been produced by anyone acting outside the correct procedure. Not the young man's instrument. The correct office's. The correct procedure's.

'Fifty-one years my father's family have been on this street,' Fatima said. 'My father was born here. I was born here. My husband's two children from his first wife were born here.' She was quiet for a moment. 'The boy in the photo in the hallway is my son. He is in Johannesburg now, in a boarding house in Fordsburg. He went because the work he could get here was not enough. He sends money every month, which gets there about ten days after he says he has sent it.' She picked up her cup and set it down without drinking. 'He has not been back for fourteen months. The boarding house has a shared kitchen and a yard.'

She stood and took the cups to the basin. She rinsed them without haste, set them in the drying rack, and turned to the dresser.

On the second shelf, between the blue-and-white enamel bread tin and a row of sealed preserving jars, a piece of paper lay folded once with the fold facing outward. Fatima's hand came down onto it, the palm flat, the fingers loosely curled. Not presenting it. Not pressing it down. Simply her hand on it, for one breath, in the way that hands go to certain things in certain rooms before the mind has consciously directed them there.

Then she moved the bread tin two inches to the right, reached to the shelf above for the biscuit tin, and turned back to the table.

'More tea?' she said.

Ruth said yes, though the second cup was not what she wanted and both of them knew it.

The kitchen settled into the quieter register of the late afternoon. The range ticking, the light through the back window moving lower, the yellow of the wall across the narrow lane shifting to a colour it had not been at midday, deeper and more specific, the kind of yellow that winter afternoon light makes on cold plaster.

They sat.

The muezzin began again. The Asr call. Neither of them moved. The call went through the kitchen and out through the back window and away over the rooflines, and when it was done the kitchen was quieter than it had been before it started.

Fatima set her cup down.

Ruth stood. She reached for her coat from the back of the chair where it had been sitting for two hours in the warmth of the kitchen. She put it on. She buttoned it.

She looked at the dresser shelf. The paper lay where it had been, in its fold, the fold facing outward.

---

Ruth left when the light was going.

Fatima gave her four koeksisters in a folded cloth and said to keep them away from the heat. Sarah had said something much like it in the Cohen kitchen in the autumn about a parcel of brisket, and Ruth held the two cloths together for a second before she stepped through the green door and heard it close behind her.

Chiappini Street was quieter than it had been at midday, the afternoon's sociability drawn back into interiors, into kitchens and prayers and the particular negotiations of Saturday evenings in houses where the week had its weight. The cold had sharpened. The mountain above the roofline was emerging from the cloudbase again, its lower face returning to visibility in the last of the daylight, the Triassic sandstone going from featureless grey to the amber of high cliff-face in a winter sunset, the colour that Thomas Lawrence had named precisely a week past on the Oranjezicht veranda.

She started down.

The cobbles on the descent required the same attention as on the way up. The greasy patch was where she had left it, and she moved across it with more preparation this time and did not need the wall.

Halfway down she came to a house she had not properly registered on the way up. The front wall was being painted. A woman stood on a low wooden step with a broad brush and a tin of yellow paint, applying a new coat to a wall that still held its previous coat fully intact, not peeling, not cracked, the old yellow sound and clean beneath the new. She was painting it anyway. The smell of the paint reached the pavement with the particular sharpness that linseed oil takes on when the cold concentrates it, so that the smell was simultaneously of paint and of winter air coming off the high rock above. The new coat went on thick and even, each stroke absorbed into the one before it before it could dry and show the edge.

She did not look up as Ruth passed.

Ruth went down to the tram stop at the bottom of Wale Street. She sat on the lower deck, her coat buttoned, the folded cloth in her lap. The city fell away below the window as the tram moved: the dense pressed streets of the lower suburbs, the harbour arc lights making their flat geometry over the ships, the Atlantic beyond them without any light on it, only the idea of open water in the dark.

She did not open her bag.

The window gave her back her own face, translucent and slightly displaced from itself in the glass, the reflection not quite aligned with the darkness pressing against the other side of it. Behind and to the left of her reflection, the hill receded: Signal Hill and Chiappini Street with its forty-ones and its scrubbed stoeps and its colours decided and repainted at every wearing-out, and its letters in drawers on Adderley Street, and its pipe under Radia's floor that belonged either to the landlord or to the council depending on which one you asked, and Yusuf on the kerb with his pliers working the axle problem through to its solution, and the hand on the folded paper for one breath before the tea was asked about.

The tram rounded the curve past Rondebosch. The harbour lights disappeared behind the buildings.

Ruth held the cloth parcel in her lap with both hands and let the tram carry her up through the dark toward the lights of the university on the hill.

She had not asked Fatima how long the koeksisters would keep.

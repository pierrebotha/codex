# Chapter 8: District Six

The tram let her off at the bottom of Hanover Street on a Tuesday midmorning, the conductor's bell still ringing as the car rounded the curve and disappeared.

Ruth stood on the pavement with her coat unbuttoned. She had been on the upper deck for the descent from the university flats, watching the city's geometry change below her: the wide, tree-lined streets of the Bowl giving way to the narrower roads of the lower suburbs, the gardens shrinking, the houses pressing closer, washing lines appearing between buildings where before there had been only roof and sky. At the bottom of De Waal Drive the tram had stopped to let on a fish merchant with two crates, the smell of it reaching the upper deck before the man himself appeared on the steps. The smell was still in her coat.

'You're looking at it like a map,' Miriam said, coming up beside her.

'I'm reading the sign.'

The corner-shop sign was hand-lettered in three registers: English above, Afrikaans beneath it, and below that a script she could not read, the letters rounded and close-set, moving right to left. Spices. Fresh fruit. Cigarettes. In the window a bolt of printed cotton stood alongside a pyramid of condensed-milk tins and a hand-written price card already yellowing at the edges. The glass was clean.

'Come on,' Miriam said.

The street ran uphill away from the sea. The buildings on each side were three and four storeys, hung with iron balconies where pot plants, bicycles, and folded washing occupied every inch of the railing. The pavement was wide enough for two people walking close and in practice most of it was taken up by trade: a man with a brazier of roasting mealies at the corner, the smoke going up in a thin, steady line before the south-easter caught it level with the first-floor windows; a woman selling guavas from a bucket, the fruit halved and bright; a boy calling something in Afrikaans that Miriam answered without breaking stride.

The noise came in layers. The fishmonger at the street's far end calling his prices in a voice that carried the whole block. A radio from a first-floor window, the sound thin and very bright, a penny-whistle melody she half-recognised. The creak of a painted sign swinging above a tailor's doorway. Children's feet on the iron stairs of the building across the street, a brief thunder that stopped as suddenly as it had begun.

Ruth walked and said nothing. The smell of the mealies followed them half a block. Then cumin or coriander from an open sack set in a doorway, the sacking dark with old oil. Then the cold mineral smell of a lane between buildings where the sun had not yet reached, the stone damp and slightly green at the base. The street gave her each register a few paces apart.

A tailor stood in the open doorway of his shop with a measuring tape around his neck and a cigarette burning in the corner of his mouth. He watched them pass with the unhurried assessment of a man who has measured a great many people.

'You looking for something?' he said. Not unfriendly.

'Just looking,' Ruth said.

He lifted his chin slightly. 'Nobody comes up Hanover Street just looking,' he said. 'Find out what you came for. Then come back when you know.'

He turned back into the shop. The measuring tape swung once against his chest and was still.

Miriam glanced at her sideways. 'He says that to everyone,' she said. 'The first time.'

Ruth looked back at the doorway. The tailor was already at his table, his head bent over a length of dark cloth, the cigarette still burning in the ashtray beside him. Through the window above his workbench she saw a child's drawing tacked to the wall: a house in blue crayon, a sun with straight lines radiating from it, the lines very even and very careful.

The tailor had done in two sentences what most of her tutorials required an hour not to do. Not welcome, not dismissal. A direction and a standing condition: come back when you know. She had not been addressed that precisely since the convent. But the convent's precision had been a correction. This was something else.

She turned back to the street. The wind off the mountain reached her here between the buildings, carrying the smell of the sea and the roasting mealies and something else underneath, something she could not name and did not try to.

Miriam walked on without looking back.

The lane they turned into was barely wide enough for two people side by side. The buildings on each flank rose close enough overhead that the iron balconies nearly met, their railings strung with washing and bicycles and in one case a birdcage with the door left open and the occupant long gone. The sound changed when they entered: harder, more contained, the cobblestones returning a different report underfoot than the wide street had, and the cold in the lane was not the mobile cold of moving air but the cold of stone that had held the sea's moisture for weeks and had no particular reason to release it.

Three shops along, a barber's pole turned in a doorway. The red and the white had gone grey in the shadow, the motor driving the rotation old enough that its movement had slowed to the pace of something considering. The Dutch door stood open at the top and shut at the bottom, and from inside came not a voice but several voices at different registers, running in parallel, none of them requiring the others to stop while they spoke: a voice attending to the man in the chair, a voice addressing someone at the counter, a third voice addressing the street at large, making an observation about the morning that nobody was required to answer.

'Miriam,' the voice said, without pause.

Miriam stopped at the doorway. 'Uncle Jonas.'

The man with the razor — short, carefully moustached, apron knotted with the economy of someone for whom the motion is entirely automatic — did not look up from the neck he was attending. 'The scholarship girl too. Nadia, chairs.'

Inside, the shop was long and low-ceilinged. The smell of it was layered: bay rum at the immediate surface, then talc, then beneath both the sweet, slightly rancid note of brilliantine applied in summer heat and still present in the winter walls, the record of the same transactions performed across a great many years. The mirror ran the length of the room, and in it Ruth saw herself and Miriam and Jonas and the customer and the girl behind the counter and the whole interior doubled, the actual proportions of the space extended beyond their actual dimensions. Above the mirror, photographs covered the wall: men in various conditions of haircut at various points in their lives, christenings and weddings and events whose occasions she could not immediately read, each annotated in a small, confident Afrikaans hand. *Van Niekerk-trouery. Reunie 1936.* A rugby match with a hand-painted banner just visible at one edge. Two photographs pinned close together: a man in a South African forces uniform, and beside it what appeared to be the same man twenty years later, seated at a kitchen table, his eyes at a point slightly above the camera lens and not at it.

The tea arrived in two cups without saucers, carried by Nadia who was perhaps fourteen and whose preoccupied air suggested her mind was already attending to something outside this room and this errand. Strong enough to stand a spoon in. Ruth held the cup in both hands.

When his customer was paid and gone, Jonas took the empty chair at the end of the row. He folded his hands on his apron with the deliberate ease of a man who makes few gestures and keeps each one legible.

'You're from the north,' he said to Ruth.

'Near Tzaneen. Lowveld.'

'Long way south.'

'Yes.'

'You staying?'

She considered it. The question had the specific gravity of a man who has asked it before and received a variety of answers and is still collecting them. 'I think so,' she said.

He looked at the far end of the mirror. Behind the reflections of the two of them the photographs receded in the glass, the faces slightly smaller, the inscriptions illegible. 'This district stays too,' he said. 'Also not certain about it. Also saying it for too long.' He stood and unwound the apron from his waist, folded it with the particular efficiency of someone who places everything in an exact position because the space requires it. 'There's a notice board on Caledon Street this morning. Same language as the last one. A little more specific about the timeline. Come back when you want a proper cup.'

He had a new customer in the chair before they had returned their cups to the counter.

---

They walked north from Caledon Street through the side streets, Miriam navigating with the orientation of someone who learned these directions long enough ago that the learning had worn away and only the result remained. She knew which lane cut cleanly through to the next street and which only appeared to, which courtyard was public passage and which was a building's private yard, which corner-shop owner would allow two people to stand in his doorway out of the wind without buying anything as long as they kept themselves to the left of the display. She pointed and named as they went, not for Ruth's benefit in particular and not explaining anything: the secondary school, the fire station that had been a church hall before the fire station, a building she indicated only by a tilt of her head whose significance Ruth could not read.

A hardware merchant's door stood open and the smell of turpentine and sawdust came out of it into the lane, a clean, sharp smell after the damp stone. Inside a man was cutting a length of timber on a board, the saw moving with the smooth, considering stroke of someone who has done this long enough that he no longer thinks about the cut but only about where it needs to end.

Further along, a butcher and a fishmonger occupied adjacent shops, their doors both open, their voices both carrying at full volume, each one calling his prices in the tone and register of a man who considers that his product's merit speaks for itself but considers also that it speaks more forcefully when amplified. The butcher called in Afrikaans. The fishmonger called in English and then repeated the same sentence in Afrikaans, as if offering both options were a courtesy the street deserved. The smell of the fish overlay the smell of the sawdust, and both were overlaid by a smell of something frying from the window of the house above, and the compound of all three was the smell of that hour on that street, entirely its own.

At the top of the slope a group of boys had taken possession of the lane's lower section for a game of football whose rules appeared to be subject to continuous renegotiation. The argument about the rules was being conducted in three languages, or what presented as three but moved between them so fluidly that the boundaries were more a matter of temperature than category, the Afrikaans cooling into formal English and then the English warming back into something that was neither and was used between them with the ease of a private syntax. The ball went into the gutter. Someone disputed whose turn it was to retrieve it. The language of this dispute was briefly all three at once.

A man in a white kufi came down the far side of the lane from the mosque above the street, moving at a pace that had purpose in it. He crossed to the sunny side near the hardware shop. A man in a dark coat came out of the hardware merchant's door at the same moment, a length of wood under one arm, and looked up. The first man said a name. The second man said a name in return, his hand coming up briefly. They did not stop. Each continued in his direction and the street absorbed both of them.

Ruth walked through this and said nothing.

From a second-storey window above the lane an accordion began, its bass notes carrying better than its reeds in the cold air, playing a melody Ruth half-recognised, something she had heard from a gramophone somewhere in a room she could not immediately place. Two women were standing on the stoep below it. They had come out of the house's front door and were on the pavement, either waiting for someone or simply standing, and they had begun singing along with the accordion melody in a quiet, domestic way, both of them getting the words slightly wrong and neither of them minding. They were still singing, or approximately singing, when Ruth and Miriam reached the corner and turned north.

---

The music came from above, from a window propped open with something folded against the cold. A penny-whistle running a melodic phrase in a repeating loop, not seeking a resolution, going around as if the point were the going rather than the arrival. And under it a guitar picking a rhythm in a different time, and the two converged at the end of every phrase as if they had an arrangement that dispensed with everything in between, arriving in the same place at the same count through different routes.

Miriam led her through an arched entrance into a courtyard. The arch was worn enough at the keystone to predate everything else in the street by a generation. The courtyard held the enclosed smell of coal smoke and old cooking and the sweet fermentation note of fruit going over in a warm interior, the compound smell of a space whose walls had been absorbing the record of daily life for a long time and did not intend to stop.

A boy of about fifteen was in the courtyard mending a bicycle with total, undisturbable concentration. He did not look up.

The stairwell was narrow, the walls a buff yellow going darker toward the top where the air did not move. On the first landing a watercolour was pinned above the door: the mountain from a viewpoint below, filling the upper half of the paper, the rooftops of the district below it dense and ordinary and unrealised. The painter had not added anything to either the buildings or the mountain that was not observationally necessary. Both were present in the painting the way things are present in fact: without invitation and without apology.

On the second floor, four men were playing cards around a cable spool stood on its end. The cards moved between them with the automatic speed of a game well past requiring thought. An argument was running simultaneously in at least three languages in the undertone beneath the card game, not interrupting it, the argument as ambient as the light in the room: something about Joseph, something about Joseph's analysis of a situation, something about the gap between Joseph's analysis and the situation. One man, grey at the temples, in a jacket that had been a good jacket some years past, looked up at Ruth.

'New face,' he said. His eyes went to Ruth.

'She reads Latin,' Ruth said.

He looked at the man across the spool. Something moved between them, not any readable expression, only the density of a private understanding. He played his card. 'Tell her we'll be needing it,' he said, 'when the university finishes sorting out our address problem.' The man to his left assessed the played card with the compressed misgiving of someone who finds a legal move philosophically inadvisable.

They climbed to the third floor.

The penny-whistle player was on a window ledge, one leg inside and one outside, the cold coming in around his leg with indifference on both sides. He was perhaps forty, with the settled quality of a man who has found the position he wants and will occupy it until he decides otherwise. The guitar was across the room: a young woman in a green jersey, her head bent over the body of the instrument in the private absorption of someone working on a passage that was not finishing right. She tried the phrase again. It did not resolve. She shook her head, not with frustration but with the mild assessment of someone crossing a word off a list, and tried it once more at a slower pace.

Nobody introduced anyone. Miriam took a crate near the door. Ruth found a chair without a back that held her weight well enough, and for a while nothing was said that required a response and the street noise came up through the open window and the woman tried the passage again, and this time found the turn in it, and went through it twice at tempo and then laid her hand flat on the strings and let it be.

She raised her head.

'There's people going door to door on Hanover again,' she said. Her voice had the quality of a weather report: accurate, undramatised, addressed to the room in general. 'Two of them. Clipboards. One's a municipal shirt. I recognised him from March. Different face, same shirt.'

The man on the window ledge moved his chin a fraction. He looked at the street below. 'Different from the property survey lot?'

'Different people. Same purpose.' She tuned the low string a quarter turn. 'My sister puts the children's drawings on the wall. The teacher told her to. Makes the room feel like a home, the teacher said. She's had seventeen months of drawings up on that wall.' A pause. 'When they come to measure the room for the register, they'll tell her the drawings are not structural.' She picked a single open chord, letting it die. 'Not a permanent fixture. Not part of the property.'

The room absorbed this. The argument from the floor below surfaced briefly through the ceiling, the discussion of Joseph's theory carrying its own internal logic, and then receded.

'Boeta's building had a letter,' the penny-whistle player said. To the street below, not to the room. 'About the zoning category. Not the rent. The category.'

The woman with the guitar raised her hand from the strings and looked at her fingers for a moment, taking an inventory of them. She lowered her hand. She began the passage again, from the beginning, slowly.

---

Coming back down the building's stairs and through the courtyard, past the boy who had not looked up from his bicycle across the full duration of their visit, they emerged onto the lane. The sound opened: the lane's compressed echo giving way to the wider register of Caledon Street, a fishmonger's voice carrying two blocks in the wind, someone on a stoep above calling to someone on an adjacent building in the easy tone of a place where the distance between people was measured in feet rather than in convention.

Ruth walked and said nothing. A woman came out of a door on the left with a basin of washing and passed on the right with a cloth bag, and these two acknowledged each other with the minimal shared nod of people who have crossed in exactly this way on every Tuesday of their adult lives, a currency between them so familiar it required no counting. A man was reading a letter on his front step with the total attention of someone in possession of news they have been waiting for and are now alone with. The football argument from the lane above had migrated half a block and shifted partly back into Afrikaans for the next round of it.

Before they reached Caledon a man came out of the café at the corner and held the door behind him for an older woman in a hat coming out at the same time. She thanked him in Afrikaans. He answered her in what was not quite either language, four words that took the shortest path between Afrikaans and English and required no decision about which one to arrive in. Both of them were already going in their separate directions before the café door had finished closing.

---

Miriam's father's shop ran the length of a narrow room off Caledon Street, the shelving made to the ceiling on both walls, the top shelves running on a ladder-rail and occupied by stock that had been there long enough to simply belong there: tins with labels beginning to lift at the corners, a large jar with a hand-written label in a script she could not read, a row of bottles on their sides in the arrangement of something not recently moved. Below the point where regular use began, the stock was dense and ordered in a logic that had nothing to do with product category as a manufacturer would organise it. Things that belonged to the same kitchen stood together: tinned tomatoes alongside canned beans alongside dried chillies alongside the specific cumin she had been smelling all morning from doorways. Things used in the same season, the same meal, the same sequence of a working week. A Lindt tin near the front door with no chocolate in it held the money.

A woman came in for samp and stayed for twenty minutes. She and Miriam's father discussed the samp, a recent adjustment in the price of samp, a man called Joseph who had a theory about the adjusted price of samp, and the general reliability of Joseph's various theories over time. The conversation about Joseph widened naturally into an examination of his youngest daughter, who had recently taken a position at the hospital on De Waal Drive, and from there into a precise and unhurried analysis of what the hospital expected the young women who worked there to provide versus what it was prepared to publicly acknowledge that it expected. This last subject was concluded with the same efficiency as the samp.

Miriam's father looked at Ruth during the final part of this conversation. She kept her face still.

'Your people farm?' he said, when the woman had gone.

'Yes. Lowveld.'

'Hard country.'

'In the dry years.'

He looked at her in the same manner Jonas had, a private assessment against private criteria. Then he reached under the counter and produced a crate. 'Miriam's aunt has been waiting for this since Monday,' he said. 'Miriam knows the stairs.'

The crate was fabric bolts: lighter than its look suggested, requiring two people not for the weight but for the angles of the stairs. Ruth took the high end. Miriam took the low. They went through the shop's back door, past the washing yard, two lines strung taut and running sheets and shirts going stiff in the cold, and up the internal stairs cut at the steep angle of a building that did not intend to waste floor space.

Miriam's aunt was a woman of about fifty with the broad-shouldered authority of someone who has been the organising intelligence of a working household for long enough that the household has organised itself entirely around her method. She received the crate without performed gratitude. She pointed at the chairs below the window.

'Sit,' she said. 'There's tea coming.'

The room was the right size for what was in it: a cutting table kept clear of everything except the current work, bolts racked on the wall in the sequence of active use, a narrow kitchen partition fitted with complete domestic efficiency, everything at the height and proximity the work required. Through the window the side lane was visible, the cobbles drying unevenly in the last of the sun, and on the building opposite a row of chrysanthemums in clay pots along the balcony, orange and yellow, receiving the afternoon light at a practised angle.

The tea came in cups that were not matched and carried no apology for it. Ruth held hers in both hands. The aunt moved through the bolts from the crate, laying each against the table edge to read the quality of the cut end before refolding, the motions brisk and silent and entirely self-sufficient. Miriam had been assigned a section of the rack to resort and was doing it in a running exchange with the aunt about which bolts went where and why, the brown rather than the grey because the grey pulled light wrong when the material moved, the light blue for the woman on Hout Street whose pattern wanted the weight without the drape. Ruth followed enough of it to know it was a domestic professional language, shorthand built from a shared history of specific jobs with specific clients over a long time.

Outside, a child was bouncing a ball against the lane wall in the repeating rhythm of someone who has found exactly the wall and the afternoon they needed. The bounce came at steady intervals. When it skipped once the small irregularity resolved itself and the rhythm resumed.

Ruth finished the first cup. The aunt set a second in front of her without looking up from the bolt end she was checking, without asking, without noting the provision of it. The motion had the ease of a hand in its own kitchen, knowing what is needed and supplying it without requiring the transaction to be visible. Ruth picked it up.

'Your father's from up-country?' the aunt said, still on the fabric.

'Near Tzaneen. Lowveld.'

'Long way south.' She refolded the bolt with exact, practised creases. 'My husband's brother had the transport route to Duiwelskloof for a while before the war. He said the bushveld in a good rain was the most beautiful country he'd seen.'

'Yes,' Ruth said. 'It is.'

The aunt nodded as if this confirmed something she had privately believed, and returned to the end of the bolt.

Miriam moved to the far end of the table, where a second bolt was wanting a second pair of hands. She took the free end and pulled it taut. The aunt ran the tape the full length with a single motion and called the number before the tape was off the material, wrote it, and passed the bolt to the rack. Ruth stood. On the table a third bolt lay with its free end beginning to drift sideways in the draught from the window. She went to the table and took the end and held it square and steady at the corner, the cloth cool against her palms.

The aunt glanced at it. She said nothing. She ran the tape, noted the measurement, and moved to the next piece. Miriam brought a bolt from the crate and resorted it to its position on the wall. The work went on. Ruth passed the third bolt to the rack without being asked where it went, went back to the table, held the next free end when it began to drift.

A year and a half of accounting for herself on arrival, of working out the right register before speaking, of being the kind of useful that came with credentials attached. This room required only that her hands stay still. The aunt neither thanked her nor noted her presence.

Ruth looked at the chrysanthemums on the balcony opposite. The wind was worrying the outer petals, lifting and flattening them with each gust, and the pots were too heavy to move, the plants taking the wind as a condition and not a problem. She counted: she had been in this room for roughly forty minutes. She had not done that calculation until now. She had not calculated anything in that time.

---

They came back down to Hanover Street in the late afternoon, the south-easter still coming off the mountain, the pennants and signs all running flat to the east. The amber light of the late winter afternoon was working the stone fronts of the terraced houses, finding the ochre and cream beneath the grey wash and briefly making them distinct. The man on the ladder at the corner was finishing the orange lettering above the shop, going back over each letter before the paint set, small corrections against the wind's interference.

Ruth watched the letters for a moment. Beneath the orange, in the ghost-layer visible at the joins and curves, the previous name was still present: lighter, the colour of something subsiding, but there. The old Afrikaans name in the substrate of the wall, the new English name over it, and when the orange dried the old letters would be invisible from the pavement without being removed.

A notice board had been fixed to the wall outside Jonas's shop. A municipal backing frame, text in two parallel columns, English and Afrikaans. From the pavement Ruth could read enough of the Afrikaans to assemble the shape of it: *hersonering*, *register van eiendomme*, a date, a reference number. A man she had not seen before was standing close to the board, reading it. His hands were deep in his jacket pockets, his shoulders pulled fractionally inward in the way of someone weathering something that has come from outside. He finished, and stood still for a moment more without moving. Then he walked back toward Caledon Street at a pace that was ordinary and unhurried.

Jonas was closing his shop, the half-door drawn to with the routine finality of end-of-day. He saw Ruth looking at the board.

'Same sheet went up on four other streets last Tuesday,' he said. Not to Ruth exactly. To the space between them and the board. 'They put them all up on the same morning. Very organised. Same language, same date, same reference number.' He lifted his chin toward the board with the respect of someone acknowledging a well-played opposing move. 'They are organised. I'll give them that.'

He went inside. The pole had stopped turning. The shop lamp came on behind the half-door.

---

The tram came down from the upper routes at twenty past four, the overhead lines hissing in the damp air. Ruth and Miriam climbed aboard without discussion and found seats. Miriam pushed her hair off her forehead. Ruth looked at the window.

The city moved backward past the glass in the reverse of the morning's direction: first the terraced rows, then the Observatory with its dome catching the last of the amber light, then the lower suburbs beginning, the stone walls and oaks coming in as the tram rose and the district's roofline lowered and then disappeared behind the curve of the hill. Ruth could see it for a moment at the De Waal intersection, the full spread of it below the cloudbase: the dense mass of the rooflines, the water tanks and washing lines on the upper floors, the mountain behind it pressing down with the same proximity the watercolour in the stairwell had shown. Then the tram moved on.

The seats in the third-class section were wooden and the cold had been in them all day. Miriam had a notebook open on her knee and was reading something she had folded inside it. Ruth watched the last buildings of the lower suburb give way to the oaks and the stone walls of the university precinct, the architecture changing as the tram climbed: the compressed, layered density of the district's terraced streets giving way to the open spacing of the upper city, buildings set back from the road with their gardens showing, their cut stone a different argument in the same stone.

The branch line from De Waal took them past the lower campus wall and the last of the light went off the stone at the exact moment they passed the gate. Grey after amber, the buildings going formal and slightly severe in the even winter dusk.

Miriam said something about a seminar Thursday, the Fitzgerald being due back in the library. Ruth said yes. She looked at the window.

The upper city came in: the university gates, the oaks along the lower avenue, the residence block dark against the sky, its upper windows carrying yellow light. The tram bell rang and they were on the pavement.

---

They walked up through the oaks from the stop in what remained of the light. Ruth's shoes were damp from the lanes. The residence was ahead, the granite walls going from brown to near-black as the last light left them.

She stopped at the bottom of the steps without deciding to.

Miriam had gone three steps up and paused, looking back.

'I'll come back,' Ruth said. It was not addressed to Miriam exactly. It came out of the same part of herself as the comment to the barber, before the considered version had revised it.

'Tuesday afternoons are good,' Miriam said. 'The aunt works on Tuesdays.' She went up.

Ruth did not follow for a moment. The cold on this side of the building was a different cold from the lanes, moving air rather than held stone, and after a long day in enclosed spaces and narrow streets the openness of it felt slightly too simple, too unoccupied. She could hear the wind in the oaks above the path. Below the residence, below the avenue and the oaks and the university gates, the lower city was under its cloud and invisible.

She had not stopped at the tailor's shop on the return to the tram. They had passed the doorway and she had not slowed. The wind had been at her back and Miriam was ahead and she had kept walking. The tailor's door had been half closed by then, the cigarette gone from the ashtray, the man still at his table with the dark cloth and his head down.

The drawing was still on the wall above his workbench. The house in blue crayon. The sun with the careful, even lines going out from it in every direction, the sun itself placed very close to the roof of the house in the composition, lower than a sun should sit in a picture of a house, the radiating lines nearly reaching the eave. Not crushing it. Closer in the drawing than a sun would be allowed to be and the house still standing under it, the lines going straight out to the edges of the paper.

A child had made it. Six, seven years old at the most, going by the pressure of the pencil lines on the blue crayon, the way the roof ridge was not quite level, the chimney added with a second decision and a slightly different blue. Done with the concentrated care of someone for whom the house mattered. Not a sketch. A record.

She had not stopped. Some things about the drawing had become clear on the walk to the tram and she had not said them to Miriam and she did not say them now.

She climbed the steps and went inside.

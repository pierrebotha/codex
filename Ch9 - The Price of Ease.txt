# Chapter 9: The Price of Ease

The fourth time Ruth sat at the Cohen table, she reached for the water carafe without thinking.

That was the specific thing: the absence of the thinking. The carafe stood where it had stood on both previous evenings, between the nearer candlestick and the bread plate, the glass heavy and slightly fogged at the base where the kitchen produced its particular condensation. Her hand found it. Her hand poured, set the carafe down, and the thing was done before she had registered doing it, and the evening continued.

Mr Cohen was asking David something about the meeting at the university hall. It moved mid-sentence into something about an architect's registration, or perhaps they were the same person. Ruth had not caught the beginning. David's hands were already off his fork, resting knuckled on the tablecloth. He ate quickly and then became still, attentive, the instrument-ready quality he had, as though the real activity were still ahead. 'The ones who speak loudest about conditions,' he said, 'have never sat in the same room as anyone whose conditions they are speaking about. They argue at a high theoretical volume and the working man is in their kitchen.'

'Not in front of company,' said Mrs Cohen. Her voice was its own instrument, dry and precise, with the economy of a woman who did not repeat herself.

'Ruth isn't company,' said Judith on Ruth's left, without looking up from the bread she was tearing. She said it the way she said most things: as a factual statement of the current situation, delivered without inflection.

Mrs Cohen made a short sound, not agreement and not refusal entirely. She lifted her wine.

'She isn't,' Mr Cohen said. Large hands around his glass, calm and measured, the sort of man who settled small questions by stating what was obviously the case and then attending to his meal. He did not look at Ruth as he said it, which was its own form of inclusion. 'Which is why she's permitted to hear it.'

David looked at Ruth once, the brief evaluating glance he had, and smiled. 'What Ruth actually thinks is the most interesting opinion at this table,' he said, 'but she won't say it in front of my mother.'

Ruth held her glass and looked at the candle flame, which was still in the closed apartment air. She said nothing, not because the remark required defence but because her silence had for some weeks ceased to register with the table as reticence. They had filed it as Ruth's tempo, the particular pace at which she moved between hearing and answering, and they had adjusted to it, and the adjustment was comfortable in both directions.

She had blushed here once, badly. The first visit: the neck and the tops of the ears, the dining-room light designed apparently to find those surfaces. She had been conscious of it and conscious simultaneously that awareness of the blush made the blush worse, which was a problem without a solution. It had not recurred at the second visit, not badly. Tonight it was simply not there.

She picked up a piece of bread because she wanted it. The basket was where it had been.

The apartment itself was familiar now in the way places become familiar before you can explain how: she knew the smell of the lobby, brass polish and floor wax and the particular damp warmth of the lift shaft, knew which stair tread gave slightly under weight, knew the angle at which the corridor from the entrance to the dining room received the last of the evening light off the sea. She knew Mr Cohen's chair, the one with the worn arm where his right hand rested. She knew the kitchen sounds from behind the half-open door, when they meant a stage of preparation and when they meant recovery. She knew all of this without having counted it, and the knowing-without-counting was itself the thing she had noted tonight when her hand found the carafe.

Sarah came through from the kitchen with both hands around the handles of the brisket platter, set it at the centre of the table with a practised and careful quiet. The black cotton dress under the stiff white apron. The face lined deep around the mouth. Eyes that met no one at the table but whose positioning was absolutely exact: the bread plate, the spare spoon, the space between the candle and the serving ladle, all of it located without a look. She set the platter down and stepped back, and the room reorganised itself around the brisket, as rooms do around the arrival of the thing they have been moving towards all evening.

Mrs Cohen served. Judith first, then Ruth, then David, then Mr Cohen, then herself. An order said something: preference, seniority, some hierarchy of claim worked out over years. Ruth had been trying to read it and had not yet managed a complete reading.

'You corrected Professor Hendricks on Thursday,' said Judith to Ruth, turning the gravy spoon. 'In the tutorial. You told him it was the genitive.'

'It was.'

'He knew it was.'

'Then he didn't mind.'

Judith laughed, a low clean sound, no performance in it. 'She's impossible,' she said to the room.

'Accurate,' said Mr Cohen, without raising his eyes from his plate. One word of precise endorsement and then the topic entirely released, as though he had signed a small document and placed it on the completed pile. Ruth had learned not to expect expansion and had come to find the brevity useful. In an intelligent person, brevity was a reliable signal of what required no further argument.

David was looking at the middle distance in the way he sometimes did, a private attending. He had the quality, when not speaking, of hearing something the room had not yet produced. 'Book four,' he said to Ruth. 'The Odyssey.'

'Yes.'

He nodded without continuing, but the interest was real, and he reached for the bread in the way of a person still thinking about something else.

The brisket was the same: onion and fat and the long sweetness of the slow braise. The candles were past their halfway point. Outside, Friday on the seafront road, the tram grinding its electric note around the lower curve towards Sea Point proper. In the kitchen, the brief sound of it, the wire brush on the iron pan, Sarah beginning the pots while the main course was still on the table, the kitchen on its own clock and its own accounting, moving parallel to the dinner and not waiting for it.

Ruth ate and said little, and the conversation went as it went: Mr Cohen with the newspaper folded on the vacant side of the table, David with the students at the university hall, Mrs Cohen with three or four precise social observations that functioned less as information than as calibration, a weekly statement of what the world was still correctly positioned to be. And in all of it, she was watching herself from a slight distance. The balance of her own body. Not checking the exits. Not measuring the candlelight for exposure. Not converting the silver into debt arithmetic in the way she had still been doing in February, when every displayed object in the room had required translation into what it cost her.

She reached for the carafe a second time because Judith's glass was low and the carafe was already in her hand, and poured without comment. The act was simpler than setting it down first.

After dessert, after the brandy David did not take and Mr Cohen did, after David had risen without announcement and gone to the piano in the sitting room as he always did, somewhere between nine and nine-thirty, which was never announced and never commented on by anyone in the family, after Mrs Cohen had excused herself in the direction of the kitchen, the two of them were left at the cleared but not yet wiped table. Two plates still with their forks placed diagonally. One candlestick had burned through and the wick sat in a small pool of hardened wax that had run and cooled against the base.

'You're quieter than usual,' said Judith.

'I'm thinking about the third declension.'

Judith received this in silence, which was correct. She had learned by now that Ruth's thinking was a separate country that continued operating at a low and continuous pitch during social hours, and that this was not distance but constitution.

From the sitting room the piano. David's habit was fragments, not full pieces. He played the way some people talk to themselves: in running thoughts, half-finished, turning back. Three descending chords. A pause. A long run in the upper register, falling, something he was finding rather than stating. Something in the bass, exploratory and then abandoned.

'Is he well?' Ruth said.

Judith looked at the spent candlewick. 'He's David,' she said. Not an answer, but said in the tone of a woman who had received the same non-answer for twenty years and had long since incorporated it.

The piano went still.

Sarah came back through to clear the remaining plates. She gathered them with the efficient and essentially invisible care of a person who had made the work disappear as a professional matter. She did not look at Ruth tonight. She looked at the tablecloth as she sometimes did: past its surface, to some middle distance the dining room did not contain. Her left hand was lower and slightly turned as she stacked, distributing the weight of the gathered plates, and Ruth watched the hand without deciding to watch it, noticing the gradual turn and the stack and the practised management of load, and the kitchen sounds beginning again through the half-open door almost before Sarah had cleared the threshold.

Ruth took the tram back alone, as she had at each visit. The offer of a cab had come on the first visit, been declined, and had not been repeated, which was one of the things about Judith: she offered once and then released the outcome, with no further investment in which way it went.

The tram was quiet. A man at the front in a damp mackintosh, newspaper folded to the middle pages. Two women with bags in their laps and a sleeping child between them, the child's head against the window glass. A young man, probably a student, with a textbook open at a page he had not been reading for some time. The lights along the seafront road came and went in the window glass, amber and white, and the occasional red of a motor car heading north.

Ruth sat with her hands in her lap and the coat buttoned to the collar. The mountain somewhere behind the window to her left, invisible in the dark but making itself felt in the temperature, the cold of the rock coming off the wind even through the tram glass. She was not calculating anything in particular. She was watching the sleeping child's profile, the newspaper-reader's thumb at the fold, the student's untouched page. She was there, or as close to simply there as she generally managed, which was closer than in February by a specific measure she could not name but could register.

She counted the stops. Not because she risked missing hers but because it occupied the hands' attention while the other kind of attending continued.

There was an apple from the residence kitchen on her desk, and a note she had started in the margin of the day's passage: a genitive plural she had marked and wanted to confirm. She ate half the apple and made the note and reached for the apple again.

The smell arrived without being summoned. Not the dinner smell, not the brisket in its onion gravy or the candlewax or the warm gathered bread of the dining room. The other one. The pan: iron and the wire brush's darker bristle, the fat at the edge, the steam that came off the first water when it hit a still-hot surface. She had not been in the Cohen kitchen that evening. She had heard the brush sound through the half-open door, had not seen the pan, but the mind had been given enough of the components.

She sat with the half-eaten apple and the two smells, and they did not resolve into a conclusion. They existed alongside each other, the dining-room smell and the kitchen smell, two things true at once in the same body, and she sat with it.

She set the apple down.

She did not pick it up.

She looked at her father's letter. It had been on the corner of the desk since Wednesday. She had read it when it arrived: once quickly, once slowly. He wrote in his brief particular way. One paragraph for the farm news, which was the news of the accounts and the rainfall and the fence line on the lower paddock that would need re-straining before the dry season. One paragraph for Anna, which this time contained a sentence she had read twice: *the doctor is satisfied with the chest, which has not worsened since autumn, and she asks for you regularly.* One paragraph for the closing, which he always handled as a technical matter requiring resolution: keep well, write when you are able. Signed W.J. MacLeod in the full formal version of his name he used for anything written on paper.

She had drafted one paragraph of a reply and stopped. The paragraph said: she was well, the Greek was going better than last term, she had been to the Cohen household again and they were kind. Every word of it was true. The paragraph was not the letter. She had known this and had left it on the desk beside his letter for four days.

William had written in January. She had replied: two lines about the scholarship and the weather, both true. She had sealed the envelope before she read them back.

She picked up the pen. She wrote *Dear Dad* on a fresh page, and the date in the upper right corner, and then she put the pen down.

Outside, the mountain wind. Rain somewhere towards Thursday at the edge of it, the smell of eucalyptus and cold rock, not the subtropical mist-belt rain she had grown up inside, which was softer and warmer at the edges, greenish when it hit the red soil. This rain was the Atlantic kind. It found the gaps in stone.

She opened the Greek grammar to Tuesday's passage, read the first three lines, and went to bed.


She found Miriam in the small kitchen at the end of the corridor the following Saturday evening. Miriam had her head inside the bread tin, conducting the methodical end-of-week excavation that turned up, generally, three heels of different loaves, a packet of unclaimed Marie biscuits, and the occasional rind of hard yellow cheese that appeared with regularity and was never owned. Ruth wanted tea. She filled the kettle and set it on the ring.

'How was Sea Point?' said Miriam, still with her head in the tin.

'Fine.'

'Third time?'

'Fourth.'

Miriam surfaced with a packet of Marie biscuits and the expression of someone who had accounted for the situation and made the necessary adjustment. She sat on the counter, which was not permitted, and opened the packet. She held it out. Ruth shook her head.

Miriam ate the first biscuit without ceremony, looking at the wall above the kettle. She had that quality: thinking was visible in her face as a specific stillness, deep and local, before it produced a conclusion or produced nothing. A second biscuit with the same unhurried attention.

'They feed you properly over there,' she said. Not a question.

'Yes,' said Ruth.

'Good brisket?'

'Yes.'

'Good,' said Miriam, and looked at the wall, and it appeared to settle the matter of the Cohen household to her current satisfaction.

The kettle was building its sound. The steam began between them. Ruth held the mug and waited.

Miriam looked at her. Not a quick look, a proper one, the direct and unblinking attention she applied to things she had decided required it. Not intrusive and not unkind. The look of a person whose conclusions were worth the cost of their directness.

'You went in those shoes again,' Miriam said, looking at Ruth's feet.

'They're my only good shoes.'

'I know,' said Miriam. She ate a third biscuit and looked at the wall.

The kettle reached its temperature and clicked off. Ruth poured. Steam went up between them. Ruth wrapped both hands around the mug.

'You're getting fluent,' Miriam said.

Ruth looked at her. 'In what?'

The sideways glance, brief, landing on nothing new. 'In everything,' Miriam said. Then she folded the biscuit packet closed and laid it on the counter beside her, and the sentence was done, and she was not going to expand it.

Ruth took her tea.

In the corridor, walking back with the mug warm between both palms, she turned the word over. Fluent. Not *good* or *settled* or *easier*, but *fluent*: a word that contained a language, and a language implied a prior one, the language you spoke at home, the one you had before you acquired the other. You became fluent when you were operating in something that was not your native register. Fluent in everything was an observation about more than one thing at once: the Sea Point dining room, the Hanover Street afternoon last week with the sign-painter and the accordion women and Miriam pointing out the ghost letters beneath the orange paint, and possibly also the residence dining hall with Professor Hendricks and the genitive that was the genitive. Miriam had been near enough to all of those occasions to have the data, and she had noted the data and produced the word. The word was not accusation. It was measurement. The measurement was probably accurate.

The bracket in the corridor was humming. It had been humming for three weeks and the duty warden had twice promised to see to it.

Ruth went into her room and sat on the edge of the bed and did not drink the tea immediately. She sat, and the bracket hummed in the corridor, and she sat with it.

After a while she put the tea on the desk and worked on the Greek until the residence was quiet.


On Tuesdays their lecture schedules aligned at half-past three and they fell into the walk without planning it, the way regular appearances become habits before they become decisions. Down the oak line, the lower campus, the old service gate at the foot. The oaks were coming off their leaves early. The warden said it was the mountain wind stripping them more aggressively than a normal summer-end, but Ruth had no previous summer here to use as a comparison.

Judith had come from the economics subsidiary and was carrying something in her quiet that was different from the usual gathered ease. Her usual was settled and untroubled. This one had an edge to it, along the jaw.

They walked. The leaves off the oaks were dry at the edges, already starting to curl and tighten.

'He played all night again,' Judith said. 'Friday. After you left. Didn't stop until the light changed. My mother gets up around three and closes his door very quietly, as though that addresses it. But you can hear it through all the walls.'

Ruth said nothing. The leaves moved ahead of their own walking in the intermittent wind, always one step forward.

'When he can't leave,' Judith said, 'he plays. That's how it works for him. He plays in the dark until it's light and then he sleeps through the middle of the day and then he has breakfast and is perfectly available and polite, and we don't discuss any of it because my mother has decided it doesn't require discussion. It requires management.'

'Where does he want to go?' Ruth said.

Judith gave the question its due time. She always did this with direct questions, gave them their proper weight before answering. 'Palestine at the moment,' she said. 'Last spring it was Paris. My mother won't hear Palestine. She heard it once and closed the idea completely, like a cupboard door. My father hears it and decides not to have heard it, which is a different method with the same result. So the wanting has nowhere to go except the piano.' She kicked a small cluster of leaves off the path. 'He won't go. He'll stay and want to leave and be clever and play in the dark and they'll all love each other too much to cost each other anything, and that's the arrangement.'

She said this without cruelty and without self-pity. She said it the way she reported facts she had fully incorporated.

'Could he go?' Ruth said. 'Practically. The money.'

'Of course. It isn't the money.' Judith looked at the oak canopy above them, the mountain visible through a break in the leaves, upper rock grey with cloud. 'Palestine is what he says when he wants to ask permission. Paris is what he means when he doesn't expect it. Either way he stays. The arrangement holds because it holds itself. Nobody breaks an arrangement that reflects back only love.'

They walked in quiet.

Ruth said: 'My father came to the station. When I left from Pietersburg. He drove in from the farm. He didn't have to come. It wasn't practical, it wasn't on his way anywhere. He stood on the platform and he said the practical things and then the train went.'

Judith looked at her.

'My mother didn't come,' Ruth said. 'She stayed at the farm.'

Judith said nothing. She did not say she was sorry and she did not ask about the mother and she did not fill the space with anything, and all three of those absences were correct.

After a moment Judith said, quietly: 'I think that's probably where it comes from. In you. The exactness. People who've been left at platforms get very precise about what they can and can't reasonably carry.'

Ruth did not answer. She turned the idea at a slight angle, the way she turned a grammar construction she was not yet sure of, suspicious of anything too complete. It was either entirely right or entirely wrong. It was probably right and wrong in the same breath, in the way that things said about you which you cannot immediately dismiss are usually at least partly right.

They reached the lower gate. The wind stronger here, direct from the mountain, colder, salt-mixed. Ruth buttoned the top button of her coat. Her hand went into the right pocket for the warmth of it and found the silk square, folded where she had last set it, weeks ago now.

She registered the awareness. Nothing more than that. Still there.

Judith glanced at the pocket. Not a long look, brief, precise, the duration of a look that says *I see that* and then moves on. She looked back at the path.

She did not ask.

Ruth did not offer.

The gate opened. They walked out onto the lower road. The oak trees behind them, the mountain above and the city below, and the leaves from the tree at the gate corner came off in the gust and moved across the road in no organised direction, east and then west as the next wind came off the rock.

They walked towards the tram stop. Judith talked about the economics paper she had not finished, and Ruth listened, and the afternoon light came in low and lateral off the bay as it did at the close of summer, flattening the white buildings slightly, deepening the shadows under the mountain.


Past ten, the corridor was quiet. The humming bracket had been repaired sometime in the last two days. Its absence was a brief disorientation, the way any removed sound draws the ear until the ear adjusts and the silence becomes ordinary.

Ruth sat at her desk with the Greek grammar open to book four. The single lamp. The mountain wind at the window frame. Somewhere below on the road a tram going south, its electric note brief and then absorbed.

The passage was book four: Telemachus in Sparta, in the palace of Menelaus, the gold and amber of the great hall, the youth's awe at wealth he had never seen, the whole household organised around a king who fought a war for ten years and came home. Ruth had read it twice in preparation. She read the first hexameter aloud in an undertone in the practised manner, measuring the quantities, the line building in its movement to the sixth foot.

She read it.

She closed the grammar.

The wind measured itself against the window glass. A second tram on the lower road, going south. Then nothing.

She reached behind her and lifted the coat from the chair-back and reached into the right pocket and removed the silk square. She unfolded it once along its crease. The emerald sat in the lamplight with the quality silk has in low light, a density that did not require brightness, something in the weave that held the colour even in a dim room. She brought it close and the smell came: lavender, cedar, the expensive-shop smell that had no single identifiable constituent but was exactly recognisable as the combined evidence of a whole way of living, the kind in which lavender was a real plant grown in a real garden, and cedar was a real wood in a real clothes press, and the shop had been in the same position on the same street for thirty years and would be there thirty years hence without any particular uncertainty about whether it would be asked to move.

She held it for a moment.

She folded the square once along its crease and put it back in the coat pocket.

She turned to the grammar and opened it to the beginning of the passage and read the first hexameter again, quietly, in the room.

She did not turn the page.

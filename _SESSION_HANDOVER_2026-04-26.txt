================================================================================
SESSION OUTCOME
================================================================================

Twenty-four canonical chapters of *Between Two Worlds* are humanised, mechanically
clean, and now coherent against a substantially clarified canon bible. The novel
runs from Ch1 (Ruth's January 1949 train south to Cape Town) through Ch24 (her
mid-January 1951 train north to Krugersdorp). Total novel word-count is
approximately 102,000-105,000 words after this session's editorial work.

The novel's structural argument — Ruth carries the Afrikaner-Scots-English
divide as cultural backbone; Lena's saving and withholding share one body; Anna
is the only character who wants what she can have and gets it; the Cohen
complicity is visible to the reader through prose architecture before it is
visible to Ruth — is now wired into the prose at every chapter where it
operates.

CANON DECISIONS MADE THIS SESSION
================================================================================

1.  Anna MacLeod is eight to nine years younger than Ruth. Ten in 1949,
    eleven from October 1949, twelve in October 1950. Grand mal (tonic-clonic)
    seizures since age four. Chronic chest condition that compounds the
    seizures: the chest weakens the post-ictal recovery of the breath. June is
    the worst month (the chest does not like the cold). Doctor Caldwell at
    Pietersburg has known her since age four. Anna survives this novel intact.
    Her canonical death occurs approximately three books hence: a stroke
    following a seizure (status-epilepticus complication producing an ischaemic
    event). Ruth is at Anna's deathbed when it comes.

2.  William MacLeod (Willi at home, William outside the house) is six to seven
    years younger than Ruth. Twelve in 1949. Attends the Merensky Farm School
    where Pa teaches. Won the second-place Latin prize at Merensky in 1949.
    First woodwork project: a wooden stool he made for Anna's stoep at the
    farm, the cat sometimes claims it. Bilingual at home. He is a child and
    the novel does not ask him to do adult work.

3.  W. J. MacLeod (Pa) is English-Scots. Long-serving teacher at Merensky Farm
    School (the lowveld farm school in the Letaba/Tzaneen area established
    under the Merensky Trust). Runs the farm at Letaba alongside the teaching;
    school holidays are farm-work full-time. Reflex language: ENGLISH ONLY.
    Greets in *Good morning*, never *Goeiemôre*. Understands Afrikaans, does
    not speak it. Brief plain English register at the kitchen door, the supper
    table, the platform. Drove Ruth down to St Joseph's Pietersburg in '44 in
    the dry winter and sat at the parlour for three hours with the
    matriculation certificate in his coat pocket until they took her name for
    the matric class. Lena did not know he had gone until the place was
    confirmed. He has not, in any of the years since, asked Ruth about the
    scholarship. The not-saying is the saying.

4.  Lena MacLeod is Afrikaans. Born of the lower Soutpansberg farms. Her
    grandmother stitched the dark wool shawl that comes down through the
    family. Reflex language: AFRIKAANS ONLY. Speaks only the canon-permitted
    English at structural moments: the Ch21 *Dit is my huis. Nie joune nie* /
    *This is my house. Not yours.* second-half English; the *Yes, Doctor* to
    Hayes-Whittaker in Ch22.

5.  Household bilingual pattern: each parent speaks their own language by
    reflex. Ruth, William and Anna grew up bilingual, addressing Pa in English
    and Lena in Afrikaans. Sentences in the kitchen sometimes change language
    mid-clause depending on which parent is being addressed.

6.  Catherine MacLeod is Pa's first cousin, English colonial Kenilworth. Has
    not been to the farm since before Willi was born. Pa has not been to
    Kenilworth since before the war. They send the Christmas card. Catherine
    introduced to Ruth via the Ch17 cream-card-in-the-pigeonhole plant; lent
    Ruth the small flat above her garage in December 1949; refused entry to
    the doctor's house in the Ch22 crisis by Lena's no.

7.  Boet van Eden is the Afrikaans family-friend driver. Twenty-eight in 1949.
    Has had a quiet eye on Ruth for the four years since she was fourteen.
    Eye not encouraged or refused; he has decided long ago that it would not
    be enacted unless Ruth one day asked it to be. Drives the lorry on the
    practical errands the household requires (Tzaneen syndicate, Pietersburg
    alternator, Ruth to and from the Politsi turn-off, Pa and Lena south to
    Cape Town for Ruth's Ch22 near-death). Will play a substantial role in
    the next book. Seeded in Ch20 (kitchen door entry), Ch21 (drives Ruth
    out), Ch22 (south for the Cape Town crisis, at the boarding house, comes
    to the doctor's front room every morning at nine), Ch23 (noon arrival at
    the shed, eats with Ruth and Anna at the workbench, drives Ruth to
    Pietersburg), Ch24 (stands at the far end of Cape Town's Platform Three
    at the Krugersdorp departure, having come south on the goods train two
    days before).

8.  Geographic canon: farm is **Letaba Estates** near Letsitele in the
    Northern Transvaal (real place); residence is **General Botha Hall** at
    UCT (the Boer general turned Empire-statesman, the institutional mirror
    of the family's English-Scots-Afrikaner fracture); Brownlow Road in
    Tamboerskloof for the Clara salon (Ch11); Catherine's Kenilworth address
    is *Edenholm, Lower Bishop's Avenue, Kenilworth*.

9.  Judith is Cohen, not Marais. The Brownlow Road rooms are her literary
    independent rooms above the salon, distinct from the Cohen Sea Point
    apartment. Hampshire return address in her Ch17/Ch18 letter is her
    mother's English family connection.

10. Ch22 is the structural summit at valence 10. Ruth's near-death from the
    Wynberg level crossing: right kidney removed by Doctor Hayes-Whittaker;
    left kidney passive at first; saved by water-feeding regimen (half a cup
    every twenty minutes for ninety-six hours) administered first by the
    doctor's wife and Judith Cohen, then from Wednesday evening by Lena. Pa
    came south with Lena. Boet drove them to Pietersburg, came on later. The
    canonical exchange: Ruth's three statements of recognition, *Then look at
    me*, Lena's eyes moving up the white sheet to Ruth's hands and away to
    the lace half-curtain, *Something inside Ruth ceased its labour*, the
    chapter close *No one answered*. Anna does NOT die in this novel.
